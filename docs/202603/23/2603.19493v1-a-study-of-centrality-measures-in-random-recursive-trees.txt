Abstract 

We investigate the behaviour of five classical centrality measures—Jordan, rumor, betweenness, degree, and closeness centralities—in the setting of uni-form random recursive trees. Motivated by applications in network archaeology, we focus on two fundamental questions: (i) the birth index (time of arrival) of the most central vertex, and (ii) the relative centrality of the root. We quantify the probability that the root is the most central vertex, analyze its expected rank under each centrality measure, and determine the expected birth index of a central vertex. In addition, we characterize the typical size of the set of top-ranked vertices that contains the root with high probability. Finally, for each centrality notion, we study the persistence properties of the center and the asymptotic behaviour of the root’s rank.

## 1 Introduction 

Centrality is a fundamental concept in network science, capturing the structural importance of vertices in complex networks. Over the years, a wide range of central-ity measures has been proposed to quantify di ff erent aspects of vertex importance. For a comprehensive survey of centrality measures, see Saxena and Iyengar [40]. 2This paper aims to provide a rigorous analysis of five prominent centrality measures in one of the most basic and canonical models of network growth: the uniform random recursive tree . The measures we study are Jordan centrality, betweenness centrality, degree centrality, rumor centrality, and closeness centrality. Precise definitions of these notions are given below. Each of these measures induces a ranking of the vertices from most to least central; we refer to a vertex of maximum centrality as a center of the network. Our investigation is primarily motivated by questions arising in network archaeology 

[37], a branch of combinatorial statistics concerned with inferring past properties of growing networks from present-day observations. A central problem in this area is root estimation : identifying the initial vertex, or root, of a randomly grown network. This problem has been studied extensively for tree-valued models, includ-ing uniform attachment trees, preferential attachment trees, and related growth processes. In this paper, we focus on the uniform random recursive tree (urrt ), also known as the uniform attachment tree . This model generates a sequence of random labelled increasing trees Tn, where for each n ≥ 1, the vertex set of Tn is V (Tn) = [ n]. The construction proceeds recursively: starting from a single vertex 1, designated as the root, the tree Tn+1 is obtained from Tn by adding a new vertex n + 1 and connecting it by an edge i, n + 1, where i is chosen uniformly at random from [ n]. To define the centrality measures considered in the paper, we introduce some notation. Given a tree Tn and a vertex v ∈ V (Tn), we write ( Tn, v ) for the tree Tn

rooted at vertex v. If u ∈ V (Tn) is another vertex, then we write ( Tn, v )u↓ for the subtree of Tn, rooted at vertex u, containing all vertices w such that the path from

1.1 Rank of the root and index of the center 

For any given centrality measure, one may order the vertices from most to least central. In this paper, we focus on the behavior of two random variables: the rank of the root and the index of the center . The rank of the root is the position of the root vertex in the ranking. This is an important quantity for root finding methods: if the rank of the root is guaranteed to be small with high probability, then the set of a few of the most central vertices contains the root with high probability, giving a small 

confidence set for root finding. An equally interesting—albeit less studied—notion for network archaeology is the index (i.e., label) of the most central vertex. If the index of the center is small with high probability, then the centrality measure is eff ective in finding an early-arriving vertex. To define these random variables rigorously, consider a centrality measure ψTn :

V (Tn) → N. This defines a ranking of the vertices γn : V (Tn) → { 1, . . . , n } such that 

γn(u) = 1 + |{ v ∈ Tn : v is more central than u}| . We recall that vertices with the same centrality value have the same rank. When the centrality measure ψTn assigns equal values to di ff erent vertices, such vertices have the same rank according to the definition above. To avoid technicalities arising from such ties, we break ties so that each vertex is guaranteed to have a unique rank. Even though the tie-breaking method has no consequence to our results, for concreteness, we consider the pessimistic tie-breaking rule. This rule orders vertices with equal centrality in reverse order of their insertion time. Hence, if ψTn (u) = ψTn (v) for some u, v ∈ [n], then γn(u) < γ n(v) if and only if u > v . Note that this rule ensures that (γn(1) , . . . γ n(n)) is a permutation of [ n]. In the rest of the paper, we implicitly assume this tie-breaking rule. 

Definition 1. Given a centrality measure ψTn , the center (or ψTn -center) is the vertex 

v ∈ [n] for which γn(v) = 1 .The index of the center (i.e., the value of the integer v) is denoted by In. Note that, thanks to tie-breaking, the center is always unique and well-defined. The rank of the root is the random variable Rn = γn(1) .

A standard notion is the centroid of a tree Tn, defined as a vertex whose removal decomposes the tree into a forest whose largest component has size at most n/ 2.It is well known that every tree has at least one and at most two centroids. It is easy to see that a centroid is most central according to Jordan, closeness, and rumor centralities (see Section 2 for details). In other words, the Jordan center, closeness center, and rumor center coincide. In particular, the random variable In is the same for these three centrality measures. At the same time, Rn behaves quite di ff erently in these three cases. 

In this paper, we study various properties of the random variables In and Rn. In particular, we investigate the following quantities and properties: • The probability P(Rn = 1) = P(In = 1) that the root is the center. It was known since Moon [36] that the centroid (i.e., the Jordan, closeness, and rumor centers) is the root vertex with probability bounded away from zero. We show that this also holds for the betweenness center. • The expected values ERn and EIn. We prove that in the case of rumor centrality, both quantities are of constant order. Interestingly, in all other cases, EIn is of a smaller order of magnitude than ERn, indicating that, even though some centrality measures fail to assign a small rank to the root vertex, the most central vertex has a small expected index. • We say that a centrality measure is good for root estimation if the sequence of random variables {Rn} is tight, that is, for every ϵ > 0 there exists a positive integer K such that for all n, P{Rn ≤ K} ≥ 1 − ϵ. If a centrality measure is good for root estimation, then the set of vertices {v ∈ V (Tn) : γn(v) ≤ K}

is a confidence set that contains the root with probability at least 1 − ϵ and has bounded size, independently of the size n of the tree. We show that Jordan, rumor, and betweenness centralities are good for root estimation, while closeness and degree centralities are not. When a centrality measure is good for root estimation, understanding the magnitude of K that guarantees 

P{Rn ≤ K} ≥ 1 − ϵ off ers a way of comparing such centrality measures. For each of these centrality measures, we establish matching upper and lower bounds for the order of magnitude of K, as a function of ϵ. Analogously, we may study the tightness of the sequence {In}. It follows from the boundedness 6of EIn that the index of the center is tight for all centrality measures, with the only exception of degree centrality. • We also study persistence of the center and persistence of the rank of the root .Persistence means that Rn and In stabilize eventually, almost surely. Formally, for a given centrality measure, we say that the center is persistent if there exist integer-valued random variables N , K with P{N < ∞} = P{K < ∞} = 1, such that In = K for all n ≥ N . Similarly, the rank of the root is persistent if there exist integer-valued random variables N , K with P{N < ∞} = P{K < ∞} = 1, such that Rn = K for all n ≥ N . For each of the five centrality measures, we determine whether In and Rn are persistent or not. The results of the paper are summarized in the tables of Section 1.4. Naturally, not all of these results are new. Previous work is surveyed in Section 1.2. 

1.2 Related work 

The study of root estimation in uniform random recursive trees ( urrt ) was pio-neered by Haigh [26], who demonstrated that the maximum likelihood estimator (mle ) of the root is the tree’s centroid. Haigh further showed that the probability of the mle correctly identifying the root converges to 1 − ln 2 as the tree size n → ∞ .A parallel result was established by Shah and Zaman [41] for rumor di ff usion processes on infinite regular trees. Building on these foundations, Bubeck et al. [12] introduced the concept of con-fidence sets —sets of vertices guaranteed to contain the root with high probability. They proved that for any ϵ > 0, there exists a confidence set whose size depends solely on ϵ (independent of n) that contains the root with probability at least 1 − ϵ.Crucially, they showed that such sets can be constructed using the most central ver-tices according to Jordan or rumor centralities—two of the five measures analyzed in the present work. Recent advancements have further refined these bounds. Addario-Berry et al. [2, 1] utilized a “leaf-stripping” method to provide bounded-size confidence sets and obtained upper bounds of the optimal order for both uniform attachment trees and di ff usions in regular trees. For preferential attachment models, optimal upper bounds were established by Contat et al. [13]. 7Properties of the centroid and betweenness. The statistical properties of the centroid were first investigated by Moon [36], who derived the limiting probability lim n→∞ P(In = 1) = 1 − ln 2 and provided exact formulas for the expected index EIn

and the expected distance between the centroid and the root. These results were later extended by Durant and Wagner [19], who obtained distributional results for the index and depth of the centroid in “very simple increasing trees.” In a separate comprehensive study, Durant and Wagner [18] explored betweenness centrality in urrt s and simply generated trees, characterizing its k-th moments, limiting distributions, and the convergence behavior of the betweenness center. 

Center coincidence and persistence. The relationship between di ff erent centrality measures is also well-documented; for instance, Zelinka [43] proved that Jordan and closeness centers coincide in any tree. A significant body of work has focused on the 

persistence of these centers—the property that the ranking of the most central nodes stabilizes as the tree grows. Jog and Loh [31, 30] and Banerjee and Bhamidi [4] established strong persistence for Jordan centers across various models, including uniform, sublinear, and general attachment trees. Similarly, the persistence of the degree center has been studied for preferential attachment models [16, 25, 3, 5, 13] and Crump-Mode-Jagers branching processes [28]. 

Alternative models and variations. Beyond simple trees, root estimation has been explored from various vantage points: • Complex Networks: Galton-Watson trees [8], random recursive dag s and Cooper-Frieze networks [9], and random nearest neighbor trees [7]. • Computational Complexity: Work by Borgs et al. [6] and Frieze and Pegden [24] shifted the focus toward the algorithmic e ffi ciency of root identification. • Arrival Order: Rather than identifying only the root, Crane and Xu [14] and Briend et al. [10] developed methods to estimate the full arrival order of vertices in attachment models. • Seeded Models: Investigations into finding the initial “seed” tree in evolving 

urrt s can be found in [15, 12, 17, 33]. 81.3 Notation

3.2 Persistence 

To establish the persistence of the root’s Jordan rank Rn, we first observe that the evolution of the subtrees adjacent to the root is stochastically equivalent to Hoppe’s urn model [27]. This process begins with a single black ball; at each discrete time step, a ball is drawn uniformly at random. If the black ball is selected, it is returned to the urn alongside a ball of a new, unique color. If a colored (non-black) ball is drawn, it is returned with an additional ball of the same color. In the context of the tree, the black ball represents the root, and each color corre-sponds to a specific subtree rooted at a neighbor of the root. An attachment to the root (which creates a new subtree) is thus modeled by drawing the black ball. Our proof begins by showing that in Hoppe’s urn, a single color almost surely emerges as the unique, permanent leader in size.

## 6 Rumor centrality 

Shah and Zaman [41] introduced the concept of rumor centrality in trees. They showed that in a di ff usion process over infinite regular trees, the likelihood of a vertex being the root is determined by its rumour centrality. Crane and Xu [14] proved that the same holds in uniform attachment trees as well. As a consequence, for every ε > 0, the set of vertices of smallest size that contains the root with probability at least 1 − ε contains the K(ε) most central vertices according to rumor centrality, for some positive integer K(ϵ). Theorem 30. The rumor rank Rn of the root is persistent. 

Proof. By Lemmas 28 and 29, there exists an almost surely finite distance beyond which no vertex can ever surpass the root in centrality. Consequently, the total set of vertices that are ever more central than the root is almost surely finite. Since Lemma 5 establishes that any finite set of vertices possesses a persistent relative ranking, it follows that the rank of the root is persistent almost surely.
