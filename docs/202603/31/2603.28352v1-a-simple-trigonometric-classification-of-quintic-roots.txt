# A Simple Trigonometric Classification of Quintic Roots 

## Sawon Pratiher ∗

## March 19, 2022 

Abstract 

This article provides a simple trigonometric method for determining how many roots of a quintic equation are real and how many are complex, without solving the equation. The approach transforms a depressed quintic t5 + mt 3 + nt 2 + pt + q = 0 with m < 0 into the trigonometric equation f (θ) = α cos 2θ + β cos θ + cos 5θ + γ = 0 via the Chebyshev identity 16 cos 5θ − 20 cos 3θ + 5 cos θ = cos 5θ. The derivation is computationally light and conceptually natural, extending the quartic case to fifth-degree equations. As the Abel–Ruffini theorem forbids a general algebraic solution for the quintic, having a simple trigonometric criterion for the nature of its roots is especially appealing. 

# 1 Introduction 

The quintic equation occupies a unique place in the history of mathematics. It was the first degree for which the impossibility of a general algebraic solution was established—by Abel [ 1]and later clarified by Galois—marking a turning point that gave birth to modern algebra. Yet the nature of a quintic’s roots—how many are real—is a far simpler question than finding the roots themselves, and one might hope for a correspondingly simpler answer. For the quadratic, the discriminant b2 − 4ac settles the question instantly. Loh [ 4]recently showed that even the quadratic formula itself admits a simple and natural proof that might have been discovered millennia earlier. For the quartic, the same trigonometric idea shows that a single substitution converts the root-classification problem into the analysis of 

a cos θ + cos 4θ + b = 0, replacing the quartic’s unwieldy discriminant with an elementary exercise. This article extends the same idea to the quintic. The Chebyshev polynomial T5(x) = 16 x5 − 20 x3 + 5 x satisfies T5(cos θ) = cos 5θ, and this identity plays the same structural role for the quintic as the identity 8 cos 4θ − 8 cos 2θ + 1 = cos 4θ played for the quartic. Matching coefficients via the substitution t = u cos θ absorbs the leading and cubic terms of the depressed quintic, leaving a residual equation in cos 2θ, cos θ, cos 5θ, and a constant. Throughout this article, all polynomials have real coefficients, so complex roots come in conjugate pairs. A quintic with real coefficients and positive leading coefficient satisfies 

P (t) → +∞ as t → +∞ and P (t) → −∞ as t → −∞ , guaranteeing at least one real root. The quintic therefore has exactly one, three, or five real roots. 

# 2 Derivation 

## 2.1 The depressed quintic 

Every monic quintic z5 + a4z4 + a3z3 + a2z2 + a1z + a0 = 0 can be converted to depressed 

form—with no z4 term—by the Tschirnhaus substitution t = z − a4/5. This is a standard change of variables (see, e.g., [3]), and the result is an equation of the form 

P (t) = t5 + mt 3 + nt 2 + pt + q = 0 . (1) By Vieta’s relations [ 7], the five roots satisfy t1 + t2 + t3 + t4 + t5 = 0, so the centroid of the five roots sits at the origin. Knowing any four roots determines the fifth. 

## 2.2 The Chebyshev identity 

The identity at the heart of our method is 16 cos 5 θ − 20 cos 3 θ + 5 cos θ = cos 5 θ, (2) valid for all θ ∈ R. This is nothing other than the statement that the degree-five Chebyshev polynomial of the first kind, T5(x) = 16 x5 − 20 x3 + 5 x, satisfies T5(cos θ) = cos 5θ. The Chebyshev polynomials have a long and distinguished history, originating in Chebyshev’s work on approximation theory in the 1850s (see, e.g., [ 5]), and the multi-angle identities they encode have been known since Euler’s time. What is perhaps less appreciated is how directly they connect to the root structure of polynomial equations.

## 2.5 What about roots outside [−u, u ]?

The substitution t = u cos θ only captures roots in [ −u, u ]. For the quartic, the exterior analysis was straightforward: P is convex outside [ −u, u ], so at most one root on each side. For the quintic, the argument is only slightly more involved, and the asymmetric end-behavior (P (t) → +∞ as t → +∞, P (t) → −∞ as t → −∞ ) introduces a new twist. 

Lemma 4 (Third-derivative positivity) . If m < 0, then P ′′′ (t) > 0 for all |t| > u .Proof. We compute P ′′′ (t) = 60 t2 + 6 m. For |t| > u , we have t2 > u 2 = −4m/ 5, so 60 t2 > −48 m, and therefore P ′′′ (t) = 60 t2 + 6 m > −48 m + 6 m = −42 m > 0. The positivity of P ′′′ means that P ′′ is strictly increasing on each half-line, which in turn means P ′ has at most one inflection point there, and ultimately: 

Corollary 5. The polynomial P has at most three roots on each of the half-lines (u, ∞) and 

(−∞ , −u). Generically (and in all examples of this article), each half-line contributes at most one root. 

The crucial difference from the quartic is the asymmetric end-behavior. Since P (t) → +∞

• On ( u, ∞): there is at least one root if and only if P (u) < 0, i.e., f (0) < 0.

# 7 The case m ≥ 0

When m ≥ 0, the substitution t = u cos θ with u = 2 p−m/ 5 is not available. A hyperbolic substitution t = v cosh ϕ with v = 2 pm/ 5 could in principle match the cosh 3ϕ coefficient via the identity 16 cosh 5ϕ − 20 cosh 3ϕ + 5 cosh ϕ = cosh 5ϕ, but since cosh is unbounded and monotonically increasing on [0 , ∞), the correspondence between zeros and roots is less clean. When m = 0, the Chebyshev identity cannot absorb the t3 term (since there is none), and the method does not apply. We leave these extensions to future work.

## 9.1 The general pattern 

The method presented here for the quintic, together with the analogous quartic analysis, reveals a general pattern. For a degree-d polynomial, the Chebyshev identity Td(cos θ) = cos dθ can absorb the highest and third-highest terms of the depressed polynomial (after the substitution 

t = u cos θ with appropriately chosen u), leaving a residual trigonometric equation involving cos kθ for k = 0 , 1, . . . , d − 3. The complexity of this residual grows with the degree, but the core mechanism remains intact: the bijection between zeros on [0 , π ] and roots in a bounded interval, supplemented by boundary-sign analysis for exterior roots. 

## 9.2 Why is the quintic special? 

The Abel–Ruffini theorem tells us that no algebraic formula exists for the roots of a general quintic. The Bring–Jerrard normal form reduces the quintic to t5 + pt + q = 0 (see [ 3]), but even this simplified form has no closed-form solution in radicals. Our trigonometric method does not solve the quintic—it classifies the nature of its roots—but it does so using only the Chebyshev identity and elementary analysis, sidestepping the algebraic impossibility entirely. There is a pleasing irony here: the very identity ( cos 5θ in terms of powers of cos θ) that 

could solve the quintic if only cos 5θ were invertible by radicals (which it is not, in general) turns out to be perfectly suited for the simpler task of determining whether the roots are real or complex. 

## 9.3 Pedagogical value 

In the spirit of Loh [ 4], who showed that the quadratic formula admits a derivation simple enough for first-time Algebra learners, we hope that this trigonometric perspective may demystify the root structure of higher-degree polynomials. The key insight—that the bounded range of cos θ translates polynomial constraints into trigonometric ones—is both elementary and powerful. A student who has seen the Chebyshev identity and the intermediate value theorem has all the tools needed to classify the roots of any quartic or quintic with m < 0. May this encourage the reader to think afresh about old things: the Chebyshev polynomials, which originated in approximation theory, turn out to be an elegant lens for viewing the geometry of polynomial roots.
