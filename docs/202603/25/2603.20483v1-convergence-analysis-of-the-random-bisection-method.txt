Abstract 

We propose a generalized version of the bisection method where the cutting point between the two subinter-vals is chosen at random following an arbitrary distribution. We compute expected convergence rates with respect to any arbitrary a priori distribution for the position of the root in the initial interval and proved that it depends only on the the expectation E[c(1 − c)] of the cut c. We also provide a generalization of the method for K random cuts and study its convergence properties. Most probabilistic derivations are kept fairly simple for the ease of understanding of a larger audience. Our theoretical results are then validated numerically using statistical simulation. 

Keywords: Root-finding Algorithms, Iterative Methods, Geometric Probability, Random Dynamical Systems, Linear Operators, Statistical Simulation 

1. Introduction 

1.1. Background and motivation 

The classical bisection method is one of the simplest and oldest algorithms for locating a root of a continuous function. At each iteration, the method halves the interval by cutting it at its midpoint, producing a sequence of nested intervals converging linearly to the root. Because of its simplicity, the bisection method often serves as a baseline against which more sophisticated root-finding procedures are compared. In this work, we investigate what happens when this elementary deterministic procedure is randomly perturbed. More precisely, instead of cutting the interval at its midpoint, we choose the cutting point at random according to a prescribed distribution. This seemingly minor modification transforms the algorithm into a stochastic dynamical system whose probabilistic behavior turns out to have surprisingly regular structure. The starting point of this work was pedagogical rather than technical. While introducing the bisection method to students, we attempted to motivate the algorithm by asking how one should choose an approxi-mation of a root known only to lie in an interval [a, b ]. From this viewpoint, selecting the midpoint is not the only reasonable option: any point in the interval could serve as a candidate approximation, and choosing such a point at random appears just as natural from an intuitive perspective. This immediately raises a simple question: what happens if the deterministic midpoint rule is replaced by a random choice? At first glance, one might expect that if the cut is chosen from a distribution centered at the midpoint, then the method should retain, at least on average, some of the behavior of the classical bisection algorithm. numerical experimentation quickly showed that this intuition was misleading. Indeed, the randomized procedure exhibited a slower convergence rate together with several regular probabilistic patterns that suggested the existence of invariant distributions and explicit convergence laws. For instance, when the position of the root is viewed across repeated runs of the algorithm, its normalized location surprisingly appears to become uniformly distributed, independently of the distribution used to generate the cuts. Moreover, empirical evidence suggests that the average reduction factor of the interval length depends only on a simple moment of the cut c, namely E[c(1 − c)] . The present paper thus naturally grew out of the effort to understand and rigorously explain these observations.

Using elementary probabilistic and geometric arguments, we derive explicit formulas for the distribution of the interval reduction factor and for its first moments. We show that the uniform distribution is stationary for the normalized root process, regardless of the distribution of the cuts , and we establish convergence toward this invariant distribution for any arbitrary distribution of the initial root. As a consequence, we obtain explicit expressions for the expected convergence rate of the stochastic bisection method. These results lead to a probabilistic optimality principle: among all of these randomized variants, the classical deterministic bisection method is essentially the only one achieving the smallest expected contraction factor. We also study a natural generalization in which K > 1 random cuts are chosen at each iteration. Although increasing the number of cuts is expected to accelerate convergence, we show that the observed acceleration can behave in a counter-intuitive way for random multisection strategies. In particular, a random trisection procedure has, on average, the same contraction factor as the classical deterministic bisection method.

It is easily shown that the interval generated by this algorithm has length [an, b n] = 2 −n(b − a). If the cuts cn are considered as approximations of the root r, the error en = |cn − r| is then always upper bounded by the half-length of the interval, i.e., en ≤ 2−(n+1) (b − a). It thus follows that this root-finding algorithm converges linearly with convergence rate 12 , that is en+1 ≈ 12 en. The algorithm generally applies to functions with multiple roots in [a, b ], but here we assume that the root is unique. Because of its simplicity and robustness, the bisection method has inspired a number of generalizations. For instance, one natural extension of the bisection algorithm is to increase the number of partitions of the current interval: the multisection methods (or K-section), of which bisection and trisection are particular cases, divide the interval into K equal subintervals [1]. These methods have faster theoretical convergence rates than classical bisection, at the cost of more function evaluations per iteration. Higher-dimensional bracketing methods inspired by bisection, for instance based on simplices, have also been introduced [11, 22]. Another direction has been to adapt the bisection algorithm to settings where the function is uncertain or noisy. The Probabilistic Bisection Algorithm (PBA) [8, 10, 16, 21] constructs and updates a stochastic 2model for the root position based on function queries. Waeber, Frazier, and Henderson [21] proved, using random-walk theory, that the expected error of PBA converges linearly to 0, as in the classical bisection method. Other stochastic variants involving the function f have also been studied; for example, [6, 13] analyze the expected behavior and complexity of the bisection method for random polynomials. The stochastic variant considered in the present paper follows a different philosophy. It is formally analogous to the classical bisection method, but with one crucial modification: the function f remains deterministic, while the cut cn, instead of being chosen as the midpoint of the current interval, is sampled at random from a distribution D supported on [an, b n]:

cut( an, b n) = cn ∼ D (an, b n), n ∈ N.

In this sense, our framework differs fundamentally from the PBA: the randomness does not come from noisy evaluations or uncertainty about the function, but from the algorithmic choice of the cut itself. Our main objective in this paper is to determine the expected convergence rate of such a method. To simplify the analysis with respect to the choices of the cuts and endpoints, a scale-invariant algorithm (Algorithm 2) generating the length of the interval is analyzed instead. The starting interval is chosen to be [0 , 1] without any loss of generality, since a function with a root in [a, b ] can be rescaled via an affine transformation to have its root in [0 , 1] . The root is then also normalized to its relative position in the interval [0 , 1] at each iteration. Note that this algorithm is not meant to be used in practice, since r is generally unknown.

Because the stochastic bisection algorithm generates an iterated random map on the unit interval, it is tempting to view it in the framework of stochastic dynamical systems and ergodic theory. In this setting, one studies stationary distributions, invariant measures, and convergence toward equilibrium for random compositions of transformations. A classical result in this field is due to Diaconis and Freedman [4], who gave sufficient conditions for the uniqueness of a stationary distribution for random iterated Lipschitz maps. This result requires that the map is contractive on average, which is not satisfied by the map T . Indeed, the random skewed Dyadic map is expanding on average, as is the deterministic Dyadic map. Hence, contraction-based ergodic results such as the one of Diaconis and Freedman cannot be used directly to establish or identify a stationary distribution in our setting. Expansive maps have long been studied in dynamical systems. As early as 1973, Lasota and Yorke [14] used the Frobenius–Perron operator to prove L1 convergence of Cesàro averages for piecewise C2 expanding maps of the unit interval, thereby establishing the existence of one or infinitely many invariant distributions 4for this class. These ideas were later extended to random maps with finitely many branches [12, 15], and related systems continue to be investigated in recent work [23]. Other interval maps with random parameters, such as skewed tent maps [2] and β-transformations [20], exhibit dynamical behaviors qualitatively similar to those of the transformation T (c, r ) considered here, including chaotic and mixing properties. Nevertheless, the stochastic bisection algorithm differs from these models in a significant way: the randomness enters through a continuous parameter governing the slope of the map at each iteration, rather than through a finite set of branches. As a result, these ergodic-theoretic results do not directly yield explicit information on the invariant distribution or on convergence rate of the process. Although it is plausible that the algorithm could be studied within a sufficiently refined framework of random dynamical systems, doing so would require technical machinery and specialized terminology. For this reason, we adopt in the present work a more elementary probabilistic approach based on geo-metric considerations. This viewpoint allows us to derive explicit formulas describing the evolution of the interval length and the distribution of the normalized root, and to complement the analytical results with numerical experiments. To the best of our knowledge, the probabilistic properties established in this paper have not been previously reported in the literature. 

1.4. Outline of the paper 

The main objective of this paper is to determine explicit expected convergence rates for the stochastic bisection algorithm. To achieve this goal, we progressively analyze the probabilistic structure of the random process generated by the algorithm under increasingly general assumptions. In Section 2, we first consider the setting where the a priori distribution of the root is uniform and the cut distribution is arbitrary. This case serves as a fundamental step toward understanding the convergence behavior of the algorithm. Using elementary probabilistic arguments and geometric interpretations, we show that all the normalized roots are i.i.d., we explicitly describe the distribution of the scaling factor ℓn and its first moments in terms of the cut distribution, and we obtain formulas for the expected contraction of the interval. Together, these results allow us to show that random perturbations of the classical bisection rule give rise to a scale-invariant stochastic dynamics: the normalized root position converges to a uniform invariant distribution, allowing explicit computation of the expected convergence rate of the algorithm. In Section 3, we remove the assumption of a uniform root distribution. This requires additional analysis, since many results of Section 2 rely on the preservation of uniformity along the process. Using tools from operator theory, we prove that any initial distribution converges toward the stationary uniform distribution and establish an explicit rate for this convergence. This shows that the convergence rates derived in Section 2 describe the asymptotic behavior of the algorithm in full generality. In Section 4, we study a natural generalization of the method, namely a stochastic multisection algorithm in which several random cuts are chosen at each iteration. For clarity, we restrict attention to the case where both the root and the cuts are uniformly distributed. We show that the uniform distribution remains stationary for this extended process and derive explicit expected convergence rates as functions of the number of cuts. This analysis allows us to compare the efficiency of randomized multisection strategies with the classical deterministic bisection method. Finally, Section 5 presents numerical experiments designed to validate the theoretical predictions obtained in the previous sections. By considering various qualitative features of the root and cut distributions (such as asymmetry or concentration of mass), these experiments also provide insight into the robustness of the predicted convergence behavior and help illustrate general trends in the performance of the algorithm.

5.3. Pathological case: correlation in the ℓi

The uniformity of r was instrumental in proving Theorem 8 (i.e., that ℓi is independent from ℓj for 

i̸ = j). We would like to construct a counterexample for a non-uniform distribution of the root. By taking a distribution with a lot of mass concentrated near one of the boundaries, for instance Beta(5 , 50) , the root will be situated near the left end of the interval with high probability. By also taking this exact distribution for the cut, we expect it to be very close to the position of the root with high probability. The following phenomenon thus happens: if the cut falls a bit to the left of the root, ℓ1 will be very long (Figure 14). On the other hand, if the cut falls a bit to the right of the root, ℓ1 will be shorter (Figure 15). On the next iteration, after the rescaling, the root will either be near the left boundary (if ℓ1 was large) or will now be near the right boundary (if ℓ1 was small).

6. Conclusion 

In this paper, we studied convergence properties for a family of stochastic bisection methods in which the cut is chosen randomly according to any arbitrary distribution. When the position of the root is assumed to be uniformly distributed over the search interval, we derived explicit expected convergence rates depending only upon the mean and variance of the distribution of the cut. In particular, we showed that the relative length of the new working interval is independent from all previous information, which allowed us to compute the expected length of the interval at iteration n in a manner similar to the classical deterministic bisection method. On average, the convergence is linear, and the expected interval length behaves as CnL0, where 

C = 1 − E[c(1 − c)] is the expected contraction factor. We then extended our results to any initial prior distribution of the root. Exploiting the stationarity of the uniform distribution for the normalized root process, we showed that the convergence rate approaches the one obtained in the uniform case. We also introduced and analyzed a multisection generalization in which 23 several random cuts are chosen at each iteration, and we determined its average convergence behavior. The theoretical predictions were validated numerically through statistical simulations, which provided confidence intervals supporting the predicted convergence rates. These experiments also suggest that convergence toward the stationary regime may be slower when the prior distribution of the root is strongly asymmetric, while in symmetric situations the theoretical rates for the distribution of the root appear conservative and convergence occurs faster in practice. Several questions remain open. (1) When the cut distribution is highly concentrated around the midpoint, numerical evidence suggests that the stochastic algorithm has a probability exceeding 50% of converging at least as fast as the classical deterministic bisection method for a fixed root. This raises the problem of identifying optimal cut distributions that minimize the number of iterations required to achieve a prescribed tolerance with high probability. (2) In this work, the cut distribution was assumed to be fixed across iterations. A natural extension would allow the distribution to vary dynamically. While it is quite reasonable to assume that multiple results that we obtained would hold in this setting, one may wonder how the choice of the distributions influences the convergence rate of the algorithm, and if there exists an optimal sequence of distributions which maximizes the convergence rates for a given prior distribution of the root. (3) Our analysis was made in the special case where the function f whose root we seek to estimate is unique in the interval [a, b ]. One may wonder how allowing the possibility of f having multiple roots in [a, b ]

might affect our analysis. It is unclear to the present authors how this case would compare to the results of this paper. (4) Finally, studying the algorithm within a more systematic framework of random dynamical systems and ergodic theory could provide a more modern interpretation of the results obtained here, and possibly give insight in the study of other similar dynamical systems. These findings illustrate how simple random perturbations of classical numerical procedures can generate rich probabilistic dynamics, potentially opening new directions for the analysis of stochastic algorithms and their convergence laws.
