# Bregman projection for calibration estimation 

## Jae Kwang Kim Yonghyun Kwon Yumou Qiu 

Abstract 

Calibration weighting is a fundamental technique in survey sampling and data inte-gration for incorporating auxiliary information and improving efficiency of estimators. Classical calibration methods are typically formulated through distance functions ap-plied to weight ratios relative to design weights. In this paper we develop a unified framework for calibration estimation based on Bregman divergence defined directly on the weight vector. We show that calibration estimators obtained from Bregman divergence admit a dual representation that depends only on the dimension of the aux-iliary variables and can be interpreted as a Bregman projection onto the calibration constraint set. This geometric structure leads to a general asymptotic representation showing that calibration estimators are equivalent to debiased regression estimators whose regression coefficient depends on the choice of the Bregman generator. The re-sult provides a unifying perspective on classical calibration methods such as quadratic calibration and exponential tilting, and reveals how the choice of divergence influences efficiency. Under Poisson sampling we further characterize the generator that minimizes the asymptotic variance of the calibration estimator and obtain an optimal contrast entropy divergence. The framework also extends naturally to settings where inclusion probabilities are unknown and must be estimated, yielding cross-fitted estimators that remain root-n consistent under mild conditions. Finally, we develop a regularized cali-bration estimator suitable for high-dimensional auxiliary variables. Simulation studies and a real data application illustrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

# 1 Introduction 

Calibration weighting plays a central role in modern survey statistics and missing data analysis, serving as a principled mechanism for integrating auxiliary information to correct for selection bias and improve estimation efficiency. Given an initial set of design weights, calibration adjusts these weights to reproduce known population totals of auxiliary variables while maintaining proximity to the original design (Fuller, 2002). The seminal formulation of Deville and S┬© arndal (1992) expressed calibration as the min-imization of a convex distance between the calibrated and design weights under linear cal-ibration constraints, giving rise to a unified framework that encompasses the exponential tilting (ET) estimator (Kim, 2010; Hainmueller, 2012) or pseudo empirical likelihood esti-mator (Wu and Rao, 2006) as a special case. This approach, and its numerous extensions, has become foundational methods in finite population inference (Devaud and Till┬┤ e, 2019), model-assisted estimation (Breidt and Opsomer, 2017), and more recently, causal inference based on covariate balancing (Imai and Ratkovic, 2014; Chan et al., 2016; Zhao, 2019). The DevilleŌĆōS┬© arndal framework is mathematically equivalent to minimizing an f -divergence (Csisz┬┤ ar and Shields, 2004) between the ratio of the final weights to the initial weightsŌĆöthat is, a measure of distortion in the relative scale of weights rather than their absolute positions in the weight space. Although the method yields consistent and efficient estimators, its ge-ometric interpretation can be somewhat misleading: the optimization operates on the ratio

Recently, Kwon et al. (2025) developed a new method of calibration based on the generalized 2entropy function of Gneiting and Raftery (2007). In this paper, we extend the idea of Kwon et al. (2025) further and develop a comprehen-sive theory of calibration weighting based on the Bregman divergence, which generalizes and unifies a wide family of existing distance functions. The Bregman divergence offers an elegant geometric foundation that interprets calibration as an information projection of the design weights onto the constraint manifold defined by auxiliary variables. Within this framework, classical distance measures such as the quadratic, KullbackŌĆōLeibler, and Hellinger metrics emerge as special cases corresponding to different convex generators G(Žē). More impor-tantly, the Bregman formalism reveals a dual optimization problem whose objective is itself a Bregman divergence of the convex conjugate function F , exposing a precise primalŌĆōdual symmetry between the weight and multiplier spaces. This duality leads to both theoretical insight and computational simplification, reducing a high-dimensional constrained optimiza-tion problem in the weights to an unconstrained, low-dimensional problem in the Lagrange multipliers (Amari and Nagaoka, 2000; Banerjee et al., 2005). From an inferential perspective, the proposed estimator retains the desirable design-consistency and asymptotic normality of traditional calibration estimators while generalizing them to an extended entropy family. A key distinction from the DevilleŌĆōS┬© arndal framework is that the calibration estimator is asymptotically equivalent to a debiased prediction estimator whose regression coefficient depends explicitly on the choice of the generator function G(┬Ę). This dependence enables deliberate tuning of efficiency through the selection of the diver-gence function. Under Poisson sampling, we identify a unique contrast-entropy function that satisfies the condition for design-optimality, yielding the minimum asymptotic variance within the proposed class. 3The contribution of the paper can be summarized as follows. First, we develop a novel calibration framework based on Bregman divergence that operates directly in weight space, revealing calibration as a geometric projection with an elegant primalŌĆōdual structure: both the weightŌĆōspace and multiplierŌĆōspace problems minimize Bregman divergences, reducing computation from n to p dimensions. Second, unlike the DevilleŌĆōS┬© arndal framework, our formulation yields a weighted regression coefficient that depends explicitly on the gener-ator G(┬Ę). This permits efficiency tuning through generator selection, and we identify a contrast-entropy function that achieves designŌĆōoptimality under Poisson sampling. Third, we establish the asymptotic properties of the proposed calibration estimator under two set-tings: (i) a design-based framework with known inclusion probabilities, where design consis-tency, asymptotic normality, and consistent variance estimation are obtained under standard sampling design conditions; and (ii) a non-probability sampling framework with unknown propensity scores, where a cross-fitting procedure yields doubly robust estimation requir-ing only that the product of the propensity and outcome model errors vanishes faster than

Lagrangian duality produces a H┬© older-conjugate penalized dual, which, in the case q = Ōł×, yields a weighted Ōäō1 penalty that performs implicit, data-adaptive selection of calibration variables. An outcome-guided choice of tolerances, analogous to adaptive-Lasso weighting, is introduced with cross-validation for tuning. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the basic setup and introduces notation. Section 3 presents the Bregman divergence formulation and its dual representation. Section 4 establishes asymptotic properties, including the design-optimal en-4tropy function, doubly robust inference with cross-fitting, and variance estimation. Section 5 develops the regularized Bregman calibration framework for high-dimensional covariates. Sec-tion 6 reports simulation evidence demonstrating the theoretical findings. Section 7 presents a real data analysis using NOAAŌĆÖs Large Pelagics Intercept Survey. Section 8 concludes with discussions and directions for future research. All technical proofs are relegated to the supplementary material (SM).

We use Large Pelagics Intercept Survey (LPIS) at National Oceanic and Atmospheric Ad-ministration (NOAA) as a test bed for calibration weighting in a setting where inclusion probabilities vary and rich frame-level auxiliary information is available (Foster et al., 2008). The public LPIS sampling frame contains 74,253 siteŌĆōdayŌĆōtime units (saltwater fishing sites crossed with day and time block) across nine Atlantic states during JuneŌĆōOctober. LPIS employs a complex stratified, multi-stage design, with unequal-probability sampling without replacement (PPSWOR). Selection probabilities are driven by cluster-level fishing pressure and day type (e.g., weekday vs. weekend; tournament vs. non-tournament), and field assign-ments vary in duration (about 2ŌĆō8 hours). In practice, operational flexibilities (e.g., variable site choice within clusters and emphasis on afternoon sampling) can induce coverage gaps that are difficult to fully account for if major design features are ignored in estimation, motivating careful use of weighting and calibration. Treating the sampling frame as a finite population, we generate population-level boat-trip counts as independent (truncated) Poisson draws and simulate eleven catch outcomes under alternative tripŌĆōcatch relationships (ranging from no association/binary catch to retention and harvest mechanisms under moderate/high catch rates). The trip counts and eleven catch outcomes (12 response variables total) are treated as yi. Calibration covariates are available at the frame level and include fishing pressure, day type (weekend versus weekday), boat mode (charter versus private), county, and a noise variable included as a negative control. For 1,000 repeated replications, we select units under unequalŌĆōprobability sampling with expected sample size n = 1 ,135, using selection probabilities proportional to frame-level 33 inclusion probabilities. We compare the DevilleŌĆōS┬© arndal (DS) and Bregman-divergence cal-ibration (BC) estimators under exponential tilting (ET), empirical likelihood (EL), and Hellinger distance (HD); we also include BC with contrast entropy (CE). Performance is summarized across the 12 outcomes, and we report RMSE scaled relative to BCŌĆōCE to facilitate comparisons. # 8 Discussion 

We have proposed a unified calibration weighting framework rooted in the Bregman di-vergence. By formulating calibration directly in the weight space rather than on weight ratios, the framework reveals calibration as a geometric projection with a primalŌĆōdual sym-metry: both the weight-space and multiplier-space problems are instances of Bregman di-vergence minimization, the latter reducing a high-dimensional constrained problem to an unconstrained optimization in p dimensions. A central finding is that, unlike the DevilleŌĆōS┬© arndal framework, the regression coeffi-cient in the equivalent debiased prediction estimator depends explicitly on the choice of the generator G(┬Ę). This dependence is a key strength: it enables deliberate efficiency tuning through generator selection. The contrast-entropy function was identified as the generator satisfying the condition for design-optimality under Poisson sampling, and the simulation studies confirmed that BC-CE achieves the best overall performance when inclusion proba-bilities are known. When propensity scores must be estimated, the cross-fitting procedure combined with calibration yields doubly robust estimation, requiring only that the product of the propensity estimation error and the outcome approximation error vanishes faster than 35 nŌłÆ1/2.For high-dimensional auxiliary information, we developed a regularized Bregman cali-bration approach that replaces exact balance with Ōäōq-norm tolerance constraints. Through Lagrangian duality, the primal constraint produces a H┬© older-conjugate penalty in the dual; the case q = Ōł× yields a weighted Ōäō1 penalty that performs implicit variable selection, and an outcome-guided choice of tolerances provides an adaptive-Lasso analogue for calibra-tion. The simulation results showed that the cross-validated data-driven procedure recovers near-oracle performance. Extending the theoretical analysis of the regularized calibration estimator, including establishing rates of convergence and oracle properties, is an impor-tant topic for future research. Also, the proposed method can be extended beyond survey sampling, including causal inference and transfer learning. Such extensions will be reported elsewhere.
