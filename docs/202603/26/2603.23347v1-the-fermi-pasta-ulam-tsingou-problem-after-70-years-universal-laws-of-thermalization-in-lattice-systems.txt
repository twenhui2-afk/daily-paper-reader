Abstract 

Over the past decade, significant progress has been made toward clarifying a central question raised by the Fermi–Pasta–Ulam–Tsingou problem: whether weakly nonlinear lattice systems thermalize and, if so, by what mechanisms. The current understanding can be summarized as follows. (a) Classical lattice systems fall into two universal classes with respect to their thermalization behavior. In the first class, the Hamiltonian possesses extended normal modes. For such systems, provided the system size is sufficiently large, the thermalization time Teq scales inversely with the square of the effective nonlinear strength g (which measures the perturbation strength or degree of non-integrability), namely Teq ∼ g−γ with γ = 2 . Consequently, in the thermodynamic limit these systems inevitably thermalize. Typical examples include common one-, two-, and three-dimensional lattice models. In the second class, all normal modes are localized. In this case the relaxation time is essentially independent of system size. Although the thermalization time can still be formally expressed as Teq ∼ g−γ ,the exponent γ diverges as g → 0. This implies that arbitrarily weak nonlinear perturbations cannot induce thermalization. For sufficiently small g, such systems may therefore be regarded as thermal insulators in a theoretical sense. (b) For systems in the first class, disorder does not hinder thermalization. On the contrary, since disorder breaks translational symmetry and thereby relaxes wave-vector resonance constraints, it increases the number of quasi-resonant processes. As a result, weak disorder can accelerate the thermalization process. (c) In systems of the second class, when both on-site potentials and disorder are present, all normal modes become localized in sufficiently large systems, leading to the suppression of thermalization. The perturbative theoretical framework underlying these conclusions will also be systematically presented. In particular, we emphasize the thermalization criterion based on the connectivity of resonance networks, an approach originating from the theory of weak wave turbulence. 

Keywords: Fermi-Pasta-Ulam-Tsingou problem, Thermalization, Universal scaling laws, Universality classes 

# 1 Introduction 

The Fermi–Pasta–Ulam–Tsingou (FPUT) problem originated from fundamental questions concerning the dynam-ical foundations of statistical mechanics [1]. The applicability of thermodynamics and equilibrium statistical mechanics relies on the assumption that nonequilibrium systems can relax toward equilibrium. In particular, in the thermodynamic limit, arbitrarily weak nonlinear interactions are thought to be sufficient to drive a many-body Hamiltonian system toward energy equipartition or thermal equilibrium [2–4]. The original FPUT numerical experiments were designed to illustrate this relaxation process and to quantify the timescale on which a weakly nonlinear lattice approaches equipartition. Unexpectedly, the simulations revealed a strikingly different behavior: Instead of thermalizing, the energy returned almost completely to the initial mode—a phenomenon now known as FPUT recurrence. Understanding and reconciling this discrepancy between numerical observations and the basic assumptions of statistical physics has constituted the central challenge of the FPUT problem for more than seventy 1

years. The FPUT studies initiated the numerical investigation of (nonlinear) dynamical systems and stimulated major developments in several directions, including chaos and fractal dynamics, soliton theory, integrable systems, discrete breathers, and anomalous heat transport [5, 6]. Despite this broad impact, a systematic understanding of the original statistical-mechanical question—whether and how weakly nonlinear lattices thermalize—has largely emerged only in recent years. The understanding of the FPUT problem was significantly advanced by the development of the Kol-mogorov–Arnold–Moser (KAM) theorem [7]. Proposed by Kolmogorov in 1954 [8] and later rigorously established by Arnold [9] and Moser [10], the KAM theorem states that for a non-integrable Hamiltonian system, most invari-ant tori persist under sufficiently weak perturbations, provided the frequencies are Diophantine and non-degeneracy conditions hold. As a result, even when integrability is broken by perturbations, the motion for a large set of initial conditions remains quasi-periodic. This quasi-periodic motion naturally explains the near-recurrence phe-nomena observed in the original FPUT experiment. However, for systems with three or more degrees of freedom, invariant tori—though still of positive measure—no longer foliate phase space into dynamically isolated regions. In 1964, Arnold showed that in such systems arbitrarily small perturbations may lead to the phenomenon now known as Arnold diffusion [11], whereby trajectories can slowly drift along resonance channels between neighbor-ing tori. In principle, this mechanism allows trajectories to explore a large portion of the phase space left by the destruction of invariant tori, although the associated diffusion process becomes exponentially slow as the pertur-bation strength decreases. Further progress was made by Nekhoroshev, who proved that for analytic Hamiltonian systems satisfying suitable non-degeneracy conditions, there exists a perturbation threshold below which the vari-ation of action variables remains bounded over exponentially long times [12]. More precisely, the stability time admits a stretched-exponential lower bound as a function of the perturbation strength. Taken together, the KAM theorem, Arnold diffusion, and Nekhoroshev stability theory indicate that thermalization in FPUT-type systems is controlled by perturbation strength: below a certain effective threshold, the thermalization time can become extraordinarily long, far exceeding accessible observation times. Although these theoretical developments provide a clear conceptual framework for the dynamics of near-integrable Hamiltonian systems, the specific questions posed by the FPUT problem—such as whether energy ultimately reaches equipartition and how the thermalization time depends on system parameters—have largely been addressed through numerical investigations. From the 1980s to the 1990s, extensive simulations gradually led to a widely accepted picture: when the nonlinear perturbation is sufficiently strong, the system eventually ther-malizes. During this process, researchers observed a transition from weak chaos to strong chaos [13]. In particular, the dependence of the thermalization time on the energy density exhibits qualitatively different behaviors in dif-ferent regimes [14, 15]. This reflects a dynamical crossover associated with the strength of nonlinear perturbations, separating regimes of extremely slow relaxation from those of relatively rapid equipartition. However, even in the regime of weak nonlinear perturbations, numerical studies have produced results that appear highly inconsistent. Some simulations support the Nekhoroshev-type prediction that the thermalization time follows a stretched-exponential scaling of the form Teq ∼ exp( c/ε a), where ε is the energy density and 0 <a < 1 [16]. Other investigations instead report a power-law dependence, ε−γ [17–20]. These discrepancies suggest that the relaxation dynamics is strongly influenced by several factors, including the choice of initial conditions, system size, nonlinear strength, and the detailed structure of the interaction potential. As a consequence, extracting universal laws of thermalization directly from numerical simulations has proven difficult. Moreover, even when power-law scaling is observed, the measured exponent γ varies significantly across different models and parameter regimes, and may differ substantially even within the same model under different conditions [14]. This variability further highlights the complexity of the relaxation process in weakly nonlinear lattices. While the KAM-based line of research elucidates mechanisms such as the breakdown of integrability and the emergence of local chaos, it is less suited for providing a complete analytical description of the macroscopic relax-ation of the system toward statistical equilibrium. Beginning in the 1980s and 1990s, several approaches from nonequilibrium statistical mechanics were gradually developed, including mode-coupling theory [21] and kinetic theories based on the statistics of resonance detuning [22]. More recently, the framework of weak wave turbulence theory has been introduced to the study of the FPUT problem [23–25]. These approaches provide a statistical-physical route based on kinetic equations for describing energy transfer among interacting modes. Wave turbulence theory has been widely applied in physical oceanography, plasma physics, nonlinear optics, and Bose–Einstein con-densation, where it offers a clear physical picture and powerful analytical tools for understanding energy cascades in weakly nonlinear wave systems. Within this framework, the evolution of the wave-action spectrum is described by multi-wave kinetic equations derived through perturbation theory. In contrast to approaches based primarily on invariant structures in phase space, the wave-turbulence perspective interprets thermalization as a diffusion pro-cess of energy among normal modes mediated by multi-wave resonances. The key question then becomes whether the corresponding resonance network is globally connected. Assuming that the long-time dynamics is dominated by 2such resonant interactions, thermalization can occur through energy spreading across this network. For the FPUT-

α [26] and FPUT-β models [27], it has been shown that the network of exact six-wave resonances is connected, leading to the conclusion that these systems ultimately thermalize with a power-law timescale. In this paper we summarize the contributions of our Xiamen University group to the study of the FPUT prob-lem. In Sec. 2 we present a general perturbative framework based on action–angle variables and the corresponding kinetic equations. This formulation applies to classic lattices and can also be extended to quantum systems. In this section we also discuss the connectivity of n-wave resonance networks and the associated quasi-resonance criteria. Section 3 presents numerical evidence showing that, under weak perturbations, the thermalization time of typical lattice systems follows the universal scaling predicted by the kinetic theory, namely an inverse-square dependence on the effective nonlinear strength. Previous numerical studies reported various power-law relations between the thermalization time and the nonlinear parameter, with different exponents. We demonstrate that these discrepancies originate primarily from the choice of reference integrable points in numerical simulations. When an appropriate integrable limit is chosen so that the non-integrable contribution acts as a weak perturbation, generic Hamiltonian lattice systems exhibit a universal scaling in the large-system limit, where the thermalization time is proportional to the inverse square of the perturbation strength. This behavior is observed in one-, two-, and three-dimensional systems. Section 4 introduces the concept of thermalization universality classes. Lattice systems can be divided into two classes according to the qualitative nature of their thermalization behavior. The classification criterion is whether the normal modes of the Hamiltonian include extended states. Systems with extended modes belong to the first class, which includes the models studied in Sec. 3 as well as most systems previously investigated in the FPUT literature. The second class consists of systems whose normal modes are entirely localized. As an illustrative example, we present numerical results for a one-dimensional lattice ϕ4 model. Owing to the presence of an on-site potential and tunable disorder, this model exhibits parameter regimes with fully extended modes, partially extended modes, and completely localized modes. The final section summarizes the results and discusses their implications for the general problem of thermalization in weakly nonlinear many-body systems.

# 3 Universal law of thermalization 

The FPUT problem can be viewed as a fundamental question concerning how a near-integrable Hamiltonian system approaches equilibrium under weak nonlinear perturbations. For sufficiently large systems, truncating the kinetic equation at second order is usually sufficient to capture the dominant scattering processes and their qualitative dynamical behavior. As a consequence, the relaxation rate of the normal modes follows the second-order perturbative scaling. Equivalently, the thermalization time is inversely proportional to the square of the perturbation strength. More precisely, writing the Hamiltonian in the form H = H0 + gV , where g characterizes the effective perturbation strength measuring the deviation from integrability, the thermalization time follows 

Teq ∝ g−2. A crucial requirement for the validity of this relation is that the integrable reference Hamiltonian 

H0 must be chosen appropriately. The choice of H0 determines both the explicit form and magnitude of the effective perturbation strength g, and therefore directly affects the quantitative characterization of the system’s thermalization capability. The various power-law exponents for thermalization reported in early studies can largely be attributed to two factors. The first is numerical bias arising from finite system sizes and limited computational resources. The second is the improper choice of the reference integrable Hamiltonian, which leads to a distorted definition of the effective perturbation strength. Extensive studies in recent years have shown that once an appropriate integrable reference system H0 is chosen and the perturbation strength g is consistently defined, the scaling relation 

Teq ∝ g−2 (19) exhibits remarkable robustness [32–37]. Importantly, this conclusion is largely insensitive to the specific mechanism by which integrability is broken. For example, integrability may be destroyed by introducing nonlinear interactions [32, 33], by incorporating mass disorder [34, 35], or through stress/strain modulation [36]. Moreover, the same scaling behavior is observed regardless of whether the initial excitation is placed in low-frequency modes or high-frequency modes (the so-called anti-FPUT scenario) [37]. In all these cases, the thermalization time follows the same inverse-square scaling once the perturbation strength is defined within a consistent perturbative framework. Consequently, the thermalization time remains essentially unchanged as the mass difference varies. Instead, the relaxation time of this system depends sensitively on the energy density, which determines the effective nonlinear perturbation strength [34]. It should be emphasized that the existence of a universal scaling law does not imply that the thermalization pathways are identical. While the symmetry of the interaction potential does not affect the final approach to energy equipartition, it can significantly modify the intermediate dynamical processes leading to equilibrium. For instance, asymmetric interaction potentials enhance the contribution of higher-order processes [38] and induce asymmetric broadening of phonon spectral peaks [32]. Studies of diatomic chains further show that the order in which equipartition is established between the acoustic and optical branches may even be reversed depending on the underlying mechanism, although the final thermalization time Teq still follows the same scaling law [34]. These results indicate that while the scaling of the thermalization time is universal, the microscopic pathways toward equilibrium can vary substantially depending on the specific interaction mechanisms. Moreover, recent studies of one-dimensional diatomic gases indicate that the inverse-square scaling of the thermalization time can persist even when the Lyapunov exponent vanishes, provided the system remains close to the integrable regime. When the system moves far away from this regime, long-time correlations associated with hydrodynamic effects may dominate the dynamics and modify the relaxation behavior. The analysis indicates that Anderson-localized modes are generally unstable in the presence of nonlinear interactions. Another important finding is that weak mass disorder can accelerate thermalization. This effect arises because the introduction of mass disorder relaxes the wave-vector constraints in the resonance conditions, thereby allowing more resonant and quasi-resonant processes to occur [35, 40]. From an application perspective, real condensed-matter systems are primarily two- and three-dimensional. Therefore, extending the analysis from one dimension to higher dimensions is not merely a formal generalization but a necessary step toward understanding realistic material systems. However, the theoretical and numerical complexity increases significantly in higher-dimensional systems, and systematic results have long been lacking. Early pioneering works in the 1970s and 1980s already identified chaos thresholds in two-dimensional FPUT models [41–44]. Due to the limited computing power available at that time, important questions such as the scaling of the threshold with system size, the persistence of weak-chaos regimes, and the ultimate fate of FPUT recurrences remained unresolved. With the advent of modern high-performance computing, Benettin and collaborators studied a two-dimensional triangular lattice and reported a power-law dependence of the thermalization time on the energy density. Interestingly, the exponent was found to depend on boundary conditions: γ = 1 for fixed or open boundaries and γ = 5 /4 for periodic boundaries [45, 46]. Meanwhile, studies motivated by realistic materials such as graphene have shown that out-of-plane flexural modes play a crucial role in energy redistribution and thermal transport [47–49]. Despite these pioneering efforts, our current understanding of thermalization in higher-dimensional lattices remains relatively limited. Existing studies are largely restricted to specific lattice geometries (such as triangular lattices) and particular interaction potentials (e.g., Lennard–Jones potentials). Consequently, the influence of spa-tial dimensionality on the qualitative and quantitative features of thermalization, as well as the role of disorder in higher-dimensional lattices, remains insufficiently understood. In recent years, the Xiamen University group has carried out systematic investigations of two- and three-dimensional lattice systems. On the one hand, these stud-ies confirm that higher-dimensional systems also follow the universal inverse-square scaling law for thermalization (see Fig. 5). On the other hand, they reveal the crucial role played by spatial dimensionality. In higher-dimensional lattices, the number of nearest-neighbor interactions grows rapidly, leading to a much more complex phase-space geometry. In particular, symmetry-induced degeneracies of characteristic modes fundamentally modify the dynami-cal properties of the system. These structural features create additional pathways for energy transport and generate dense resonance-coupling networks, which significantly facilitate the thermalization process. These findings high-light the importance of dimensionality and resonance-network structure in determining the efficiency of energy redistribution in nonlinear lattice systems.

# 5 Conclusion and Discussion 

In summary, on the occasion of the 70th anniversary of the FPUT problem, the thermalization laws of weakly nonlinear lattices in the thermodynamic limit have become increasingly clear. Perturbative analysis generally leads to a kinetic equation describing the evolution of normal-mode energies, from which the thermalization time is predicted to scale with the effective nonlinear strength according to the inverse-square law. Specifically, if the Hamiltonian is written as H = H0 + gV , where H0 represents the integrable reference system and g characterizes the effective perturbation strength, the thermalization time follows Teq ∝ g−2. However, further examination of the validity conditions of the kinetic equation deepens our understanding of lattice thermalization. Within this framework, all eigenmodes must be connected through n-wave resonances to form a globally connected resonance network, allowing energy to propagate from any mode to any other mode. The investigation of this connectivity leads naturally to the discovery of different universality classes of thermalization. For lattice systems possessing extended modes, the lowest-order nonlinear interaction in the Taylor expansion of the interaction potential always generates a connected n-wave resonance network in the thermodynamic limit. Consequently, the inverse-square 11 thermalization law Teq ∝ g−2 emerges as a universal feature in most lattice systems. Numerical simulations indicate that this scaling already appears clearly in systems containing only several thousand particles. An important point is that the observation of this law requires an appropriate decomposition of the Hamiltonian: the system must be separated into an optimal integrable part and a perturbation around that integrable point so that the effective nonlinear parameter correctly measures the deviation from integrability. These systems constitute the first universality class of thermalization. A qualitatively different behavior arises in lattices whose eigenmodes are completely localized. In such systems the connectivity of the resonance network decreases monotonically as the effective nonlinear strength decreases, eventually leading to fragmentation of the n-wave resonance network. As a consequence, the thermalization time follows a hierarchy of scaling laws Teq ∝ g−m, m = 2 , 4, 6, 8, · · · , where the exponent m increases as the nonlinear strength becomes weaker. This implies that these systems cannot be thermalized by arbitrarily small perturbations, suggesting the existence of thermal insulating states. Another notable feature is that the thermalization law in this class is essentially independent of system size. The investigation of resonance-network connectivity also raises the long-standing question of whether finite lattices can always thermalize. Based on exact resonance conditions, it has been shown that six-wave resonances under periodic boundary conditions can form a connected network [26], suggesting that finite systems should in principle thermalize with a scaling Teq ∝ g−6. However, our recent studies indicate that the kinetic equation derived from exact resonances may break down during the evolution due to intrinsic symmetry constraints. Instead, quasi-resonances appear to dominate energy diffusion. Since the strength of quasi-resonances and the connectivity of the corresponding resonance network depend on the effective nonlinear parameter, the thermalization time follows the hierarchy Teq ∝ g−m, with the exponent m increasing as the nonlinearity decreases. This suggests the existence of a nonlinear threshold for thermalization in finite systems. Clarifying this issue is important not only for revisiting the original FPUT experiment but also for establishing the statistical-mechanical foundations of finite systems. Despite these important advances, the FPUT problem remains far from fully resolved. Seventy years after its discovery, it continues to open new directions of research. From a theoretical perspective, several important prob-lems remain. First, while the weakly nonlinear regime is relatively well understood, the thermalization laws in the strongly nonlinear regime are still poorly characterized. Numerical simulations indicate systematic deviations from the inverse-square scaling in this regime, implying that perturbative kinetic theory is no longer sufficient and that new theoretical approaches are required. Strong nonlinearity can also lead to rich phenomena, including many-body localization-like behaviors in classical lattices [57, 58]. Second, thermalization and thermal transport are believed to be closely related at the microscopic level. Previous studies have revealed deterministic relations between the scaling exponents governing thermalization and anomalous heat conduction [59]. Recently, we have established such connections systematically in a one-dimensional diatomic gas model [60]. Further exploration of this relationship is important because heat conduction is one of the fundamental macroscopic properties of materi-als. Third, the study of high-dimensional lattices has only just begun. Although the inverse-square thermalization law appears to remain universal, the dynamical processes in higher dimensions are considerably richer. The spa-tial structure of higher-dimensional lattices introduces additional effects such as mode degeneracy, interactions between acoustic and optical phonon branches, and disorder-induced localization, all of which can significantly influence energy transfer processes in materials. Understanding the roles of different multi-phonon processes, such as three-phonon and four-phonon interactions, therefore provides promising directions for future research. More broadly, the thermalization of open systems also deserves extensive investigation. Within such a frame-work, the role of integrability in determining the relaxation of a system toward equilibrium must be reconsidered from new perspectives [61]. Finally, exploring the similarities and differences between thermalization in classical and quantum systems represents another important direction for future studies.
