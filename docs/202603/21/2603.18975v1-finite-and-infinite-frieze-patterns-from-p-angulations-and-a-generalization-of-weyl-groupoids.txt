FINITE AND INFINITE FRIEZE PATTERNS FROM p-ANGULATIONS AND A GENERALIZATION OF WEYL GROUPOIDS 

MICHAEL CUNTZ, THORSTEN HOLM, AND PETER JØRGENSEN 

Abstract. A classic result of Conway and Coxeter on frieze patterns has recently been general-ized by establishing for every integer p ≥ 3 a bijection between p-angulations of regular polygons and a new class of frieze patterns, called frieze patterns of type Λ p. One of the features of Conway-Coxeter theory is a combinatorial procedure to obtain from the triangulation all entries of the corresponding frieze pattern. We first show how to extend this to dissections and to frieze patterns of type Λ p, that is, we present a combinatorial algorithm, involving Chebyshev poly-nomials, for obtaining from a dissection all entries of the corresponding frieze pattern. As an application we obtain an explicit characterisation of frieze patterns of types Λ 4 and Λ 6 in terms of all entries (not only the quiddity cycle), partially answering a question in an earlier paper. We then study infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p, which appeared in a preprint by Banaian and Chen, generalizing the infinite frieze patterns of positive integers studied by Baur, Parsons and Tschabold. As our main result we obtain a combinatorial model for infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p, that is, we show that these are in bijection with certain p-angulations of an infinite strip. This extends results by Baur, Parsons and Tschabold from p = 3 to arbitrary p ≥ 3, but we provide new insight even in the classic case in that our combinatorial model gives a bijection and also a more transparent constructive approach. It is known that infinite frieze patterns of positive integers naturally appear in the context of Weyl groupoids. In the final section we extend this connection to infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p for any p ≥ 3 by introducing a generalization of Cartan graphs and Weyl groupoids called reflection groupoids. We show that, up to equivalence, there is a 1-1 correspondence between connected simply connected Cartan graphs of type Λ p of rank two with infinitely many vertices permitting a root system and infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p.

1. Introduction 

Triangulations and, more generally, dissections of polygons are ubiquitous objects in mathe-matics. One instance where triangulations appear is the theory of frieze patterns, where a classic result of Conway and Coxeter [6] yields a bijection between triangulations of regular polygons and frieze patterns over positive integers. Only recently, this beautiful theory could be generalized from triangulations to dissections of polygons: In [18] we show that for every natural number 

p ≥ 3 there is a bijection between p-angulations of polygons and a new class of frieze patterns, called frieze patterns of type Λ p. These frieze patterns have entries in the ring of integers of the algebraic number field Q(2 cos( πp )). In our paper [18] we actually show more: for every dissection (not only for p-angulations) one can naturally associate a frieze pattern. Namely, given a dissec-tion, say with subpolygons of sizes q1, . . . , q r, we label each qi-gon by λqi = 2 cos( πqi ). At each vertex of the polygon we then form the sum of the labels of the subpolygons attached to this vertex. Then the series of these sums forms the first non-trivial row of the corresponding frieze pattern. This first row actually determines the entire frieze pattern since the frieze patterns are shown to have no entries equal to zero.

The combinatorial procedure described above only determines the entries in the first non-trivial row. For the classic Conway-Coxeter frieze patterns there is also a nice combinatorial method to determine all entries of the corresponding frieze patterns directly from the triangulation, see for instance [5]. As a useful tool we establish a combinatorial algorithm for producing all entries of the frieze patterns corresponding to arbitrary dissections. This is done in Section 4, see in particular Algorithm 4.1. This procedure needs a variation of the well-known Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind. We introduce these polynomials in Section 3 and recall some of their fundamental properties needed later in the paper. In Section 5 we apply the combinatorial algorithm from Section 4 to give a partial answer to a fundamental question posed in [18], namely whether there is a useful characterisation of all entries of frieze patterns of type Λ p. In Theorem 5.1 we give such an answer for the cases p = 4 and p = 6, extending earlier work by Andritsch [1]. 

Theorem 5.1. Let F be a frieze pattern. Then the following statements are equivalent: (a) F is a frieze pattern of type Λ4 or Λ6.(b) The entries of F satisfy the following two conditions. (i) The odd-numbered rows consist of positive integers. (ii) The even-numbered rows consist of positive integral multiples of √2 (for type Λ4) or √3 (for type Λ6), respectively. 

In Sections 6 and 7 we introduce and study infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p. Infinite frieze patterns of positive integers have been introduced by Tschabold [21] and studied further by Baur, Parsons, Tschabold [4]. Infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p appeared already in the preprint by Banaian and Chen [3], where more generally infinite frieze patterns for dissections have been considered. In all this earlier work, the most important goal has not been achieved, namely finding a bijection between these infinite frieze patterns and a combinatorial model (in the spirit of the classic Conway-Coxeter bijection between frieze patterns of positive integers and triangulations of polygons). Even for the classic case p = 3, the model presented in [4] does not give a bijection. Our main result in Section 7 provides such a bijection for infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p, for any p ≥ 3. 

Theorem 7.17. Let p ≥ 3. There is a bijection between infinite frieze patterns of type Λp and locally finite p-angulations of infinite strips (with vertices Z on the lower line and vertices from a subset S ⊆ Z on the upper line), up to translational renumbering of the vertices on the upper line. All entries of the infinite frieze pattern corresponding to a p-angulation can be computed with Algorithm 4.1. 

This main theorem and the results obtained along the way generalize results on infinite frieze patterns over positive integers to infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p and provide new insight even in the classic case p = 3. In particular, starting from an infinite frieze pattern of type Λ p we obtain an explicit construction for the corresponding p-angulation of an infinite strip. As one consequence we obtain bounds for the entries of infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p (for finite frieze patterns of type Λ p this is a consequence of Algorithm 4.1, see Corollary 4.4). 

Corollary 7.18. Let F be an infinite frieze pattern of type Λp. Then the entries of F belong to the set {1} ∪ [λp, ∞).

Note that this is obvious for p = 3 (as λ3 = 1), but a non-trivial statement for p > 3. As another consequence we get the analog of Theorem 5.1 also for infinite frieze patterns of type Λ 4 and Λ 6 in Corollary 7.19. 3

Weyl groupoids were first introduced under the name of arithmetic root systems by Hecken-berger [14], [15] in order to classify finite dimensional Nichols algebras of diagonal type. Under-standing Nichols algebras is a step within the classification of pointed Hopf algebras proposed by Andruskiewitsch and Schneider [2]. After a system of axioms for Weyl groupoids was proposed in [16] and [9], Heckenberger and the first author gave a complete classification of finite Weyl groupoids in all ranks [11]. The definition of Weyl groupoids uses Cartan graphs, and one feature of these is that Cartan matrices (known from Lie theory) are attached to the vertices. It is known that Weyl groupoids of rank 2 (admitting a root system) are closely related to finite and infinite frieze patterns of positive integers. In the final section of this article we generalize this to frieze patterns of type Λp for any integer p ≥ 3. To this end, we introduce generalized Cartan matrices of type Λ p

and from this Cartan graphs of type Λ p (see Definition 8.2). This leads us to the new notion of reflection groupoids, which generalizes Weyl groupoids from p = 3 to arbitrary p ≥ 3. As our main result in Section 8 get a bijection between infinite frieze patterns of type Λ p and certain Cartan graphs (or reflection groupoids): 

Theorem 8.36. There is a 1-1 correspondence between connected simply connected Cartan graphs of type Λp of rank two with infinitely many vertices permitting a root system up to equiv-alence and (tame) infinite frieze patterns of type Λp up to equivalence.

4. Computing the entries of the frieze patterns 

By definition, the entries of a frieze pattern have to satisfy the unimodular rule. However, the entries of a frieze pattern (with non-zero entries) satisfy many more conditions, the so-called 

Ptolemy relations . We briefly recall this viewpoint here; see for instance [18, Section 2] for more details. Let F be a frieze pattern of height n − 3, and denote the entries by fi,j , as in Definition 2.1. Note that the indices appearing in the triangular fundamental region (highlighted in red in Definition 2.1) are in bijection with the diagonals of a regular n-gon (where the 1’s at the bottom correspond to the edges of the polygon). Since the frieze pattern has a glide symmetry, this viewpoint implies that a frieze pattern of height n − 3 can also be described as an assignment of (positive real) numbers to the edges and diagonals of an n-gon P such that edges are mapped to 1 and for each pair ( i, j ) and ( k, ℓ ) of crossing diagonals, the Ptolemy relation 

fi,j fk,ℓ = fi,k fj,ℓ + fi,ℓ fk,j 

holds (for a proof see for instance [12, Theorem 3.3]). Note that the unimodular rule is just a special case of the Ptolemy relations, namely for the crossing diagonals ( i, j ) and ( i + 1 , j + 1). Let T be a dissection of a polygon P . In Section 2 we have summarized the results from [18] showing how to obtain a frieze pattern FT corresponding to T . More precisely, the quiddity row of FT is specified (summing the labels of subpolygons at each vertex of P ), and the results of 8

[18] imply that this is indeed the quiddity row of a frieze pattern (i.e. by repeatedly applying the unimodular rule one obtains a closing row of 1’s). The aim of this section is to provide a fairly simple procedure, performed on the dissection 

T , for computing all entries of the corresponding frieze pattern FT . That is, we introduce a combinatorial algorithm which produces for every pair ( i, j ) of vertices of the n-gon P a (positive real) number ci,j . We then go on to show that the numbers ci,j coincide with the entries fi,j 

of the frieze pattern FT from [18]. An alternative algorithm, using matchings, appears in [3, Section 6].

Frieze patterns of type Λ p are defined in terms of their quiddity rows (see Definition 2.4). The aim of this section is to provide a combinatorial model for infinite frieze patterns of type Λp for any p ≥ 3. 

Definition 7.1. We consider the two lines in the plane R2 of all points with second coordinate 0 and 1, respectively. We denote the lower line by I and the upper line by II . On the lower line we mark the points with integer coefficients and denote them by iI for i ∈ Z. On the upper line we can have any subset S of the set of points with integer coefficients; to distinguish them from the points on the lower line we denote them by jII for j ∈ S. Note that S could be the empty set. We call these two lines with the marked points iI on the lower line and some subset S of marked points on the upper line an infinite strip . The lower line I is called an infinity-gon .An arc of the infinite strip is connecting two non-neighbouring marked points on the lower line (called peripheral arc and denoted ( iI , j I )) or a marked point on the lower line with a marked point on the upper line (called bridging arc and denoted ( iI , j II )). These arcs are called admissible. We do not allow arcs connecting two non-neighbouring marked points on the upper line. Geometrically, arcs are realized as paths modulo isotopy relative to the end points. We call ( iI , (i + 1) I ) for i ∈ Z an edge of the lower line, and similarly for neighbouring vertices of the upper line. 14 

Definition 7.2. Let p ≥ 3. A p-angulation of an infinite strip with vertex set S on the upper line is a maximal collection of admissible arcs which are pairwise non-crossing and such that the resulting subpolygons are p-gons. A p-angulation T of an infinite strip is called locally finite if each vertex iI on the lower line is connected to only finitely many arcs of T .We consider two p-angulations to be equal if one can be obtained from the other by a translation of the vertex sets on the upper line (that is, the p-angulations are equal up to a renumbering of the upper vertex sets induced by a translation).

All entries in the infinite frieze pattern F are positive (by Definition 6.1), so (R1) is satisfied (cf. Remark 8.35). Moreover, we have 0’s only on the bottom line of F, thus also (R2) is satisfied.
