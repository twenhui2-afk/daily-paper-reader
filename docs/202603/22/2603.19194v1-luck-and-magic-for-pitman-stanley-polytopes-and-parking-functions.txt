LUCK AND MAGIC FOR PITMAN–STANLEY POLYTOPES AND PARKING FUNCTIONS 

NICOLAS AVILA, LUIS FERRONI, ALEJANDRO H. MORALES 

Abstract. Motivated by the combinatorics of parking functions and their several generaliza-tions, we study the Ehrhart theory of Pitman–Stanley polytopes. We prove a strong positivity phenomenon called magic positivity for the Ehrhart polynomials of these polytopes, which in turn implies that their h∗-polynomials are real-rooted (and thus log-concave and unimodal). Our result is achieved by interpreting the coefficients of these Ehrhart polynomials in the magic basis in terms of the number of lucky cars in a modified parking protocol. Furthermore, we address the magic positivity problem for y-generalized permutohedra and also discuss a magic 

combinatorial interpretation for them, under the assumption that the input parameters are sufficiently large.

A consequence of the interpretation for ad is that the Ehrhart polynomial of P provides a discrete analog for the notion of volumes. The computation of volumes of polytopes constitutes by itself a recurring and crucial problem in many areas of mathematics. Due to the geometric interpretations for the three coefficients above, one may be inclined to believe that something analogous happens for the remaining coefficients. In reality, the coefficients a1, a 2, . . . , a d−2 are in general very complicated. There exist general closed formulas by McMullen [McM77], but they depend on certain choices and are not easy to state. Not even the sign pattern for these coefficients is fully understood. As it turns out, they can be negative (and all simultaneously at once, see [HHTY19]). When all the coefficients a1, . . . , a d−2 are non-negative, we say that P is Ehrhart positive. 3.3. Block parking protocol. The goal in this subsection is to complete the first two steps of the overall strategy described in the guide presented earlier in this section. Recall that a parking function of order n is a word p = ( p1, . . . , p n) of integers p1, . . . , p n ∈ [n], satisfying that, for each j ∈ [n], there are at least j values that are at most j. This notion is often visualized as follows: there are n cars and n parking spaces numbered from 1 to n; the i-th car has a preferred parking space pi. The cars enter the parking lot in order, and the i-th car goes directly to the space in position pi. If it finds it free then it parks there, and otherwise it continues driving until the next available space. A parking function corresponds to a list of preferred spaces that allows all the cars to park. 13

As recapitulated in Section 3.3, the classical notion of parking functions refers to the set of words PF n ⊆ [n]n for which all cars successfully park. Among the several generalizations of this notion proposed in the literature, one particularly useful for our endeavors is the one studied by Kenyon and Yin in [KY23]. We say that a word 

w ∈ [m]n is a generalized parking function if all n cars successfully park in the m available spaces. The set of generalized parking functions is denoted by PF( n, m ). Kenyon and Yin [KY23] provide the following characterization that allows one to determine whether a word belongs to PF( n, m ). 

Proposition 4.1 ([KY23, Prop. 2.1]) Let w ∈ [m]n be a word whose non-decreasing rearrange-ment is b1 ≤ b2 ≤ · · · ≤ bn. Then w ∈ PF( n, m ) if and only if 

bj ≤ m − n + j for all j = 1 , . . . , n. 

The above proposition provides a useful criterion for determining whether a preference word 

w ∈ [m]n allows all n cars to park successfully. 

Remark 4.2 By Proposition 4.1 and Definition 2.6 of y-parking functions there is a connection between generalized parking functions and y-parking functions. Specifically, by setting y =(m − n + 1 , 1, 1, . . . , 1), we obtain the identity PF( y) = PF( n, m ). From this point on, whenever the interpretation of the parking-protocol is used, we adopt the convention that n denotes the number of cars and m the number of parking spaces.

5.1. Other parking protocols for y-parking functions. In [FHMS25], the authors study another parking protocol for y-parking functions that exhibits some similarities to the one treated in the present paper. We know that y-parking functions are equivalent to u-parking functions under the identification ui = y1 + · · · + yi.Given y = ( y1, . . . , y n) ∈ Zn 

> ≥0

with m = y1 + · · · + yn, and the word w ∈ [m]n, they interpret 

w = ( w1, . . . , w n) as the parking preferences of n cars that try to park in m available spaces, each space j ∈ [m] with capacity mj (u) the multiplicity of j in u. A car can park at a space if there is still capacity. Each car i ∈ [n] tries to park in its preferred space wi. If that space wi is already occupied by mwi (u) cars, the car moves to the next available space wi + 1. If no available space is found after reaching the end, the car is considered to have failed to park. In this setting, the words w

that lead to all cars parking successfully are exactly the y-parking functions in PF n(y). In contrast, our parking protocol for y-parking functions considers m = ∑ni=1 yi linearly ordered spaces with unit capacity. While every y-parking function parks successfully under our protocol (see Proposition 4.3), the converse does not hold in general: there exist preference sequences that park successfully but are not y-parking functions. For example, if y = (1 , 2), the word w = (2 , 3) parks successfully in m = 3 spaces, but it is not a y-parking function. Note that both protocols agree in the classical case when y = (1 , . . . , 1). As a consequence, the notion of “lucky cars” depends on the chosen protocol and is not pre-served under this correspondence. In particular, the associated statistics are not equidistributed as the next example shows.
