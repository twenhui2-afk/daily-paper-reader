ON SUBGROUPS OF BRIN-THOMPSON GROUPS nV                

> SADAYOSHI KOJIMA AND XIAOBING SHENG
> Abstract. We prove that the Brin-Thompson group nV is torsion locally finite for n≥1 which is known only when n= 1, and nV contains elements of infinite order admitting roots with arbitrary large order for n≥2 which is known to not be true for the n= 1 case.

1. Introduction 

Thompson’s groups F , T and V were first introduced by Richard J. Thompson in the 1960s and their unusual properties have made them a good source of counterex-amples to many conjectures in group theory. Various generalisations of these groups have been studied in more recent years, including the higher dimensional Thompson groups (known as the Brin-Thompson groups) defined by Brin [2] in 2004. Roughly speaking, the Brin-Thompson group, denoted by nV, comprises self-homeomorphisms of the product Cn of n copies of the Cantor set C which send dyadic block decompo-sitions of Cn to other ones piecewise affinely. This paper is motivated by Burillo, Cleary and R¨ over’s survey [4] on the subgroups of Thompson’s group V . Among others, they presented two results on V , one is torsion locally finiteness proved by R¨ over in [10, 11], and the other is the non-existence of roots of arbitrary large order, also previously shown by Higman in [8]. We study these properties for the higher dimensional version nV from a more combinatorial perspective. Recall that a group is torsion locally finite if all of its finitely generated torsion subgroups are finite. The property of torsion groups being finite seems to be generic as Burnside proposed as a problem in 1902 and was proved to be false. While this slightly different version of the property, torsion locally finite, holds for many groups including hyperbolic groups and some small cancellation groups. However, the Grigorchuk group appeared in [7] does not have this property for example. We first prove that nV is not exceptional, namely, 

Theorem 1.1. nV is torsion locally finite for n ≥ 1.

Secondly, we show in contrast to Higman’s result mentioned above that 

Theorem 1.2. nV contains a subgroup isomorphic to an additive group of the dyadic rationals if n ≥ 2, and in particular, it contains elements of infinite order having roots with arbitrarily large order.

We first recall the definition of roots, 

Definition 4.1. For a group G, an element h ∈ G is said to be a root of g ∈ G if there is k ≥ 2 such that hk = g, and the smallest such k is called the order of the root h.

Remark 4.1 . The set of orders of roots of elements of infinite order cannot be bounded since if g is of infinite order, then gk has an obvious root g of order k and k could be arbitrary large. Thus, what is interesting is to find a maximal order of the roots of a particular element. 5. Future Topics 

Brin-Thompson groups are already interesting by themselves and have a few gen-eralisations [1]. However, we want to further study the generalisations of these groups without torsion local finiteness [12] and their coarse geometric interpretations. On the other hand, Brin questioned in [2, Remark 4.9] that “There seems to be no group 2F ” while 2 T seems to exist and coincide with 2 V . Hence we would like to further study the possible higher dimensional analogue for F and T , especially when the dimension is 2.
