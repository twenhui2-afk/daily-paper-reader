Abstract 

Graph transformers achieve strong results on molecular and long-range reasoning tasks, yet remain hampered by over-smoothing (the progressive collapse of node representations with depth) and attention entropy degeneration. We observe that these pathologies share a root cause with attention sinks in large language models: softmax attention’s sum-to-one constraint forces every node to attend somewhere, even when no informative signal exists. Motivated by recent findings that element-wise sigmoid gating eliminates atten-tion sinks in large language models, we propose SigGate-GT , a graph transformer that applies learned, per-head sigmoid gates to the attention output within the GraphGPS framework. Each gate can suppress activations toward zero, enabling heads to selec-tively silence uninformative connections. On five standard benchmarks, SigGate-GT 

matches the prior best on ZINC ( 0.059 MAE) and sets new state-of-the-art on ogbg-molhiv ( 82 .47% ROC-AUC), with statistically significant gains over GraphGPS across all five datasets ( p < 0.05 ). Ablations show that gating reduces over-smoothing by 30% 

(mean relative MAD gain across 4–16 layers), increases attention entropy, and stabilizes training across a 10 × learning rate range, with about 1% parameter overhead on OGB. 

Keywords: Graph Transformers, Gated Attention, Over-Smoothing, Molecular Prop-erty Prediction, Deep Learning. 

1. Introduction 

Graph-structured data is ubiquitous in scientific domains, particularly molecular prop-erty prediction [1]. While message passing neural networks (MPNNs) [1–3] have been the dominant paradigm, their expressivity is fundamentally limited by the Weisfeiler-Leman hierarchy [3] and their ability to capture long-range dependencies degrades with graph di-ameter [4]. Graph transformers address these limitations by computing global self-attention over all nodes [5, 6], enabling direct information flow between distant nodes and achieving strong results on molecular benchmarks [7, 8]. Despite their promise, graph transformers suffer from three persistent pathologies. First, 

over-smoothing , the convergence of node representations to indistinguishable vectors as depth increases, affects graph transformers just as it does MPNNs, since repeated global attention acts as Laplacian smoothing [9–12]. Second, attention entropy collapse , where attention distributions degenerate toward uniform or peaked configurations, destabilizes training [13]. Third, training instability in deep architectures remains a practical barrier, compounded by the fact that softmax attention forces all weights to be non-negative and sum to one. A remarkably simple solution was recently discovered in language modeling. Qiu et al. [14] demonstrated at NeurIPS 2025 that adding a head-specific sigmoid gate after SDPA in LLMs eliminates attention sinks [15], improves stability, and enhances extrapolation. Their evaluation across 30 variants identified element-wise sigmoid gating at the attention output as optimal. Related approaches such as GLU variants [16] further established gating as a beneficial transformer inductive bias. Gating in graph neural networks takes several forms, notably edge-level sigmoid gating in GatedGCN [17] and node-level GRU gating in GGNN [18]. However, none of these existing approaches operate as element-wise sigmoid gates on the attention output in graph transformers. This gap is significant because graph transformers face the same structural

constraints that sigmoid gating addresses in LLMs: softmax attention produces sum-to-one weights that cannot represent “no attention,” contributing to over-smoothing, and the value-output projection creates a low-rank bottleneck limiting head expressivity. We propose SigGate-GT , a graph transformer that integrates element-wise sigmoid gat-ing into the attention mechanism of GraphGPS [7]. After computing the standard softmax attention output, each head applies a learned, input-dependent sigmoid gate controlling in-formation flow (Figure 1). This enables three key properties: (1) heads can output near-zero activations, suppressing uninformative connections and mitigating over-smoothing; (2) the element-wise sigmoid gate breaks the rank bound imposed by the softmax-attention oper-ator, raising the effective (stable) rank of per-head outputs, as we verify analytically and empirically in Appendix A; and (3) the gate stabilizes gradient flow during training. Our contributions are: 

• We introduce SigGate-GT , the first graph transformer to incorporate element-wise sigmoid gating on the attention output, transferring a proven LLM innovation [14] to graphs and addressing over-smoothing, entropy collapse, and training instability. 

• We provide extensive ablation studies with statistical significance testing, analyzing gate placement, per-head vs. shared gating, over-smoothing depth, attention entropy, and training dynamics with quantitative evidence. 

• We achieve state-of-the-art on ogbg-molhiv, match the prior best on ZINC, and obtain competitive results on LRGB and ogbg-molpcba, with small parameter overhead. 2. Related Work  

> 2.1. Graph Transformers

The application of transformers to graphs has progressed rapidly, from Graphormer’s [6] centrality and shortest-path biases and SAN’s [19] spectral attention to the GraphGPS framework [7], which unified positional encoding, local message passing, and global atten-tion into a modular architecture. Key subsequent developments include GRIT [8], which removes message passing entirely in favor of learned relative positional encodings, and Ex-phormer [20], which uses expander graphs for sparse attention. Cai et al. [21] formally connected MPNNs and graph transformers, showing that standard MPNNs can be viewed as a special case of graph transformers with restricted attention patterns. Despite these advances, no graph transformer incorporates element-wise sigmoid gating on the attention output, a mechanism that, as we demonstrate, addresses fundamental limitations of the softmax attention operator in the graph domain.  

> 2.2. Over-Smoothing, Over-Squashing, and Attention Pathologies

Over-smoothing was first characterized by Li et al. [9] as Laplacian smoothing, with Oono and Suzuki [10] proving exponential convergence; Rusch et al. [12] provide a comprehensive survey. Mitigations include DropEdge [22], PairNorm [23], and DeeperGCN [24]. The related bottleneck of over-squashing (exponential information loss between distant nodes) is analyzed by Alon and Yahav [4]. Zhai et al. [13] showed attention entropy can collapse exponentially, while Choi et al. [11] demonstrated that softmax attention induces over-smoothing in transformers broadly.  

> 2.3. Gating Mechanisms in Graph Neural Networks

Gating in GNNs operates at different levels than our proposal: GatedGCN [17] applies edge-level sigmoid gating to control message flow along individual edges, while GGNN [18] uses GRU node-level gates to regulate how much of the incoming message updates each node’s hidden state. These mechanisms modulate local information flow within the mes-sage passing framework. In contrast, SigGate-GT ’s element-wise attention output gating operates on the global attention pathway, modulating the aggregated representation after all-pairs attention has been computed. This distinction is important: local edge gating cannot address the fundamental sum-to-one constraint of softmax attention over the full graph, whereas output gating directly controls the magnitude and dimensionality of the global attention contribution to each node’s updated representation.  

> 2.4. Sigmoid Attention and Alternative Attention Functions

A closely related line of work replaces softmax with sigmoid entirely. Ramapuram et al. [25] provide theory for sigmoid self-attention, producing non-normalized weights. Our approach is fundamentally different: SigGate-GT retains softmax attention and applies sigmoid as a post-hoc modulation gate on the output. This preserves the probabilistic inter-pretation (attention weights remain a distribution over nodes) while adding per-dimension output suppression. Sigmoid attention abandons normalization entirely, risking unbounded activations; sigmoid gating on the output is inherently bounded and acts as a smooth “vol-ume control.” 3. Method

3.3. Why Sigmoid Gating Benefits Graph Transformers 

The sigmoid gate addresses three fundamental limitations of standard graph transformer attention: 

Breaking the sum-to-one and constant-magnitude constraints. Softmax atten-tion produces non-negative, sum-to-one weights, meaning every node must attend to all other nodes with positive weight. In graphs where most node pairs lack meaningful relation-ships, this forces heads to spread probability mass over irrelevant connections, a structural analog of attention sinks in LLMs [15]. The sigmoid gate allows gk → 0, enabling heads to effectively output zero for uninformative patterns. While the gate itself produces values in 

[0 , 1] (which are non-negative), the critical point is that it breaks the constraint that the output magnitude is fully determined by the value vectors. Instead, the gate provides an independent “volume control” per dimension. 

Increasing per-head expressivity via a higher effective rank. The standard at-tention output Y = softmax (QK ⊤/√dk)V is row-stochastic-times-V, so each row of Y

is a convex combination of rows of V [14]; the stable rank of Y is bounded above by 

min( srank (A), srank (V)) and is further compressed because softmax concentrates proba-bility mass. Element-wise sigmoid gating breaks this bound: Y⊙g applies a row- and column-specific multiplicative pattern that cannot be represented as a row-stochastic reweighting of 

V. In the calibrated synthetic study of Appendix A, the mean stable rank rises from 3.18 

to 3.41 (7.2% relative gain; n = 64 , dk = 32 , 5 seeds), robustly across attention-concentration and graph-sparsity regimes. This additional rank capacity is valuable for graph-level tasks that require discrimination of fine-grained structural differences [3]. 

Stabilizing gradient flow. The sigmoid gate provides a smooth, bounded [0 , 1] multi-plicative factor, acting as a gradient modulator. During backpropagation, the gate’s deriv-ative σ′(z) = σ(z)(1 − σ(z)) prevents both vanishing and exploding gradients through the attention pathway, complementing layer normalization [26]. 

Interaction with local MPNN gating. GraphGPS uses GatedGCN [17] with sigmoid edge gates for local message passing. SigGate operates at a different level by gating the 

global attention output rather than local edges. These mechanisms are complementary: GatedGCN controls which edges carry local information, while SigGate controls which global attention outputs propagate.

<3% (measured on a single A100), as the gate computation is a single matrix multiply, sigmoid, and Hadamard product per head. 4. Experiments  

> 4.1. Datasets and Evaluation Protocol

We evaluate on five standard graph-level benchmarks: ZINC [27]: 12K molecular graphs for solubility regression (MAE ↓), 500K parameter budget. ogbg-molhiv [28]: 41K molecules for HIV inhibition (AUC ↑), scaffold split. ogbg-molpcba [28]: 438K molecules across 128 bioassays (AP ↑). Peptides-func [29]: 15.5K peptides for functional annotation (AP ↑). Peptides-struct [29]: Peptide structural property regression (MAE ↓).  

> 4.2. Implementation Details

We implement SigGate-GT using PyTorch Geometric (v2.4), building on the official GraphGPS codebase. The local MPNN uses GatedGCN [17] and we use LapPE (dim 16, SignNet [30]) combined with RWSE (16 steps) [7, 31]. ZINC : 10 layers, dim 64, 8 heads, 2000 epochs, AdamW [32] (lr= 10 −3, wd= 10 −5), cosine annealing, batch 32. OGB : 5 layers, dim 256, 8 heads, 100 epochs, lr= 10 −4, batch 256/512. LRGB : 10 layers, dim 128, 8 heads, 200 epochs, lr= 5×10 −4, batch 64. Gate bias initialized to 0.5. All experiments on a single A100, 5 seeds (0–4). We report mean ± std and paired t-test p-values against the GraphGPS baseline. Full hyperparameter grids and wall-clock/memory measurements are provided in Appendix B. Code will be released upon acceptance.

4.4. Ablation Studies 

4.4.1. Gate Placement and Sharing Strategy 

Table 5 evaluates gate placement and sharing. For placement, we test four positions following Qiu et al. [14]: G1 (output gating, our default), G2 (value gating), G3 (pre-softmax logit gating, i.e., softmax (σ(·) ⊙ QK ⊤/√dk)V), and no gating. G1 consistently outperforms alternatives, confirming that the optimal placement from LLMs transfers to graphs. G3 degrades performance because it disrupts the attention probability distribution. For sharing strategy, per-head gating (each head has its own Wgk) outperforms shared gating (single projection for all heads), confirming that head-specific control enables functional specialization.

4.4.4. Training Stability 

We quantitatively assess training stability by measuring final performance across a range of learning rates on ZINC (Table 8 and Figure 2). GraphGPS achieves its best result at lr= 10 −3 but degrades sharply at higher rates (MAE increases from 0.070 to 0.112 at lr= 3×10 −3). SigGate-GT maintains strong performance (MAE ≤ 0.065 ) across the entire range. We quantify this as the performance range (max MAE − min MAE across learning rates): GraphGPS has a range of 0.042, while SigGate-GT has a range of only 0.006, a 7× reduction in sensitivity to the learning rate. The sigmoid gate acts as an implicit gradi-ent regularizer: large attention outputs are multiplicatively dampened, preventing sudden gradient spikes.

Discussion Why graphs amplify the attention sink problem. In language models, attention sinks arise because positional structure creates natural anchor tokens that absorb excess probability mass [15]. Graphs lack this structure entirely: all n nodes are a priori equivalent attention targets, so softmax must spread mass across O(n2) pairs, most of which carry no meaningful signal. The sigmoid gate provides a learned “soft null”: heads can suppress their output when no informative pattern exists, rather than being forced to attend somewhere. This explains why improvements are largest on small molecular graphs (ZINC, ogbg-molhiv), where the proportion of uninformative pairs is high relative to graph size yet softmax still assigns them positive weight. 

Sigmoid gating as a per-dimension smoothing bypass. Over-smoothing has a spectral interpretation: repeated attention acts as adaptive Laplacian smoothing, driving representations toward the leading eigenvector [9, 10]. The sum-to-one constraint ensures that every attention layer performs some averaging, even when it is harmful. In the gated output (AkVk) ⊙ σ(HW gk + bgk), dimensions where σ(·) ≈ 0 effectively bypass the smooth-ing step entirely, preserving information in the residual stream. This creates a learned, per-dimension, per-head choice between “smooth” and “skip,” which is a mechanism funda-mentally unavailable to standard attention. The MAD retention at depth (73% vs. 51% at 16 layers; Table 6) quantifies this bypass effect. 

Gate bias initialization. We initialize bgk = 0 .5 (gate ≈ 0.62 ) across all benchmarks. Preliminary experiments suggest lower initialization ( bgk = 0 , gate ≈ 0.5) may benefit very deep models ( >16 layers) where more aggressive early suppression helps prevent represen-tation collapse. 6. Conclusion 

We presented SigGate-GT , a graph transformer that integrates element-wise sigmoid gating into the attention mechanism, transferring the NeurIPS 2025 Best Paper innovation from LLMs to graph-structured data. Through experiments on five standard benchmarks with statistical significance testing, we demonstrated that this simple modification matches the prior best on ZINC, achieves state-of-the-art on ogbg-molhiv, and obtains competitive results on LRGB and ogbg-molpcba. Ablation studies with quantitative evidence establish that output gating (G1) with per-head strategy is optimal, and that sigmoid gating reduces over-smoothing, increases attention entropy, and stabilizes training across learning rates. The small parameter overhead and consistent improvements suggest that sigmoid gating should be considered a standard component in future graph transformer designs. 

Limitations. First, every reported result uses the GraphGPS backbone. Because our mechanism modifies only the per-head post-SDPA computation and does not interact with positional encodings or the MPNN branch, it ports unchanged to any softmax-attention graph transformer (e.g. Graphormer [6], Exphormer [20]); however, “ports unchanged” is not “is empirically beneficial,” so we regard the single-backbone scope as a genuine re-striction rather than evidence of cross-backbone generalization. Appendix D discusses the considerations for porting SigGate-GT to Exphormer and Graphormer, and we commit to a cross-backbone evaluation in follow-on work. Second, the depth analysis (Table 6) trains separate models per depth rather than probing intermediate layers, conflating archi-tectural and optimization effects. Third, all benchmarks are graph-level molecular/peptide tasks; node-level and link-prediction settings, which lack graph-level pooling, may exhibit different dynamics. 

Future work. We plan to extend SigGate to node-level and link-prediction tasks, in-vestigate interaction with emerging positional encodings, explore theoretical connections to the WL hierarchy [3, 21], and evaluate on heterogeneous and temporal graphs.

> A.2. Rank Bound for Softmax Attention

Let A ∈ Rn×n be a row-stochastic matrix (rows non-negative, sum to 1), and let V ∈

Rn×dk . Then Y = AV satisfies srank (Y) ≤ min  srank (A), srank (V). (A.2) Furthermore, each row of Y is a convex combination of rows of V, so the rows of Y lie in the convex hull of rows of V. Softmax with sharp logits produces A with low stable rank (probability mass concentrates on a few columns), tightening (A.2) and shrinking effective rank further. This is a restatement of [14]’s low-rank-bottleneck observation in the graph-transformer setting.  

> A.3. Element-Wise Sigmoid Gating Breaks the Bound

Consider the gated output eY = Y ⊙ g with gij ∈ (0 , 1) input-dependent. The map 

Y 7 → eY is not a row-stochastic reweighting of V: different rows of eY use different per-column scalings, so in general eY cannot be written as A′V for any row-stochastic A′.Consequently, the bound (A.2) does not apply to eY: its stable rank can (and typically does) exceed srank (Y).

Appendix D. Porting SigGate to Other Backbones What the mechanism requires. SigGate modifies only the per-head computation between the SDPA output and the output projection WO (Eq. 3.3). It does not require access to positional encodings, edge features, the MPNN branch, or any backbone-specific inductive bias. Any architecture whose global-attention module produces an output of the form softmax (·)V is, in principle, a valid host. 

Exphormer [20]. Exphormer uses sparse attention over a union of expander graphs, neighbourhoods, and virtual nodes, but still applies softmax within each head on the selected pairs. The gating equation head gated  

> k

= ( AkVk) ⊙ σ(HW gk + bgk) applies unchanged, with 

Ak simply being sparser. We expect the relative rank gain to be modestly larger than in dense softmax attention because a row-sparse A is closer to deterministic selection, which tightens bound (A.2). 

Graphormer [6]. Graphormer adds centrality and spatial biases inside the softmax (logits become QK ⊤/√dk +bcentr +bspatial ) but retains the row-stochastic output structure. SigGate again applies unchanged. The spatial bias tends to make A more peaked (attention concentrates on graph-distance-1 neighbours), further tightening the rank bound, which the gate can compensate for. 

What we expect to change. (i) Optimal gate-bias initialization may differ if the host architecture uses a different WO scale. (ii) The absolute MAE/AUC improvement depends on how much headroom exists relative to the backbone’s existing regularization. (iii) On Graphormer’s shortest-path-distance features, there is a risk that the gate partially replicates the function of the spatial bias, reducing marginal gain. A full cross-backbone evaluation is reserved for follow-on work, as discussed in the main paper’s Limitations.
