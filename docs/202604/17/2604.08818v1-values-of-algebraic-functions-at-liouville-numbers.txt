# Values of algebraic functions at Liouville numbers 

## Yuri Bilu, Diego Marques April 13, 2026 

Abstract 

In 1953 LeVeque proved the existence of Um-numbers by showing that for some specially defined Liouville number λ, the mth root λ1/m is in Um.In this article we study the following question: let u be an algebraic function of degree m and λ a Liouville number; under which conditions 

u(λ) is a Um-number? We consider a more refined notion of L-numbers, and show that, under very general assumptions, an algebraic function of degree m takes Um-values at all L-numbers.

# 1 Introduction 

In this article m denotes a positive integer. LeVeque [14] introduced Um as the set of the complex transcendental numbers that can be well approximated by algebraic numbers of degree m but not by algebraic numbers of smaller degree. Precisely, a complex transcendental number λ belongs to Um if m is the smallest integer with the following property: there exists an infinite sequence of complex algebraic numbers ( βn)n≥0 of degree m and an infinite sequence of positive real numbers ( wn)n≥0 such that h( βn) → ∞ , wn → ∞ , |λ − βn| ≤ e−wn h( βn) (n ≥ 0) . (1.1) Here h( ·) denotes the usual absolute logarithmic height. (LeVeque uses a differ-ent, but equivalent language, see [2, Remark 2.6].) The elements of the set Um

are called Um-numbers . In particular, the Liouville numbers are exactly the 

U1-numbers. LeVeque [14, Theorem 5] shows that Um-numbers exist for every m, by showing that for some specially defined Liouville number λ, the mth root λ1/m 

is in Um; see also Bugeaud [5, Theorem 7.4]. In this article we study the following question: given an algebraic function u

of degree m and a Liouville number λ, under which conditions we can expect 

u(λ) to be a Um-number? Here, by an algebraic function of degree m we mean a holomorphic func-tion u, which is defined on a non-empty open subset 1 of C and is algebraic of degree m over the field of rational functions Q(z); that is, there exists a polyno-mial P (X, Y ) ∈ Q[X, Y ], irreducible in the ring Q[X, Y ], such that deg Y P = m

and P (z, u (z)) = 0 for all z in the domain of definition of u. This polynomial will be called the minimal polynomial of u; it is well-defined up to a non-zero constant factor. Of course, one cannot expect u(λ) to be a Um-number for any u of degree m

and any Liouville number λ. For instance, if λ = κ2, where κ is another Liouville number, then λ1/2 is not in U2.Moreover, assume that u is a real-valued algebraic function of degree m > 1; that is, there is a non-empty interval I ⊆ R, where u is defined and takes real values. In this case, which is the most interesting in our context, u cannot take 

Um-values at all Liouville numbers, by the following general principle: if f is a non-constant continuous function defined on a sub-interval of R, then there exists a Liouville number λ such that f (λ) is a Liouville number as well 2.

## 1.1 L-numbers 

Thus, some conditions have to be imposed. Let L be the set of real numbers λ

with the following property: there exist a sequence ( αn)n≥0 of rational numbers and a sequence ( vn)n≥0 of positive real numbers such that h( αn) → ∞ , vn → ∞ , |λ − αn| ≤ e−vn h( αn) (n ≥ 0) , (1.2) and, moreover, there exists A > 0 such that h( αn+1 ) ≤ Av n h( αn) (n ≥ 0) . (1.3) Condition (1.3) can be informally expressed as “good rational approximations for λ are not too sparse”. The elements of the set L will be called L-numbers. Clearly, L-numbers are Liouville numbers, but the converse is not true: using continued fractions, it is easy to give an example of a Liouville number which is not in L.

L-numbers as well, which means that L-numbers is a much wider class than strong Liouville numbers. Many known results about strong Liouville numbers extend, without much effort, to L-numbers. This concerns, in particular, Theorems 1 and 2 of Al-nia¸ cik [1], see also [6, 7]. On the contrary, if one tries to relax the definition of L-numbers, many results no longer remain true [7]. This implies that L-numbers is a reasonable subset of Liouville numbers to study: it is sufficiently rich to contain interesting examples, and sufficiently refined to have many desired properties.

The conditions on u imposed in this theorem are “generic”, in the sense that a “randomly selected” algebraic function of degree m is expected to satisfy them. This vague statement can be made mathematically precise in many ways, but we prefer not to go into it here. As noted above, one needs to replace the set of Liouville numbers by a smaller subset (like L-numbers) if one works with a real-valued algebraic function. The situation changes if we consider algebraic functions taking non-real values on R.For instance, it is obvious that the algebraic function u(z) := z + i of degree 2 takes U2-values at all Liouville numbers, not just at L-numbers. Let γ be an algebraic number of degree m and let M be the Galois closure of Q(γ) over Q. Theorem 1.3. Let γ be a non-real complex algebraic number of degree m and of Galois group Sm. Then for any Liuoville number λ we have λ + γ ∈ Um.

It would be interesting to discover more general classes of (non-real-valued) algebraic functions of degree m with Um-values at all Liouville numbers.

We denote by h( ·) the usual absolute logarithmic height on the field of alge-braic numbers ¯Q, and call it simply height . We will use (often without special reference) the standard properties of the height, like h( α + β) ≤ h( α) + h( β) + log 2 , h( αβ ) ≤ h( α) + h( β) (α, β ∈ ¯Q)and so on, as given, for instance, in [2, Section 2]. In particular, we will use Liouville’s inequality as follows. 

Proposition 2.1 (Liouville’s inequality) . Let β and β′ be distinct complex al-gebraic numbers of degrees d and d′, respectively. Then 

|β − β′| ≥ e−dd ′(h( β)+h( β′)+log 2) .

The proof can be found, for instance, in [4, Theorem 1.5.21]. We also need to compare the heights of two algebraic numbers satisfying an algebraic relation. 

Proposition 2.2. Let P (X, Y ) ∈ ¯Q[X, Y ] be a polynomial with algebraic co-efficients, and let α, β ∈ ¯Q be such that P (α, β ) = 0 . Assume that P (X, Y ) is not divisible by the polynomial X − α (that is, the polynomial P (α, Y ) is not identically 0). Then 

h( β) ≤ N h( α) + O(1) , (2.1) 

where N := deg X P and the implied constant depends only on the polynomial P .

The proof can be found, for instance, in [3, Proposition 5.2] 4. Assuming the polynomial P (X, Y ) irreducible, much sharper results are known; see, for instance, [11]. However, simple estimate (2.1) is sufficient for our purposes.

# 4 Hilbert-M¨ uller irreducibility theorem over Q

We thank them most warmly for their generous help. Let P (X, Y ) ∈ Q[X, Y ] be a Q-irreducible polynomial. Classical Hilbert’s ir-reducibility theorem asserts that, for infinitely many ξ ∈ Z, the specialized poly-nomial P (ξ, Y ) ∈ Q[Y ] is Q-irreducible. However, there can still be infinitely many ξ ∈ Z for which P (ξ, Y ) is Q-reducible, as the example P (X, Y ) := Y 2 − X

shows. M¨ uller [16] showed that, imposing on P extra conditions, one would obtain a much stronger statement: P (ξ, Y ) is Q-irreducible for all ξ ∈ Z with finitely many exceptions. Let us introduce some notation. We set m := deg Y P , and we denote by 

G = Gal Y (P ) the Galois group of P (x, Y ) over the field Q(x) of rational func-tions, realized as a subgroup of the symmetric group Sm. Finally, we denote by g the genus of the algebraic curve P (X, Y ) = 0. The following is Theorem 1.2 from [16]. 

Theorem 4.1 (M¨ uller) . Assume that G = Sm or that m is a prime number. Assume also that g ≥ 1. Then P (ξ, Y ) is Q-irreducible for all but finitely many 

ξ ∈ Z.
