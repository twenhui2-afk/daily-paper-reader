# Optimal multiple testing under family-wise error control: elementary symmetric polynomials and a scalable algorithm 

Prasanjit Dubey ∗ Xiaoming Huo H. Milton Stewart School of Industrial and Systems Engineering, Georgia Institute of Technology, Atlanta, GA 30332, U.S.A. 

Abstract 

Simultaneously testing 𝐾 hypotheses while controlling the family-wise error rate is a fundamental problem in statistics. Existing procedures (Bonferroni, Holm, Hochberg, Hommel) provide valid control but sacrifice power, increasingly so as 𝐾 grows, because they base decisions on marginal 𝑝 -value ranks rather than the joint likelihood. Rosset et al. (2022) formulated the most powerful family-wise-error-rate-controlling test as a dual program and proved the existence of an optimal dual vector 𝜇 ∗, but left its computation as an open problem. We solve this problem for 𝐾 exchangeable hypotheses. The key insight is that the family-wise error rate constraint coefficients 𝑏 𝑙,𝑘 ( ® 𝑢 ) admit closed-form expressions through elementary symmetric polynomials of the likelihood-ratio values 𝑔 (𝑢 1), . . . , 𝑔 (𝑢 𝐾 ). This al-gebraic structure implies a global monotonicity theorem: the target functions 𝐹 𝛾 (𝜇 ) = FWER 𝛾 ( ® 𝐷 𝜇 )

are simultaneously non-increasing in every component of 𝜇 , for arbitrary 𝐾 , which guarantees unique coordinate-wise roots and enables a bisection-based coordinate-descent algorithm with 𝑂 (log 𝜀 −1) con-vergence rate. The relative power gain over Hommel’s method grows from 15% at 𝐾 =3 to 84% at 𝐾 =12. Applications to replication studies, a clinical trial, and a replicability assessment illustrate both the power gains and the role of the exchangeability assumption. 

Keywords: Coordinate descent; Elementary symmetric polynomial; Family-wise error rate; Most powerful test; Multiple hypothesis testing; Strong control. 

1 Introduction 

Multiple-hypothesis testing with family-wise error rate control remains essential when even a single false rejection carries high cost: in clinical trials with multiple endpoints [Pocock et al., 1987, Bretz et al., 2009, Vickerstaff et al., 2019], in economic policy evaluation [Romano and Wolf, 2005, List et al., 2016, Viviano et al., 2025], and in large-scale experimentation [Johari et al., 2022]. Given 𝐾 hypotheses 𝐻 1, . . . , 𝐻 𝐾 and a significance level 𝛼 , the goal is to find the testing policy ®𝐷 = (𝐷 1, . . . , 𝐷 𝐾 ) ∈ { 0, 1}𝐾 that maximises power while ensuring pr (𝑉 > 0) ≤ 𝛼 for every configuration of true and false nulls, where 𝑉 counts the false rejections.

5.2 SPRINT blood pressure trial 

The Systolic Blood Pressure Intervention Trial [SPRINT, SPRINT Research Group, 2015] randomised 9 ,361 participants to intensive ( < 120 mmHg) versus standard ( < 140 mmHg) blood pressure targets. We apply Algorithm 1 with 𝐾 = 5 to the five individual cardiovascular endpoints (excluding the primary composite, which overlaps with the individual components). 

Study design. The five endpoints and their reported 𝑝 -values are: myocardial infarction ( 𝑝 = 0·19), non-MI acute coronary syndrome ( 𝑝 = 0·99), stroke ( 𝑝 = 0·50), heart failure ( 𝑝 = 0·002), and cardiovascular death (𝑝 = 0·005). We specify 𝑔 (𝑢 ) as a truncated normal with 𝜃 = −2·0, corresponding to the overall treatment effect (hazard ratio 0 ·75). This result illustrates an important limitation. The exchangeability assumption posits a common al-ternative density: the same treatment effect for all endpoints. But the SPRINT data strongly violate this: the hazard ratios are 0 ·83 (MI), 1 ·00 (ACS), 0 ·89 (stroke), 0 ·62 (heart failure), and 0 ·57 (CV death). The endpoints are not drawn from a common alternative; some are clearly null. In this heterogeneous setting, the algorithm’s joint likelihood computation is penalised by the null-like endpoints (ACS at 𝑝 = 0·99, stroke at 

𝑝 = 0·50), which pull the cumulative sum Í𝑙 𝑖 =1 𝑅 𝑖 negative. The SPRINT example serves as a cautionary illustration: the optimal algorithm is most powerful when the exchangeability assumption holds, but can be less powerful than standard methods when the effects are heterogeneous. 

Hierarchical remedy. The hierarchical approach discussed in Section 6 addresses this. We partition the five endpoints into two groups based on prior clinical expectation: Group A (strong-effect: heart failure and CV death, 𝐾 𝐴 = 2) and Group B (uncertain-effect: MI, ACS, stroke, 𝐾 𝐵 = 3), applying Algorithm 1 within each group at Bonferroni-adjusted level 𝛼 /2 = 0·025. In Group A, the algorithm rejects both hypotheses (𝑙 ∗ = 2), recovering the rejections that standard methods find. In Group B, no method rejects any hypothesis (𝑙 ∗ = 0), which is the correct answer given the large 𝑝 -values. The hierarchical approach thus preserves the algorithm’s power within exchangeable groups while respecting heterogeneity across groups.

6.1 Summary of contributions 

We have shown that the family-wise error rate constraint coefficients in the dual formulation of Rosset et al. [2022] admit a closed-form expression through elementary symmetric polynomials (Lemma 3.1), and that this algebraic structure implies global monotonicity of all target functions (Theorem 3.7). The resulting coordinate-descent algorithm computes the optimal dual multipliers with linear convergence for arbitrary 𝐾 . The power gains are substantial and grow with 𝐾 : for a practitioner running a clinical trial with 

𝐾 =6 endpoints, adopting the optimal procedure over Hommel’s method yields a 38% power gain at zero additional cost in data collection. 

6.2 Limitations 

The main restriction is the exchangeability assumption (Assumption 2.1), which requires all hypotheses to have the same null distribution and the same alternative distribution. This is satisfied in many symmetric testing problems, such as subgroup analyses where subgroups are defined a priori with no reason to expect heterogeneous effects, but excludes settings where hypotheses have different effect sizes or different sample sizes. The contraction condition in Lemma 3.10 is assumed, not proved. While the empirical evidence (Section 4.4) strongly supports geometric convergence, an analytic proof would strengthen the theoretical contribution. A potential approach is to establish a diagonal dominance condition on the Jacobian of the update map 𝑇 : if |𝜕𝑇 𝛾 /𝜕𝜇 𝛾 | < 1 and the cross-partial derivatives are small relative to the diagonal, contraction follows from standard results in iterative methods. Finally, the Monte Carlo evaluation introduces sampling error in the FWER estimates. While common random numbers within each bisection ensure monotonicity of the objective, the final FWER guarantee is approximate. A conservative correction, replacing 𝛼 by 𝛼 − 𝑐 /√𝑁 for a suitable constant 𝑐 , can ensure that the empirical FWER remains below 𝛼 with high probability, at a small cost in power. 

6.3 Extensions 

Several directions for future work are natural. 

Relaxing exchangeability. A hierarchical approach could extend applicability: partition hypotheses into exchangeable groups (e.g., by endpoint type or biomarker class), apply the optimal policy within each group at a Bonferroni-adjusted significance level 𝛼 /𝐺 where 𝐺 is the number of groups, and aggregate the results. This preserves the theoretical guarantees within each group while accommodating heterogeneity across groups. For example, in a clinical trial with 𝐾 = 12 endpoints consisting of 𝐺 = 3 groups of 4 exchangeable endpoints (efficacy, safety, biomarker), one would apply Algorithm 1 with 𝐾 = 4 and 

𝛼 ′ = 0·05 /3 ≈ 0·017 within each group. The power loss from the Bonferroni correction across groups is partially offset by the power gain from the optimal policy within each group, particularly when 𝐾 /𝐺 

is moderate. More ambitiously, a non-exchangeable extension of the dual formulation would require 𝑏 𝑙,𝑘 

18 coefficients that depend on the specific identities of the null and alternative hypotheses, not just their counts; the elementary symmetric polynomial structure would not carry over directly, but the monotonicity principle might. 

Relationship to false discovery rate control. The family-wise error rate and the false discovery rate [Benjamini and Hochberg, 1995] control fundamentally different quantities: the former bounds pr (𝑉 > 0) ≤ 

𝛼 , tolerating zero false positives, while the latter bounds E {𝑉 /max (𝑅, 1)} ≤ 𝑞 where 𝑅 is the total number of rejections, tolerating a fraction of false positives proportional to the number of discoveries. The choice between them is problem-dependent and orthogonal to the optimality contribution of this paper: we make family-wise error rate control as powerful as possible for settings where it is the appropriate criterion, but do not argue that it should replace false discovery rate control where the latter is more suitable. Several considerations guide the choice. Family-wise error rate control is the standard when the cost of even a single false rejection is high, and the number of hypotheses is moderate: regulatory approval of drugs with multiple endpoints [Bretz et al., 2009], confirmatory replication studies [Camerer et al., 2018], and economic policy evaluation [List et al., 2016]. False discovery rate control is preferred when 𝐾 is large (hundreds to thousands), the goal is screening rather than confirmation, and individual false positives are tolerable: genome-wide association studies, gene expression profiling, and large-scale A/B testing. Within its domain, the optimal family-wise-error-rate-controlling test computed by Algorithm 1 has three advantages over false-discovery-rate procedures. First, it provides a stronger guarantee: controlling pr (𝑉 > 0) implies controlling E (𝑉 /𝑅 ), but not conversely. Second, it exploits the full joint likelihood 

Î𝐾 𝑘 =1 𝑔 (𝑢 𝑘 ) rather than only the 𝑝 -value ranks, which is the source of the 15–84% power gains documented in Section 4. Third, it is optimal within its class, in the sense that no other family-wise-error-rate-controlling test has higher power, whereas the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure, though optimal among step-up procedures, is not optimal among all false-discovery-rate-controlling tests. However, false discovery rate methods have complementary strengths. First, their power does not degrade with 𝐾 : the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure maintains roughly constant power as 𝐾 grows (because the threshold 𝑞 · 𝑘 /𝐾 adapts to the number of hypotheses), while even the optimal family-wise-error-rate procedure loses power (Table 1: Π12 = 0·615 versus Π3 = 0·635). For very large 𝐾 , the family-wise error rate constraint becomes so stringent that even the optimal test has negligible power. Second, false discovery rate methods require no distributional assumptions beyond independence or positive dependence of the 𝑝 -values, while our algorithm requires the exchangeability assumption (Assumption 2.1) and a specified alternative density 𝑔 (𝑢 ). The sensitivity analysis in Table 5 shows that the algorithm’s decisions can depend on the choice of 𝑔 , particularly for peaked densities. Third, computational cost differs by orders of magnitude: the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure requires 𝑂 (𝐾 log 𝐾 ) operations (sort and compare), while Algorithm 1 requires 𝑂 (𝐾 3 𝑁 log (𝑈 max /𝛿 )) per outer iteration with 𝑁 Monte Carlo samples. The linear structure of the power and error representations (4) suggests that analogous dual formulations could be developed for other error metrics, including 𝑘 -family-wise error rate [Romano and Wolf, 2007], false discovery rate, and weighted power objectives that prioritise certain hypotheses. Whether the elementary symmetric polynomial structure and the resulting monotonicity carry over to these settings is an open question. 19 Large 𝐾 . While our simulations extend to 𝐾 =12, the theory applies to arbitrary 𝐾 . For very large 𝐾 ,two computational challenges arise. First, the elementary symmetric polynomial computation may suffer numerical overflow or underflow when the 𝑔 -values span many orders of magnitude; log-domain arithmetic or the log-sum-exp trick can address this. Second, the 𝑂 (𝐾 3 𝑁 ) cost per outer iteration may become prohibitive; approximations based on saddlepoint methods or importance sampling could reduce the effective dimension.
