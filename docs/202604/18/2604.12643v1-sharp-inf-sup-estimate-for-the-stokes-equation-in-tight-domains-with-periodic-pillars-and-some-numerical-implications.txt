# Sharp inf-sup estimate for the Stokes equation in tight domains with periodic pillars and some numerical implications 

Qi Xin a,b , Shihua Gong a,b, ∗, Jinchao Xu c

Abstract 

The predictive simulation of fluid dynamics in densely packed microfluidic devices, such as Determinis-tic Lateral Displacement (DLD) arrays, is severely bottlenecked by the stagnation of standard iterative solvers. In this paper, we reveal that this failure is not an algorithmic artifact, but fundamentally rooted in the pre-asymptotic degradation of the pressure-velocity coupling stability. By rigorously an-alyzing periodic pillar geometries in this generalized lattice framework, we prove that the continuous Ladyzhenskaya-Babuška-Brezzi (LBB) condition, also called the inf-sup constant deteriorates exactly as 

m−1 up to a positive multiplicative constant, where m is the pillar density (the number of pillars per unit length). This causes a severe a priori error amplification and extreme ill-conditioning in Schur complement of the saddle point system. To overcome this theoretical limit, we propose a parameter-free, adaptively scaled Augmented Lagrangian (AL) stabilization strategy. Extensive numerical experiments on both standard square and highly asymmetric DLD arrays validate our theoretical bounds and demonstrate the robustness of the proposed AL method. 

Keywords: Stokes equation, inf-sup estimate, finite element methods, preconditioning 

1. Introduction 

Microfluidic devices featuring dense periodic pillar arrays have significantly advanced biomedical engi-neering, chemical processing, and precise particle manipulation. A prominent exemplar of this technology is the Deterministic Lateral Displacement (DLD) device [1, 2, 3, 4], which is widely employed in high-throughput cell sorting and biophysical fractionations. These micro-devices typically consist of thousands to millions of microscopic solid pillars strategically arranged in a shifted Bravais lattice to precisely control fluid streamlines and particle trajectories. While the fabrication of such devices has matured, the predictive computational modeling of the fluid dynamics and fluid-structure interactions (FSI) within these extremely dense arrays remains a formidable challenge. Computational mathematicians and engineers frequently encounter a bottleneck: as the pillar density increases (or the inter-pillar gap narrows), standard numerical solvers for the incompressible Stokes equations experience severe performance degradation, often leading to iteration stagnation [5]. The performance of standard iterative frameworks and classical block-preconditioning techniques (e.g., standard Schur complement approximations) degrades substantially in dense pillar arrays. The root cause of this vulnerability lies strictly within the fundamental mathematical structure of the saddle-point problem. Specifically, the convergence rate of these preconditioned iterative solvers is intrinsically dictated by the stability of the pressure-velocity coupling, quantified by the continuous and discrete inf-sup (LBB) constants [6, 7, 8]. At the same time, it is a well-established mathematical fact that the inf-sup constant depends on the underlying domain geometry, deteriorating significantly in elongated, highly anisotropic, or heavily perforated configurations [9, 10, 11]. Consequently, as the pillar packing density increases, the stability of the system inevitably collapses, rendering standard parameter tuning ineffective. The fundamental importance of the continuous inf-sup constant extends far beyond theoretical exis-tence proofs. In the context of densely packed microfluidic arrays, the magnitude of inf-sup constant β

• Stagnation of Iterative Solvers: At the linear algebra level, the condition number of the pressure Schur complement operator S is strictly proportional to β−2. Such extreme ill-conditioning intrin-sically dictates the convergence stagnation of classical segregated iterative methods (such as Uzawa or block preconditioner algorithms)[7, 15] and neutralizes the efficiency of standard block-diagonal preconditioners in Krylov subspace methods (e.g., GMRES). While the degradation of the continuous inf-sup constant in slender domains is well-documented in the literature [9, 10, 11], its precise pre-asymptotic estimate within the complex, multiply-connected topologies of dense pillar arrays lacks explicit quantitative characterization. Classical homogenization theory [16, 17] studies the asymptotic limit as the characteristic cell size ϵ → 0 (or equivalently, the pillar density m → ∞ ), effectively "smearing out" the discrete solids to yield a continuous macroscopic Darcy’s law. However, practical CFD simulations of DLD devices operate in the pre-asymptotic regime: the pillars are discrete and finite, and the full Stokes equations must be solved explicitly to resolve local shear stresses for precise particle tracking. To bridge this critical gap, this paper establishes the sharp pre-asymptotic degradation rate of the continuous LBB constant β in these generalized periodic pillar geometries. By rigorously constructing global linear pressure fields and leveraging local restriction operators, we mathematically prove that the inf-sup constant degrades exactly as m−1 up to a positive multiplicative constant. This rigorous mathematical characterization confirms that the failure of classical decoupled solvers in highly packed micro-channels is not an artifact of poor implementation, but an intrinsic mathematical property dictated by the PDE itself. Furthermore, it explicitly exposes the severe resolution challenge where standard finite element discretizations suffer from a priori error amplification. Consequently, our sharp estimate provides more than just a diagnostic tool; it offers a rigorous quan-titative foundation for designing robust stabilization strategies. Specifically, it reveals that advanced monolithic architectures—such as Augmented Lagrangian (AL) methods—can bypass this geometric penalty entirely [18, 19, 20, 21], provided the penalty parameter is adaptively chosen proportional to 

m2 guided by our pre-asymptotic bounds. Ultimately, this work mathematically validates the necessity of adopting such theoretically-scaled AL formulations, laying the groundwork for high-fidelity, optimal scalable incompressible flow simulations. The organization of this paper is as follows. In Section 2, we rigorously define the geometric configura-tion of the periodic pillar array, introduce the governing Stokes equations, and state the main theoretical results regarding the degradation of the continuous inf-sup constant. In Section 3, we establish three foundational lemmas that are instrumental in proving our main theorems. In Section 4, we prove the main theorems. In Section 5, we present numerical experiments for the validation of our theoretical results, and discuss the numerical implications. Finally, in Section 6, we summarize our findings and dis-cuss their implications for future research in numerical methods for fluid-structure interactions in tight microfluidic chips.

Step 1: Absolute Scaling of the Physical Fields. Before analyzing the errors, we must establish the physical baselines driven by different boundary conditions. 5.6. Validation II: Robustness on Standard Square Micro-pillar Arrays 

Before extending our methodology to complex microfluidic designs, we first validate the proposed parameter-free Augmented Lagrangian (AL) preconditioner on a standard benchmark: a regular square 16 micro-pillar array ( δ = 0 ). We maintain a constant solid packing ratio κ = 0 .25 (i.e., the pillar radius is fixed at 0.25 times the characteristic unit cell diameter) and systematically refine the characteristic mesh density m from 2 to 64 .To rigorously evaluate the algorithmic scalability and preclude any false convergence artifacts (such as volumetric locking inherent to high penalty parameters in P2 − P1 elements), the FGMRES solver is driven by the unpreconditioned physical residual, enforced with a stringent absolute tolerance of 10 −10 .Moreover, to isolate the macroscopic conditioning of the pressure Schur complement from the specific choice of inner algebraic preconditioners, we employ a robust sparse direct solver (e.g., UMFPACK) for all inner velocity block inversions. The development of optimal, scalable multigrid preconditioners for the highly ill-conditioned AL velocity block in these densely packed geometries is a distinct and substantial challenge [18, 19], which falls outside the scope of this theoretical benchmark and is deferred to our future algorithmic research.

Validation III: Generalization to Realistic DLD Arrays 

To definitively demonstrate the generalizability and practical engineering efficacy of our parameter-free stabilization strategy, we turn to a realistic Deterministic Lateral Displacement (DLD) microfluidic array. Unlike standard Cartesian grids, DLD arrays are characterized by a shifted Bravais lattice ( δ > 0), which breaks geometric symmetry and introduces complex, tortuous streamlines responsible for particle fractionation. We construct a sequence of DLD pillar arrays with a characteristic row shift fraction δ = 1 /3 and maintain the solid geometric ratio κ = 0 .25 . The mesh density m is scaled from 2 up to an extreme case of 64 , culminating in a highly ill-conditioned saddle-point system with over 1.76 million degrees of freedom. Crucially, to preclude any spurious convergence artifacts (e.g., false volumetric locking) that can be induced by the highly scaled penalty parameter ( γ = m2) in the P2 −P1 space, we strictly evaluate the unpreconditioned physical residual. The FGMRES solver is enforced to satisfy a stringent absolute tolerance of 10 −10 alongside a relative tolerance of 10 −8.

6. Conclusions and Future Directions 

In this paper, we have rigorously established a sharp pre-asymptotic estimate for the continuous inf-sup constant in densely packed microfluidic arrays. We proved that the constant deteriorates at a sharp rate of Θ( m−1) as the pillar density m increases, exposing the fundamental geometric penalty that dictates the stability of the multiscale Stokes system. Our numerical investigations confirm the significant impact of this stability degradation on both dis-cretization accuracy and the convergence of iterative solvers. By leveraging the sharp inf-sup bound, we proposed an adaptively scaled Augmented Lagrangian (AL) method with γ ∝ m2. Numerical experiments on various complex geometries, including asymmetric DLD arrays, demonstrate that this theoretically-scaled strategy effectively neutralizes geometric ill-conditioning and achieves optimal algorithmic scala-bility for systems with millions of degrees of freedom. Future research will focus on developing geometry-aware multigrid preconditioners for the inner AL velocity blocks and extending this framework to multi-scale Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) problems. These advancements will enable predictive, fully resolved simulations of complex cell deformations and trajectories within intricate micro-channel topologies.
