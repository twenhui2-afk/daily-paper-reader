WEAKLY SPECIAL VARIETIES, CAMPANA STACKS, AND REMARKS ON ORBIFOLD MORDELL 

FINN BARTSCH AND ARIYAN JAVANPEYKAR 

Abstract. We construct the first weakly special surfaces that are not Campana-special, includ-ing the complement of the plane curve x2y3 = 1 in A2. We prove that the set of OK,S -integral points on this surface is non-dense for every number field K and finite set S of finite places of K

if and only if Campana’s Orbifold Mordell conjecture holds for ( Gm, 12 [1]). This basic example carries a natural Gm-action, and the quotient stack is an Artin stack parametrizing points on a C-pair. This leads to the introduction of “Campana stacks”, which encode morphisms of C-pairs in a manner analogous to the role of root stacks for integral points satisfying prescribed divisibility conditions.

The first aim of this paper is to present new examples of weakly special varieties that are not Campana-special. Our basic example is the complement of the plane curve x2y3 = 1 in A2. This yields a two-dimensional weakly special but nonspecial variety and illustrates that the notions “weakly special” and “special” already differ in dimension two. 

Theorem A (Corollary 2.8) . The surface A2 \ Z(x2y3 − 1) is weakly special but not special. 

To prove that A2 \ Z(x2y3 − 1) is weakly special, we apply a fibration theorem (Theorem B). This result is established in Section 2, where we also construct further weakly special nonspecial varieties. 1.1. Weakly special varieties. In the projective setting, Harris–Tschinkel, following suggestions of Abramovich and Colliot-Th´ el` ene, asked whether the converse of Conjecture 1.6 holds (see [HT00a, Conjecture 1.2]). More generally, one may ask whether a weakly special variety over a number field is arithmetically special. We record this as a conjecture (although it is expected to fail in general; see the discussion below). 

Conjecture 1.7 (Weakly Special Conjecture) . If X is a weakly special variety over a number field K, then X has a potentially dense set of integral points. 

Conjecture 1.7 holds for abelian varieties, Enriques surfaces, and certain K3 surfaces and Calabi–Yau threefolds (see [Has03, HT00b, BHPT18, BT00]). Nevertheless, it is expected to fail for several weakly special varieties. Another conjecture, formulated by Campana in [Cam04, Cam11], is the following. 

Conjecture 1.8 (Campana) . Let X be a variety over a number field K. Then X is special if and only if the set of integral points on X is potentially dense. 

Campana introduced the terminology “weakly special varieties” to emphasize the distinction between weakly special and special varieties (see [Cam05]). The first examples of weakly special nonspecial varieties were constructed by Bogomolov–Tschinkel [BT04]. These threefolds fail to be special because they dominate a Deligne–Mumford root stack of general type, indicating that the Weakly Special Conjecture contradicts the analogue of Lang–Vojta for root stacks. In [BJR25, GRTW26, RTW21] (resp. [CP07, CW09]) some of the threefolds of Bogomolov and Tschinkel were used to disprove certain function field (resp. complex-analytic) analogues of the Weakly Special Conjecture. Subsequently, [BCJW] established the existence of nonspecial threefolds over Q that are weakly special but do not dominate any (Deligne–Mumford) root stack of general type. Us-ing these threefolds, [BCJW] further shows that the Weakly Special Conjecture contradicts Campana’s Orbifold Mordell Conjecture discussed below. 1.4. Families of weakly special varieties. To prove that A2 \ Z(x2y3 − 1) is weakly special (Theorem A), we show that the total space of a family of weakly special varieties over a weakly special base is itself weakly special, provided that the family contains no divisible fibres. This criterion was first proved in the projective setting in [BCJW, §2]; here we extend it to the quasi-projective case. 

Theorem B (Theorem 2.3) . Let k be a field of characteristic zero. Let X → S be a surjective morphism of smooth varieties over k whose generic fibre is geometrically connected (hence non-empty). Assume that the following hold. (i) The variety S is weakly special. (ii) The set of s in S(k) for which Xs is a weakly special variety is dense in S.(iii) For every codimension one point s in S, the fibre Xs is not divisible. Then X is weakly special. 

The fact that X = A2 \ Z(x2y3 − 1) is weakly special (Theorem A) follows from applying Theorem B to the surjective morphism X → A1 \ { 1} given by ( x, y ) 7 → x2y3. 4. Campana stacks of generalized C-pairs 

Campana’s theory studies multiplicity conditions such as “at least m” and “divisible by m”. As we saw in the previous section, these conditions admit natural stacky interpretations via Campana stacks and root stacks. It is therefore natural to ask for a broader framework allowing other multiplicity conditions. In this section, we allow the multiplicities to lie in subsets of Z≥1 which are given as finite unions of subsemigroups. This leads to the notion of a generalized C-pair. Such data naturally give rise to Campana stacks built from suitable open subsets of Ar; see Section 4.2. WEAKLY SPECIAL VARIETIES, CAMPANA STACKS, ORBIFOLD MORDELL 19

is a variety over a field k and G is a group scheme over Y , a morphism X → Y is a G-torsor (for the fppf topology) if G acts freely and transitively on X and there exists an fppf covering 

Y ′ → Y such that X ×Y Y ′ is isomorphic to G ×k Y ′; see [Poo17]. Let k be a field of characteristic zero. Campana’s notion of a special variety ascends along finite ´ etale morphisms. More generally, if G is an algebraic group, the property of being special ascends along G-torsors. 

Proposition 1 (Campana) . Let Y be a variety over k. Let G → Y be a finite type separated group scheme with geometrically connected fibres. Let X → Y be a G-torsor. Then X is special if and only if Y is special. Proof. Assume X is special. Since X → Y is surjective and X is special, it follows that Y is special [BJL25, Lemma 2.8]. Assume that Y is special. Since the geometric fibres of X → Y are connected algebraic groups, they are special (see [Barb, Lemma 2.11]). Therefore, as the fibres of 

X → Y are smooth (by Cartier’s theorem), the statement follows from [Barb, Lemma 2.10]. □

The same property holds in the setting of weakly special varieties. 

Proposition 2. Let Y be a variety over k. Let G → Y be a finite type separated group scheme with geometrically connected fibres. Let X → Y be a G-torsor. Then X is weakly special if and only if Y is weakly special. Proof. Assume X is weakly special. Since X → Y is surjective, it follows that Y is weakly special. Conversely, assume that Y is weakly special. Since the general fibres of X → Y are connected algebraic groups, they are special [Barb, Lemma 2.11] and thus weakly special. Since 

X → Y is smooth and its general fibres are weakly special varieties, it follows from Theorem B that X is weakly special. □

Campana’s conjecture that a variety is special if and only if it has potentially dense integral points suggests the following ascension statement: Let Y be an arithmetically special variety and let X → Y be a G-torsor, where G → Y is a finite type separated group scheme with geometrically connected fibres. Then X is arithmetically special. In this appendix, we will prove this expectation assuming G is constant (but also in a few other cases such as when 

G → Y is an abelian scheme or reductive group scheme). 

Theorem 3 (Constant group scheme) . Let Y be a variety over a number field K and let G be a finite type group scheme over K. Write GY = G × Y and let X → Y be a GY -torsor. Then 

X is arithmetically special if and only if Y is arithmetically special. 

The proof of the classical Chevalley–Weil theorem (corresponding to the case where G is finite in Theorem 3) relies on Hermite–Minkowski’s finiteness theorem: given an integer d, a number field K, and a finite set of finite places S of K, the set of isomorphism classes of degree d field extensions L/K unramified outside S is finite. Our proof of Theorem 3 relies instead on other finiteness results for the ring OK,S of S-integers in a number field K. For example, if G is an affine finite type group scheme over OK,S , then the set H 1fppf (Spec OK,S , G ) of isomorphism classes of G-torsors over OK,S is finite [GMB13, Proposition 5.1]. More broadly, finiteness properties for objects defined over OK,S , such as G-torsors, G-gerbes, Brauer classes, smooth proper curves of fixed genus g > 1, or certain Galois representations, 34 FINN BARTSCH AND ARIYAN JAVANPEYKAR 

often lead to Chevalley–Weil type statements via liftability properties for integral points along suitable morphisms; see Remark 7 for a brief discussion. From this perspective, one aspect of the Chevalley–Weil theorem may be viewed as a statement about the liftability of integral points, a viewpoint that extends naturally to algebraic stacks (see for example [JL21, §5]). We establish this lifting result for G-torsors with G affine in this more general setting, as this perspective originally led us to Theorem 3. Our motivation comes from Campana’s Orbifold Mordell conjecture, which requires working in the stacky setting. Note that no connectedness assumption on G is needed in the following result; in particular, it generalizes the lifting part of the classical Chevalley–Weil theorem.

Proposition 5. Let Y be a smooth finite type integral scheme over OK,S . Let G → Y be a finite type group scheme such that one of the following conditions holds. (i) G → Y is an abelian scheme. (ii) G → Y is a smooth affine torus (i.e., there is an fppf covering Y′ → Y such that GY′ is isomorphic to Grm over Y′). (iii) The morphism G → Y is smooth and its geometric fibres are (affine) reductive groups. Then, there is a number field L and a finite set of finite places T of L containing all the places of L lying over S such that Y(OK,S ) is contained in the image of X (OL,T ) → Y (OL,T ).Proof. The set of OK,S -isomorphism classes of abelian schemes (resp. smooth affine tori, resp. reductive group schemes) of bounded dimension over OK,S is finite by Faltings’s theorem [Fal83] and [JL15, §1]) (which itself builds on [BS64]). Thus, if P ∈ Y (OK,S ) and GP is the pull-back of G → Y along P , then the GP range over finitely many OK,S -isomorphism classes of group schemes over OK,S . For a fixed such group scheme G over OK,S , the set of G-torsors over OK,S is finite in case (2) and (3) by the finiteness of H 1fppf (Spec OK,S , G ) (see [GMB13, Proposition 5.1]). In the case of an abelian scheme A over Y, as Y is a regular noetherian scheme, the group of 

A-torsors over Y is torsion [Ray70, Proposition XIII.2.6]. Let n ∈ Z≥1 be the order of X → Y 

(assuming G → Y is an abelian scheme). Then each XP is a GP -torsor of order dividing n,and thus lies in H 1(Spec OK,S , GP )[ n] which is finite. This implies that the XP run over finitely WEAKLY SPECIAL VARIETIES, CAMPANA STACKS, ORBIFOLD MORDELL 35

Theorem 6. Let Y be a finite type scheme over OK,S . Let G be a finite type group scheme over 

OK,S and let X → Y be a G-torsor. Then, there is a number field L/K and a finite set of finite places T of L containing all the places of L lying over S such that every OK,S -point of Y lifts to an OL,T -point of X .Proof. We first reduce to the case that G is geometrically connected over OK,S . To do so, enlarging S, if necessary, we note that there is a normal closed subgroup scheme G0 ⊂ G over 

OK,S such that G/G0 is finite ´ etale over OK,S and G0 is geometrically connected over OK,S . By applying Proposition 4 (or the classical Chevalley–Weil theorem) to the finite ´ etale morphism 

X /G0 → Y , we reduce to the case that G = G0.By applying Chevalley’s structure theorem [Con02] to GK , we may assume that there is an affine finite type group scheme Ga over OK,S , an abelian scheme A over OK,S and a short exact sequence 1 → G a → G → A → 1.

The statement now follows by first applying Proposition 5 to the A-torsor X /Ga → Y and then applying Proposition 4 to the Ga-torsor X → X /Ga. □

Remark 7. The question of whether integral points on a variety (or stack) X over a number field 

K lift to a variety Y dominating X has been widely studied. It is closely related to the Hilbert property [Ser08, §3] and to the notion of arithmetic surjectivity (see, for instance, [LSS20]). In the terminology of Hassett and Tschinkel [HT01, Definition 2.1], the above implies that G-torsors are arithmetically continuous when G is constant or an abelian scheme over Y . Projective bundles provide further examples: by Theorem 2.8 of loc. cit. (due to Colliot-Th´ el` ene), they are arithmetically continuous. A result of Colliot-Th´ el` ene and Iyer shows that any smooth projective family of homogeneous spaces for linear algebraic groups is arithmetically continuous; see [CTI12]. 

Proof of Theorem 3. If X is arithmetically special, then so is Y . Conversely, suppose that Y

is arithmetically special. Then, replacing K by a finite extension if necessary, we may choose a finite set of finite places S of K, a model Y for Y over OK,S with Y(OK,S ) dense, a finite type group scheme G over OK,S with generic fibre G, and a G-torsor X → Y such that XK is isomorphic to X. By potential density of integral points on finite type group schemes, replacing 

K by a finite field extension and S by a larger finite set of finite places if necessary, we may also assume that G(OK,S ) is dense. Then, by Theorem 6, there is a number field L/K and a finite set of finite places T of L containing all the places of L lying over S such that the image of X (OL,T ) in Y(OL,T ) contains Y(OK,S ). In particular, for every P ∈ Y (OK,S ), the fibre XP is isomorphic to G over OL,T . Thus, since G(OK,S ) is dense in G, it follows that XP (OL,T ) is dense in XP .
