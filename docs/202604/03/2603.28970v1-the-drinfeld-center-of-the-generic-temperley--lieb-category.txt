Given any tensor category C, Joyal and Street [JoyStr], based on the work of Drinfeld [Dri], gave a general construction of a braided tensor category Z (C), called its Drinfeld center . This allowed for the discovery of many interesting braided tensor categories, playing a central role in modern mathematics ranging from statistical mechanics to invariants of knots and manifolds to topological quantum field theories and topological quantum computation (for example, see [Tur], [BakKir], [Wan]). Despite the simplicity of its definition (see Definition 2.2), the Drinfeld center of a given tensor category is typically difficult to describe explicitly. In the special case when 

C is a modular tensor category, it was shown in [DGNO] that the center has a particularly nice description. Interestingly, the Drinfeld centers of the representation categories of quantum groups Uq (g) for a Lie algebra g

have not been described explicitly until now. It is the main goal of this paper to describe this center completely in the case when g = sl 2 and q is generic, as well as to suggest an approach for exploring the center for other Lie types. The category of type I representations of Uq (sl 2) over a field K is known to be equivalent to the Temperley–Lieb category TL (q; K), so we work with the latter diagrammatic version. In [And] and [AndPar], Andersen and Paradowski described certain fusion categories associated with any quantum group at a given roots of unity q. Namely, they considered a semisimple quotient of the category of tilting modules of the given quantum group at q. These categories were also considered by Reshetikhin and Turaev in [ResTur]

in the context of 3-manifold invariants. The fusion rules in the g = sl 2 case had already appeared in the work of Verlinde on conformal field theories [Ver]. In the case of the quantum group Uq (sl 2), one can equivalently describe these categories as a semisimple quotient of TL (q; K) for a root of unity q ∈ K. It turns out the categories constructed in this way are modular tensor categories whenever q is a primitive root of unity of order 2κ for an integer κ ≥ 3. We denote these categories by Aκ−1 (K).Our approach for understanding the center of the Temperley–Lieb category is to “approximate” TL (q; C) as a “limit” of the modular tensor categories Aκ−1. More precisely, we express TL (q; C) (for generic q ∈ C) as a subcategory of the ultraproduct of a family of categories Aκi−1(Ki) where κi → ∞ and char Ki → 0. The use of ultraproducts as a way of taking limits of tensor categories was first introduced by Deligne in his seminal paper [Del07]; therein, he described the category Rep (St) for transcendental t ∈ C as a subcategory of the ultraproduct of Rep (Sn) for integers n → ∞ . Later, Harman [Har] extended this result for algebraic t by varying both n as well as the characteristic of the underlying field. This ultraproduct description was used in [FHL] to describe the indecomposable objects of the center of Deligne’s interpolation category Rep (St). It was a rare instance in the subject of representation theory where knowledge about the finite characteristic case (which is typically more difficult) informs our knowledge about the characteristic 0 case. Our results show that the usefulness of this approach is not unique to Deligne’s interpolation categories, but is a more widely applicable phenomenon. First, we observe that the braiding and the Z/2Z–grading on the Temperley–Lieb category induce a canonical functor 

Z : TL (q; C) ⊠ TL (q; C)rev ⊠ Rep (Z/2Z) → Z (TL (q; C)) .

Then, we show that for every q ∈ C× that is not a root of unity, we can identify q with a sequence qi of roots of unity of even orders in the ultraproduct of some fields Ki. In the case of transcendental q, it is enough to take 

Ki = C and qi any sequence of roots of unity of unbounded degree. When q is algebraic, we use a generalization of Zsigmondy’s Theorem to algebraic integers, due to Postnikova and Schinzel [PosSch], to find a sequence of prime numbers pi → ∞ and a sequence of roots of unity qi of even orders 2κi → ∞ in Ki = Fpi whose ultraproduct limit can be identified with q. As a result, we are able to express TL (q; C) as a subcategory in the ultraproduct of Aκi−1(Ki).By relating the center of TL (q; C) to the center of the ultraproduct factors, we are able to extract information about Z (TL (q; C)) through our knowledge of Z (Aκi−1(Ki)) ; the latter ones are easy to describe since Aκi−1(Ki)

are modular tensor categories. Using this ultraproduct approach, we show that, for generic q ∈ C, the center 

Z (TL (q; C)) is semisimple and we describe its simple objects and fusion rules completely. As a result, our main theorem (Theorem 5.17) is that the canonical functor Z is an equivalence of monoidal categories; upon twisting the braiding by a certain (Z/2Z)⊕3 bicharacter, this functor becomes a braided equivalence. We remark that many of the techniques we use should extend in a somewhat straightforward way to other Lie types; one can approximate the categories Rep (Uq (g)) as an “ultralimit” of semisimplifications of the categories of tilting modules over Uq (g) at certain roots of unity. However, we leave this investigation for a later work. One can also apply this ultraproduct technology to express the classical case of q = 1 by using the Verlinde categories Ver p as an approximating sequence. But since the centers of Ver p are more difficult, this approach is not the most fruitful in the classical limit. A geometric approach is more useful in this case; the Drinfeld center of the representation category of an algebraic group G can be described as the category of G-equivariant coherent sheaves supported at finitely many points. Setting G = SL 2, this gives a sheaf-theoretic description of the Temperley–Lieb center at q = 1 .For the case of q = −1, we show that the Temperley–Lieb category is related to the q = 1 case by a cocycle twist. We prove in Proposition 2.27 that the Drinfeld center of a category that is twisted by a cocycle is equivalent to a cocycle twist of the Drinfeld center of the original category. As a result, we get an explicit description of the center in terms of finitely-supported equivariant coherent sheaves on SL 2 in this case as well. Finally, we also discuss the center in the crystal Temperley–Lieb category in Section 6. In a previous joint work with Stroiński [AlqStr], we studied the crystal limit of TL (q; K) as q → 0. We showed that the resulting diagrammatic category CrysTL is equivalent to the linearized category of sl 2–crystals. In a private communica-tion, Etingof asked whether the Drinfeld center of CrysTL contains any interesting objects (other than the ones THE DRINFELD CENTER OF THE GENERIC TEMPERLEY–LIEB CATEGORY 3

induced by the Z/2Z–grading). We conjecture that the answer is no and provide some partial results towards proving this conjecture. The article is organized as follows: In Section 2, we collect the needed constructions and background from the general theory of tensor categories. In Section 3, we give all the necessary background on the Temperley–Lieb category and its semisimplifications when q is root of unity. The goal of Section 4 is to prove that the Temperley– Lieb category can be identified with a subcategory of the ultraproduct of a family of modular tensor categories 

Aκi−1(Ki). Section 5 uses this identification to describe the Drinfeld center of the Temperley–Lieb category for generic q. We make some remarks and formulate some conjectures in Section 6 on the centers of Rep (Uq (g)) 

for other lie algebras g or for exceptional values of q. Finally, Appendix A is a quick self-contained exposition of ultraproducts and their applications to the theories of tensor categories and tensor functors. Despite their apparent usefulness, ultraproducts of tensor categories have only been formally documented in the literature (in the case of Tannakian categories) in [Cru]. Building on the theory developed in loc. cit. , we hope that Appendix A will help address this gap in the literature.

Cauchy Completion. A general construction of completion exists for any pre-additive category A to an additive Karoubian category Ac (i.e. an additive category where every idempotent splits), called its Cauchy completion, which we now describe. First, we form the additive closure of A, whose objects are formal direct sums of objects in A and whose morphisms are formal matrices whose columns and rows are indexed by the summands of the domain and codomain and whose entries are given by a morphism in A between the corresponding summands; let us denote this additive envelope of A by A⊕. Note that if A is monoidal, then so is A⊕ via the obvious formulae for distributivity of ⊗ over ⊕. Next, if B is any additive category, we may consider its Karoubian envelope Bkar 

which formally adjoins idempotents; it is defined by

Note that a spherical tensor category that contains a negligible morphism cannot possibly be semisimple, since a negligible morphism between indecomposables must lie in the Jacobson radical. indeed, if f : X → Y is such a morphism, then, for any g : Y → X, we get g ◦ f being not invertible, for otherwise X would be a summand of 

Y . It follows then that g ◦ f must lie in the Jacobson radical of End( X) since it is outside its unique maximal ideal ( X being indecomposable implies that End( X) is a local ring). Deligne’s Tensor Product of Tensor Categories. Given two finite abelian K-linear categories A and B

over a perfect field K, Deligne [Del90, Section 5] showed the existence of an abelian K-linear category A ⊠ B

and a bifunctor ⊠ : A × B → A ⊠ B which is right-exact in both A and B and is universal with this property; i.e. for any bifunctor F : A × B → C that is right-exact in both A and B, there exists a unique right-exact functor ˜F : A ⊠ B → C satisfying F = ˜F ◦ ⊠. Later, López Franco [LF] extended Deligne’s construction to all finitely cocomplete (for example, semisimple) abelian K-linear categories by showing that Deligne’s construction coincides with Kelly’s tensor product [Kel]. When the categories A and B are both semisimple, their Deligne tensor product A ⊠ B is again semisimple. The simple objects of A ⊠ B are exactly those of the form X ⊠ Y where X and Y are simple objects in A and B,respectively. Languages, Theories, and Models. Definition A.1. A first-order language L is a set of symbols of three types: constant symbols ci, function symbols fj and relation symbols Rk. Associated to each function symbol and relation symbol is a positive integer called its arity .

Secondly, one keeps the additive category structure but drops the axioms of being an abelian category, i.e. the existence of kernels and cokernels and the normality of monos and epis. Now, we would like to examine the semisimplicity of (subcategories of) an ultraproduct of semisimple categories. In general, the ultraproduct of semisimple categories will not be semisimple, but sufficiently well-behaved sub-categories thereof will still be semisimple. To exploit Łoś’s Theorem, we need to express semisimplicity by a first-order language sentence. The next two general folklore lemmas do the trick. Recall that a morphism is called split if it can be written as a composition ι ◦ π where ι is a split monomorphism and π is a split epimorphism. 

Lemma A.24. A tensor category C over an algebraically closed field is semisimple if and only if every morphism of C is split. THE DRINFELD CENTER OF THE GENERIC TEMPERLEY–LIEB CATEGORY 31 

Proof.
