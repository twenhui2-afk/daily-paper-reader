Abstract 

Consider an infinite, rooted, connected graph where each vertex is labelled with an independent and identically distributed Uniform(0,1) random variable, plus a parameter őł

times its distance from the root ŌĀ. That is, we label vertex v with Xv = Uv + őłd (ŌĀ, v ).We say that accessibility percolation occurs if there is an infinite path started from ŌĀ along which the vertex labels are increasing. When the graph is a Bienaym√©-Galton-Watson tree, we give an exact characterisation of the critical value őłc such that there is accessibility percolation with positive probability if and only if őł > őł c. We also give more explicit bounds on the value of őłc. The lower bound holds for a much more general class of trees. When the graph is the lattice Zn for n ‚©ĺ 2, we show that there is a non-trivial phase transition and give some first bounds on őłc. To do this we introduce a novel coupling with oriented percolation. 

# 1 Introduction 

Accessibility percolation is a model inspired by evolutionary biology. We assign a random fitness value to each vertex in a graph. A path is considered accessible if the fitness values along it are strictly increasing, and accessibility percolation occurs when an accessible path exists from the root or origin to ‚ąě (or to a target set of vertices on a finite graph). To introduce some notation, consider a locally-finite connected graph G = ( V, E ) with a specified root ŌĀ. To each vertex v ‚ąą V , attach a label Xv. We are interested in (simple) paths, starting from the root ŌĀ, along which the labels are increasing. We call these increasing 

or accessible paths. The model of accessibility percolation on G is when we keep only those vertices v for which there is an increasing path from ŌĀ to v. As above, we say that accessibility percolation occurs if there is an infinite accessible path; or an accessible path from ŌĀ to some specified target set in the case of finite graphs. The simplest and most-studied case is when the labels are independent and identically distributed (except possibly the label XŌĀ of the root). This is known as the House of Cards 

model, and was introduced by Nowak and Krug in [18], with a biological motivation [1, 11, 13] which we will detail later. Results on this topic include those of Roberts and Zhuo Zhao [20] and Chen [10], who considered the House of Cards model on regular trees, and Berestycki, Brunet and Shi [5, 6], who considered the unit hypercube, along with an inhomogeneous tree designed to mimic the structure of the hypercube. A very similar problem has arisen in the context of 

temporal networks , motivated by computer science, where the labels are often assigned to the edges rather than the vertices; in this case the most-studied underlying graph is the ErdŇĎs-R√©nyi graph [2, 8, 9]. 1.3 Motivation 

Accessibility percolation was introduced by Nowak and Krug [18] as a model for evolution where potential genotypes or phenotypes are represented as the vertices of a graph. Each vertex has an associated fitness, corresponding to the ability of a living organism to survive and reproduce. Mutations in the species‚Äô DNA correspond to moving along an edge to a neighbouring vertex; and on large time scales, it is assumed that only species whose fitnesses are greater than their predecessors can fixate in the population. The focus therefore lies on identifying paths of vertices whose fitness values progressively increase; these paths can be seen as viable evolutionary trajectories. Different biological hypotheses then correspond to different fitness landscapes , or distri-butions for the collection of random variables that capture the fitnesses of the genotypes or phenotypes. The simplest possibility is to assume that the fitnesses are independent and iden-tically distributed. This is called the House of Cards (HoC) model [14], and most mathematical works on accessibility percolation have used this assumption due to its tractability. From the biological perspective, the HoC model implies that the effect of each and every mutation on fitness is so significant that it totally disrupts the underlying genotype‚Äôs fitness architecture, analogous to the collapse of a house of cards. In this paper, we consider a model where fitness values are not i.i.d. but are influenced by both new mutations and preceding genotypes; there is a deterministic bias or evolution-oriented drift over time/generation. This is called the Rough Mount Fuji model, reflecting the fact that fitnesses ascend at a constant rate on average over time/generation, but with significant noise around this gradient. This model was also considered by Nowak and Krug [18] but has received less attention, especially in the mathematical literature, due to the more complex fitness landscape.

x/n , then the number of accessible paths from 0 to 1 rescaled by n converges in distribution to e‚ąíx times the product of two independent exponential random variables. The question of finding the longest increasing path, when independent random variables are assigned to each edge of a graph, has also been studied in computer science, in the context of 

temporal networks [2, 8, 9]. The idea is that we have some underlying graph whose edges are opened at random times, the key question being which vertices can be reached by following edges that appear in increasing order. # 2 Upper bound on the probability of one path being increasing 

In this section, we will provide an upper bound on the probability that the labels along one single path are increasing. Furthermore, the bricklayer lattice (VL, E L) is homeomorphic to the graph consisting of vertices 

Z2+ with all up and right edges, i.e. the first quadrant of the usual oriented square lattice. Since oriented site percolation on Z2+ has an infinite component with probability tending to 1 as the percolation probability converges to 1, we deduce that there exists a directed path of good bricks with probability tending to 1 as n ‚Üí ‚ąě . Finally, by our third observation above, a directed path of good bricks entails existence of an infinite path of directed open edges starting from (0 , 0) that remains within the union of all the (good) bricks; that is, there is n-bricklayer percolation. The final observation that we would like to make is that if őł is sufficiently close to 1, open edges in the inhomogeneous dependent oriented percolation model correspond to edges along which the RMF labels are increasing. If this were true, then an infinite open path in the percolation model would entail an infinite increasing path in RMF accessibility percolation. It is, unfortunately, not necessarily true for all edges; but it is true for those within bricks sufficiently far to the right. This will be enough to complete the proof by extending the path to 0 via an additional finite segment.
