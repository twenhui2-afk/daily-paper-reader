I. CONTEXT AND CHALLENGES 

In the circular restricted three-body problem (here-after CRTBP), a particle moves in the gravity field of two point-like massive stars orbiting each other at con-stant rate and separation. This problem occupies a cen-tral place in celestial mechanics and dynamical astron-omy for more than two centuries. It is fundamental for the physics of binary stars (e.g., Roche lobe overflow, lightcurves), for the dynamics of particles in star-planet systems and protoplanetary discs, and for space guid-ance and Solar System exploration. It is widely reviewed in textbooks [e.g. 1–5], and has several interesting vari-eties [e.g. 6–14]. A remarkable property of the CRTBP is that the test particle is allowed to visit not all but cer-tain regions of space, depending on the Jacobi constant 

C = −2W (r) − v2, which is set by initial conditions in velocity v and position r. The knowledge of equipoten-tial surfaces W (r) = const . (also known as Hill’s surfaces or even “zero-velocity surfaces”) that precisely separate permitted and forbidden regions is therefore of great in-terest. In the plane of the binary, these frontiers draw famous patterns: tadpole-shaped curves around equilat-eral Lagrange points, degenerating in horseshoe shapes as the potential decreases, then in a central peanut fig-ure and an outer oval. Then comes the Roche lobes and Hill’s quasi-spheres [15, 16]. The literature appar-ently does not evoke the eventuality that W (r) is re-versible, unless some approximations [17, 18]. Usually, an equipotential W0 is found from three different numer-ical techniques, which are easily accessible with mod-ern programming language and softwares: i) by suc-cessive approximations based on the fixed-point interac-tion, for instance z = f (z; x, y, W 0), ii) by integration of a total derivative formed by differentiating W , e.g. 

dx/dy |w0 = −∂y W/∂ xW in a plane z = const ., or iii) by detecting the zeros of the function W (x, y, z ) − W0

inside a precomputed grid from a root-finding method. In this article, we derive the solution r(W0) by analyti-cal means, therefore avoiding any systematic and compli-cated numerical procedure, and the generation of tables [e.g. 19, 20]. The solution is exact, generally degenerate (roots of a cubic equation), and involves no series but standard functions.

> D. Remarks

This method yields exact solutions and works very well. Three slight issues must be quoted. First, the ra-dial sampling plays a crucial role, especially when −w

becomes large. As expected, the equipotentials become more and more circular as the energy decreases (in the vicinity of each star, and far enough from the system), and cos ϕ becomes less and less sensitive to r; see Fig. 2c. This can be improved by a densification of points at the edges of branches (see below). A second difficulty is caused by a double degeneracy: a single value of the parameter 3 p ∈ [p(rmin ), −1] is obtained by two different values of r, and there are at most two roots at a given ra-dius. It means that each branch is not revealed in a con-tinuous fashion, but piecewise. This is visible in Fig. 2b: as r decreases, the roots ∆ 0 and ∆ 1 feed both branches A0 and A 1 of component A, then, there is a short range of radii for which both components A and B are constructed simultaneously (namely, 1 .28 ≲ r ≲ 1.36), then branches B0 and B 1 are revealed in parallel. This can be circum-vented by making an inventory of the intersections of an equipotential with the binary axis, as explained in Sect. III. Third, unless a happy coincidence, there is no value of r radius in the user-selected interval that exactly leads to | cos ϕ| = 1 in (5). It means that the intersections of the equipotential with the binary axis (if needed) are not expected to be output from the cubic. This point can be fixed (see Sect. III).

B. Procedure (decision tree) 

From these preliminary quantities, the procedure to construct a given component or branch is as follows (there is no solution for w0 > w L4 ) : 

• case I (tadpole shape): wL3 < w 0 ≤ wL4 . The equipotential has a single component A: branch A 0

(outside the main separatrix S∆) is obtained with ∆0 for r ∈ [r−, r +] (see Sect. II), while branch A 1

is obtained with ∆ 1 in the same interval in radius. Note that r− = r+ = 1 at L 4.

• case II (horseshoe shape): wL2 < w0 ≤ wL3 .Again, there is single component. Branch A 1 is still revealed for r ∈ [r−, r +], but branch A 0 has two parts: the interior part is obtained for r ∈ [−x1, r +]and the exterior part is found for r ∈ [−x2, r −]. 

• case III (interior peanut shape): wL1 < w 0 ≤ wL2 .The equipotential has two components, A and B. Component A has two branches, A 0 and A 1, com-puted for r ∈ [−x1, r +] and r ∈ [r+, x 6], respec-tively. Component B has also two branches, B 0 and B1, computed for r ∈ [−x2, r −] and r ∈ [r−, x 5], re-spectively. Branch B 0 corresponds to the interior part of branch A 0 of case II. 

• case IV (Hill’s quasi-spheres): w∆ < w 0 ≤ wL1 .The equipotential has now three components, A, B and C. Component A is determined as in case III. Components B surrounds the primary and is made of two branches, B 0 and B 1, computed for 

r ∈ [−x2, r −] and r ∈ [r−, x 3], respectively. Com-ponent C has single branch, C 1. It surrounds the secondary and is obtained for r ∈ [x4, x 5]. Note that the Roche lobes appear for w0 = wL1 when components B and C get in contact. 

• case V (Hill’s quasi-spheres; continued): w0 ≤ w∆.The three components are obtained as for case IV, except for component A, which is now fully outside the main separatrix. There is therefore a unique branch A 0, which is computed for r ∈ [−x1, x 6]. This five cases are easily implemented in any program. For each interval in radius here-above, the sampling in radius remains totally free (it can be uniform or not). We observe that the density of points obtained by a uniform spacing is generally low close to the edges of branches (i.e. vicinity of the main separatrix and vicinity of the binary axis). This can be corrected by an adaptive sampling, for instance from a scaled Chebyshev grid.

The function W (r) is therefore fully reversible at any point r of the equatorial plane of the binary (see Ap-pendix B for the off-plane case). The formula for the three components of r(W0) are simple and easy to im-plement in codes. Numerical solvers (see the Introduc-tion) can therefore be avoided to construct equipotential maps, which already represents a huge gain in terms of computing time. As usual in Physics, having a formula is always a considerable advantage over numerical ap-proaches, in particular when it is exact. This opens a few interesting perspectives, to begin with the dynamics of the test particle in the CRTBP itself. The expres-sion r(W0) can be used for perturbative calculus, in the value of W0, in the position r or in the mass ratio q. It is also interesting in the context of binary evolution, as long as the point-mass approximation for the two mas-sive bodies is justified. The geometry of peanut shapes, Roches lobes and Hill’s quasi-spheres rule mass transfers between the two components and subsequently impact the orbital elements [5, 24–27]. In particular, in semi-detached and contact binaries, Roche lobe overflow is a complex problem which requires an accurate description of the equipotential in the vicinity of the inner Lagrange point L 1 where matter transits [28, 29]. The knowledge of a closed-form for r(W0) enables to get the exact shape of the Roche lobes (see Fig. 4 as an example obtained with the formula with q = 0 .3) and around, and this should help in improving the realism of models. Actu-ally, the potential is usually Taylor-expanded at point L 1

[28, 30], which has limited validity, in contrast with the relationship r(W0). The geometry of equipotentials is also a key ingredient for simulating light-curves of close binaries [31–33], from semi-detached ot overcontact bi-naries. The results presented here are particularly well suited to such a task, as the local normal to any equipo-tential surface can be determined exactly by differential calculus from ϕ(r, θ ). This should considerably simplify the production of synthetic diagrams, that could serve as true references.
