# Machine learning methods for finite population parameter estimation in survey sampling 

# Mehdi Dagdoug *

Department of Mathematics and Statistics McGill University, Montréal, Canada 

# David Haziza †

Department of Mathematics and Statistics University of Ottawa, Ottawa, Canada 

Abstract 

This pedagogical review examines the use of machine learning methods in finite-population inference for survey sampling, with an emphasis on design-based validity and statistical inference. While flexible prediction tools offer substantial gains in estimation accuracy, they also introduce important challenges, primarily due to the dependence between the fitted predictors and the sample. We focus on settings in which such predictions enter survey estimation through model-assisted estimation, item nonresponse imputation, and unit nonresponse adjustment. For model-assisted estimation and item nonresponse, we show how cross-fitting and Neyman-orthogonal estimating equations can adapt ideas from dou-ble/debiased machine learning to survey data, allowing the use of high-dimensional or nonparametric learners while preserving root-n consistency and asymptotic normality under suitable conditions. In contrast, for unit nonresponse, standard inverse-probability weighting remains outcome-agnostic and operationally attractive, but this same feature makes doubly robust and orthogonal constructions harder to deploy in official statistics. We also briefly discuss related developments in small area estimation and probability/nonprobability data integration. Overall, the paper highlights both the promise of machine learning and the fundamental inferential challenges it raises for survey practice. 

Keywords: cross-fitting, doubly robust estimation, imputation, inverse probability sampling, model-assisted estimation, Neyman orthogonality, sampling design, variance estimation.

# 1. Introduction 

In the last decade, machine learning (ML) methods have begun to attract considerable attention within National Statistical Offices (NSOs) as potential tools for enhancing the production of official statistics. Their emergence coincides with increasing availability of large and complex auxiliary data sources, advances in computational power, and sustained interest in modernising statistical systems. Although probability sampling and traditional inferential frameworks remain central to the mandate of NSOs, ML offers a flexible predictive layer that is capable of exploiting high-dimensional information, captur-ing nonlinear patterns, and generating accurate and robust predictions. In this paper, we use the term machine learning to refer broadly to a class of data-adaptive prediction methods that estimate an un-known regression or propensity function over a potentially high-dimensional class of functions, rather than by specifying a fixed finite-dimensional parametric model. Typical examples include tree-based methods, kernel and spline smoothers, and neural networks, among many others. Throughout, we use the term architecture to refer to a specific learning procedure together with its associated tuning parameters (or hyperparameters) (e.g., tree depth, regularization parameters, number of layers). Before turning to specific methodological developments, it is useful to highlight several settings in which ML-based prediction is increasingly considered as a key ingredient in survey inference. These settings differ in their inferential goals and modelling assumptions, but share the common feature that predictions serve as components in estimators of finite population parameters such as overall totals, means, or domain-specific estimates. The major applications can be grouped into several broad themes, each reflecting a distinct role played by prediction in the construction of survey estimators. (i) Model-assisted estimation (e.g., Särndal (1992); Breidt and Opsomer (2017)) arises in a design-based framework where the sampling design remains the source of validity, but auxiliary information is incorporated through a working prediction model to improve efficiency. Most often, the estimator retains design-consistency even when the model is misspecified; a classical example is the generalized regression estimator, which combines predictions obtained through a regression model with design weights to improve precision. (ii) Missing data in surveys arise either through unit nonresponse or item nonresponse. Unit nonresponse occurs when sampled units fail to respond to the survey altogether and therefore provide no information on any survey variables. This may arise because of the failure to contact the household despite repeated call attempts or because respondents refuse to participate due to lack of interest or survey burden. Item nonresponse, in contrast, arises when responding units omit values for one or more variables while still participating in the survey. Such missingness often results from sensitivity or privacy concerns for specific questions (e.g., income or health information) or from recall difficulties when respondents are unable to provide accurate values for certain items. We distinguish between these two forms because they require different methodological treatments. For item nonresponse, a common strategy is single imputation, whereby missing values are replaced by plausible substitutes constructed from auxiliary information using deterministic or stochastic imputation procedures (e.g., Chen and Haziza (2019)). For unit nonresponse, treatment typically relies on inverse probability weighting (IPW), in which response propensities are modelled using auxiliary variables and the resulting inverse probabilities are incorporated into the weights to restore representativeness. These adjustments may be combined with calibration to known population totals when available (e.g., Kalton and Flores-Cervantes (2003); Kim and Kim (2007); Haziza and Beaumont (2017)). (iii) Small area estimation (e.g., Rao and Molina (2015)) addresses contexts in which reliable direct survey estimates cannot be produced for specific domains—such as regions, demographic subgroups, or business strata—because of insufficient sam-ple sizes. In such cases, indirect estimation procedures borrow strength across areas using auxiliary information or statistical models to produce stable and interpretable domain-level estimates. (iv) The integration of probability and non-probability samples has gained considerable attention in recent years (e.g., Elliott and Valliant (2017); Kim and Wang (2019); Beaumont (2020); Chen et al. (2020)). This trend is partly driven by the increasing availability of alternative data sources such as adminis-trative records and web panels, but also by the growing difficulties faced by traditional probability surveys, including declining response rates and rising data-collection costs. As a result, relying solely on probability samples has become increasingly difficult in many applications. Integrating probability and non-probability data aims to combine the strengths of both sources: probability samples provide a principled basis for statistical inference, while non-probability data often offer larger sample sizes, better timeliness, or more detailed information. However, valid inference requires appropriate adjust-ments to account for the unknown or uncontrolled selection mechanisms that typically characterize non-probability samples. The increasing use of machine learning methods in survey sampling has opened new opportunities for improving estimation through flexible prediction tools. Unlike classical parametric models, these methods are highly adaptive and are typically trained on the same data used for estimation, thereby introducing complex dependencies between the fitted predictor and the sample. Such dependencies can invalidate standard arguments, affect both bias and variance estimation, and complicate the con-struction of valid confidence intervals. Several mechanisms contribute to these difficulties. First, the prediction functions produced by many algorithms often converge at rates slower than 1/√n, with rates depending on the smoothness of the underlying regression function and on the dimensionality of the covariate space. When such predictions are incorporated into survey estimators—through impu-tation, model-assisted estimation, or propensity modelling—the resulting estimators may fail to satisfy the usual √n asymptotics, making standard variance estimation and confidence interval construction difficult. Second, these estimators are typically not Neyman-orthogonal, so that estimation error in the fitted nuisance functions contributes at the first order rather than vanishing asymptotically. While in classical survey settings this additional variability can often be handled using linearization or analytic approximations, this becomes considerably more challenging when nuisance functions are estimated using highly flexible and non-smooth machine learning procedures. In such cases, resampling methods such as the bootstrap may also fail to accurately reproduce the sampling distribution of the estimator. Third, when prediction error dominates sampling variability, asymptotic normality may fail even under standard regularity conditions. Without √n-consistency, the limiting distribution of the estimator may be non-Gaussian or otherwise difficult to characterize, thereby preventing the use of Machine learning methods for survey sampling 4

standard large-sample inference tools. In this paper, we argue that these issues can be traced back to two main underlying causes: inappropriate use of predictions by some classical estimators, and the dependence between predictions and the sample. This perspective provides a unifying lens through which to understand the behavior of machine learning methods in several key survey sampling settings, including model-assisted estimation and imputation for item nonresponse. A growing body of work (e.g., Chernozhukov et al. (2018); Kennedy (2024); Ahrens et al. (2025)) addresses these challenges through the use of doubly robust or Neyman-orthogonal estimators. Tradi-tionally, doubly robust estimators were introduced to protect against model misspecification: they remain consistent if either the outcome model or the propensity model is correctly specified, though not necessarily both (e.g., Robins et al. (1994); Kang and Schafer (2007); Cao et al. (2009); Kim and Haziza (2014)). A less widely emphasized property is that, when appropriately constructed, doubly robust estimators can also achieve √n-consistency even when the nuisance functions—such as response propensities or outcome regression functions—converge at rates slower than 1/√n. This property relies on orthogonal estimating equations, which remove the first-order sensitivity of the estimator to errors in the estimated nuisance functions. As a result, slower convergence rates affect the estimator only through higher-order terms. This feature becomes particularly valuable when nuisance models are estimated using flexible ML methods, whose convergence rates may be substantially slower than 1/√n.A second key ingredient is cross-fitting (e.g., Chernozhukov et al. (2018); Newey and Robins (2018); Lu et al. (2025)), in which prediction models are trained on one subset of the data and evaluated on another. This procedure mitigates overfitting and allows the orthogonal estimating equations to behave as if the nuisance functions had been estimated on an independent sample, which helps restore asymptotic normality and thereby enables standard Wald-type inference. In this paper, our goal is pedagogical rather than methodological: we review how the double debiased machine learning (DML) framework can be adapted to survey data collected under possibly complex sampling designs. We organize the discussion around three core settings in which prediction plays a central role. First, Section 3 reviews model-assisted estimation and explains how orthogonalization ideas from DML can help recover design-based inference even when prediction models converge more slowly than n−1/2. Second, Section 4 turns to imputation for item nonresponse and shows how debiasing principles can be applied when imputed values are constructed using flexible ML models rather than parametric regression. In both settings, we emphasize how orthogonal estimating equations and cross-fitting help control the bias induced by complex nuisance estimation. Section 5 addresses the treatment of unit nonresponse, where inverse probability weighting remains the dominant tool in official statistics. Unlike imputation, nonresponse-adjusted weights are typically used across many survey variables rather than for a single target parameter, making it difficult to formulate orthogonal score equations or to fully account for variability in estimated response propensities. We therefore discuss why DML is harder to deploy in this setting and highlight several open challenges. Finally, Section 6 briefly considers two additional areas in which ML is increasingly used in survey methodology: (i) small area estimation, where flexible prediction models are used to borrow strength across domains, and (ii) the integration Machine learning methods for survey sampling 5

of probability and non-probability samples, where ML can help estimate selection mechanisms or construct synthetic populations. These topics are treated at a high level, and we provide some key references.

# 3. Model-assisted estimation 

While the Horvitz–Thompson estimator ̂ μHT is d-unbiased under Assumption (1) , it can be inefficient when the survey variable Y is strongly related to auxiliary variables x, because the estimator does not exploit this information. Model-assisted estimation addresses this limitation by explicitly incorporating auxiliary variables through a predictive statistical model, with the objective of improving efficiency relative to the Horvitz–Thompson estimator. The key feature of the model-assisted approach is that the finite-population values y = ( y1, . . . , y N )⊤ are treated as fixed quantities, and inference is eval-uated with respect to the sampling design. We emphasize that the working model is not assumed to be correctly specified; rather, it is used as a device to construct estimators that exploit the association between Y and auxiliary variables while retaining a design-based validity and interpretation. The roots of model-assisted estimation trace back to early work on regression estimation in survey sampling. Classical references include Cochran (1942, 1977) on regression estimators, and Cassel et al. (1976) on regression and generalized regression ideas. Important contributions to the model-assisted paradigm were provided by Särndal (1980) and by Robinson and Särndal (1983), who clarified the role of working models in design-based inference. These developments were synthesized in the monograph of Särndal et al. (1992), which formalized the model-assisted approach. A comprehensive modern review of model-assisted estimation and its extensions can be found in Breidt and Opsomer (2017).

# 5. Inverse probability weighting for the treatment of unit nonresponse 

Unit nonresponse in surveys (see Table (1) ) is typically handled through weight adjustment. Response propensities are estimated using auxiliary information available for both respondents and nonrespon-dents, and the initial design weights wk = 1 /π k are modified accordingly to produce a single set of nonresponse-adjusted weights. These adjusted weights are then applied uniformly to all survey variables 

Y1, . . . , Y p. This variable-agnostic approach is a defining feature of survey weighting and ensures both internal and external consistency across estimates. External consistency means that estimates remain coherent across levels of aggregation; for example, the sum of estimated totals for subpopulations equals the estimated total for the full population. Internal consistency means that known relation-ships between variables are preserved at the estimation stage; for example, if y1 + y2 = y3, then the corresponding estimated totals satisfy the same identity. See, for example, Haziza and Beaumont (2017). In this section, we focus exclusively on unit nonresponse and ignore item nonresponse. Thus, for a given sampled unit, either all survey variables Y1, . . . , Y p are observed (respondents) or none of them is observed (nonrespondents). A method that has been widely used in survey practice is propensity score stratification, also known as the score method. The idea is to first obtain preliminary estimates of the response probabilities, typically from a parametric model such as logistic regression. These preliminary propensities ̂ p(xk)

are then used to partition the sample into groups of units with similar estimated probabilities of re-sponse. This approach has a long history in survey methodology; see, for example, Little (1986); Eltinge and Yansaneh (1997) and Haziza and Beaumont (2007). Closely related stratification methods based on propensity scores have also been studied in the causal inference literature; see, for example, Lunceford and Davidian (2004). More flexible approaches based on nonparametric estimation of the response propensity have also been proposed. In particular, Silva and Opsomer (2006) consider kernel smoothing methods, while Da Silva and Opsomer (2009) extend this framework using local polynomial regression. These approaches avoid the need to specify a parametric link function but typically suffer from the curse of dimensionality, which limits their practical applicability when the number of auxiliary variables is moderate to large. More recently, Opsomer and Riddles (2025) develop a design-based recursive partitioning method (sCHAID) for constructing nonresponse adjustment cells. The approach integrates sampling weights and design features into tree-based algorithms, provid-ing a flexible and interpretable alternative to traditional propensity modeling. provide flexible alternatives, there has been growing interest in the use of ML methods for estimating response probabilities in NSOs. For example, regression trees have been studied for modeling response propensities; e.g., Phipps and Toth (2012). Other ML methods, such as random forests or boosting procedures, have also been explored in recent work. Most of these contributions rely on simulation studies or empirical comparisons to assess the impact of ML-based propensity estimation on IPW estimators; see, for example, Lohr et al. (2015), Kern et al. (2019), and Larbi et al. (2025). While these studies provide useful empirical insights, a comprehensive theoretical understanding of ML-based propensity estimation in survey settings remains limited. From a theoretical point of view, the use of flexible nonparametric or ML methods to estimate response probabilities raises important challenges. In this setting, the behavior of the remainder term in (31) becomes critical. Many ML estimators of p(·) converge at rates slower than n−1/2, and their estimation error may lack the smoothness required for standard Taylor expansions to provide accurate approxima-tions. As a result, the remainder term may no longer be negligible. When this occurs, root-n consistency may fail, the usual central limit theorem may break down, and standard inference procedures can become unreliable. These challenges naturally raise the question of whether the DML framework could help address these issues. As discussed earlier for imputation (see Section 4), DML allows the use of flexible ML meth-ods while preserving root-n inference through Neyman-orthogonal estimating equations. However, extending this idea to survey nonresponse is not straightforward. The main difficulty is that DML is typically built around outcome-specific estimating equations, such as the AIPW estimator, which must be constructed separately for each outcome variable. In contrast, standard survey practice aims to construct a single set of nonresponse-adjusted weights that can be applied to all survey variables. Because AIPW estimators depend explicitly on the outcome variable Y , they do not naturally fit into this framework. Thus, DML methods are inherently outcome-specific, whereas survey weighting is designed to be outcome-agnostic. Reconciling these two perspectives therefore requires methodological development and remains an important area for future research. In contrast with the parametric setting, variance estimation becomes substantially more challenging when the response model is estimated using flexible ML procedures. In general, standard linearization techniques are no longer readily available, as the estimators of p(·) may be highly adaptive and lack the smoothness required for classical Taylor expansions. As a result, resampling methods such as the bootstrap may fail to capture the true variability of IPW estimators, especially when the estimated weights are unstable. This can occur when some estimated response probabilities are very small or when the ML estimator of p(·) is highly non-smooth. That said, bootstrap failure is not inherent to ML itself. When the response probability estimator is reasonably stable—so that small changes in the data lead to small changes in the fitted probabilities—the bootstrap may still provide useful finite-sample approximations. However, as the learning algorithm becomes more adaptive, and in the absence of Neyman orthogonality, the reliability of bootstrap-based inference becomes increasingly uncertain. In Machine learning methods for survey sampling 31 

Appendix B, we illustrate the use of a pseudo-population bootstrap procedure (e.g., Mashreghi et al. (2016)) for IPW estimation and examine its finite-sample performance for several ML methods used to estimate the response probabilities. Finally, the choice of model architecture for estimating response probabilities raises a fundamental issue. In ML, model selection is typically driven by predictive performance, for example through cross-validation aimed at minimizing the mean squared error of ̂ p(x). However, minimizing the prediction error of the response model does not necessarily minimize the mean squared error of the resulting IPW estimator. A response model that achieves good predictive accuracy may still yield highly variable response-adjusted weights w∗ 

> k

and an unstable estimator of μ. In particular, including auxiliary variables that are highly predictive of response but unrelated to the survey variable does not reduce nonresponse bias, yet may substantially inflate variance. Such variables can induce small estimated response probabilities, leading to highly dispersed weights and unstable estimators, for reasons analogous to Basu’s paradox (Basu, 1971). As a result, variables that are predictive of response but weakly or not related to the survey outcome may increase variance without any corresponding bias reduction and are often best excluded from the response model Little and Vartivarian (2005); Beaumont (2005); Park et al. (2019). From an inferential perspective, the relevant objective is therefore not prediction accuracy per se, but rather control of the mean squared error of the IPW estimator itself. Determining how to select an appropriate model architecture and an effective criterion for doing so remains an important topic for further research. 

# 6. Final remarks 

In this final section, we briefly discuss two areas where the integration of ML methods raises important methodological questions: small area estimation and the integration of probability and nonprobability samples. 

6.1. Small area estimation 

Another important problem in official statistics is that of small area estimation (SAE). The goal is to produce reliable estimates for small subpopulations or geographic domains (e.g., regions, municipalities, demographic groups), for which the available sample sizes are often very limited or even zer (e.g., Rao and Molina (2015)). As a result, direct design-based estimators tend to be highly variable or unusable in practice. To address this issue, SAE methods rely on statistical models that allow one to “borrow strength” across domains or from auxiliary information. In particular, linear mixed models are widely used to combine information from multiple areas and improve estimation accuracy in the presence of small samples. Two main modeling frameworks are typically distinguished: area-level models and unit-level models. Area-level models are based on aggregated data (e.g., direct estimators and their sampling variances), whereas unit-level models rely on individual-level data and explicit modeling of the outcome variable. Machine learning methods for survey sampling 32 

While both approaches share the same objective, they differ substantially in their statistical structure and in the type of information available. The connection with machine learning arises naturally, as SAE is fundamentally a prediction problem. A growing literature has explored the use of ML methods in this context, with most developments focusing on area-level models; see, for example, Krennmair and Schmid (2022); Viljanen et al. (2022); Michal et al. (2024); Krennmair et al. (2026); Frink and Schmid (2024). Despite these advances, a fully rigorous methodological framework with provable guarantees is still lacking. In particular, extending modern approaches such as DML to SAE raises important challenges. The hierarchical structure of the data, the need to borrow strength across domains, and the differ-ences between area-level and unit-level settings lead to nontrivial questions regarding identification, estimation, and asymptotic theory. We believe that developing such a framework is an important and promising direction for future research, and this is currently the subject of ongoing work. 

6.2. Data integration of probability and nonprobability samples 

Data integration has become an important topic in survey statistics due to the increasing availability of alternative data sources and the growing challenges faced by traditional surveys. A common frame-work for data integration considers the combination of a probability sample (denoted by SP ) and a nonprobability sample (denoted by SN P ). For units in the probability sample SP , auxiliary variables 

xk are observed together with the sampling weights wk = 1 /π k, but the survey variable yk may not be available. In contrast, for units in the nonprobability sample SN P , both the survey variable yk and the auxiliary variables xk are observed, but the inclusion mechanism in the sample SN P is unknown. The goal is to combine the information from these two sources in order to estimate population quantities such as the finite population mean of Y .Several approaches have been proposed in the literature to address this problem. One class of methods relies on propensity score weighting, where the probability of inclusion in the nonprobability sample is modeled as a function of the auxiliary variables and used to construct weights that correct for selection bias. A second class of methods is based on mass imputation, where the relationship between Y and 

x is estimated using the nonprobability sample and then used to impute values of Y for units in the probability sample. More recently, doubly robust estimators have been proposed, combining both approaches and remaining consistent if either the propensity score model or the outcome model is correctly specified. Important contributions in this direction include the doubly robust framework proposed by Chen et al. (2020), which develops estimators for combining probability and nonprob-ability samples under parametric models for both the propensity score and the outcome regression functions. In recent years, ML methods have been increasingly used to estimate the nuisance functions involved in Machine learning methods for survey sampling 33 

these procedures, such as the selection probability or the outcome regression function. These flexible data-adaptive methods can help mitigate the risk of model misspecification when simple parametric models are inadequate. A number of recent studies have explored the use of ML techniques in the context of data integration, including the use of classification and regression trees, random forests, or boosting methods to estimate propensity scores or outcome models (e.g., Ferri-García and Rueda (2020); Ferri-García et al. (2022); Ferri-Garcia et al. (2024); Beaumont et al. (2024)). These approaches aim to improve robustness to model misspecification and to better capture complex relationships between the study variable and auxiliary variables. However, the use of ML in this context raises important questions regarding statistical inference. In particular, standard estimators may inherit the slow convergence rates of ML estimators, which can lead to non-negligible bias and invalid confidence intervals. As noted by Seaman (2025), the existing literature on data integration has largely focused on point estimation and has paid comparatively little attention to the validity of statistical inference when ML estimators are used for the nuisance functions. To address this issue, Seaman (2025) proposed a framework based on DML, which combines doubly robust estimators with cross-fitting to obtain asymptotically normal estimators even when flexible ML methods are used. To the best of our knowl-edge, this work represents the first theoretical justification for the use of DML methods in the context of data integration combining probability and nonprobability samples.

# B. Simulation study 

We conducted a Monte Carlo simulation study to compare several methods for estimating the response probabilities used in inverse probability weighting. For each scenario, a finite population of size N was generated once and then kept fixed throughout the simulation. Conditional on this finite population, we repeatedly selected simple random samples without replacement and generated nonresponse within each selected sample. Hence, the Monte Carlo variation reflects both the sampling design and the Machine learning methods for survey sampling 43 

nonresponse mechanism, while the underlying population remains fixed. For each scenario, we considered a finite population of size N ∈ { 4000 , 10000 , 20000 }. For each unit 

i ∈ { 1, . . . , N }, four auxiliary variables were generated independently as 

xk1 ∼ N (0 , 1) , xk2 ∼ N (0 , 1) , xk3 ∼ N (0 , 1) , xk4 ∼ U (0 , 1) , k ∈ U. 

The survey variable Y was then generated according to the linear model 

Yk = 10 + 2 xk1 − 1.5xk2 + xk3 + 3 xk4 + εi, εk ∼ N (0 , 22) k ∈ U, 

independently across units. For each fixed population, we drew 10 ,000 independent simple random samples without replacement of size n, where 

(n, N ) ∈ { (200 , 4000) , (500 , 10000) , (1000 , 20000) }.

Thus, the sampling fraction was fixed at n/N = 0 .05 in all scenarios. Within each selected sample, nonresponse was generated independently across sampled units according to a missing-at-random mechanism depending only on the auxiliary variables. More precisely, for a sampled unit k, the response indicator rk was generated from a Bernoulli distribution with response probability

In addition to evaluating the performance of the proposed variance estimators, we also investigated the performance of a pseudo-population bootstrap procedure for variance estimation. Pseudo-population bootstrap methods have been studied extensively in the survey sampling literature; see, for example, Mashreghi et al. (2016) for an overview. In this section, we describe a pseudo-population bootstrap approach tailored to inverse probability weighting (IPW) estimation. The main idea is to approximate the sampling variability of the estimator while treating the response mechanism as fixed. Each sampled unit is replicated a fixed number of times to form an artificial population of size N , from which bootstrap samples are repeatedly drawn using the original sampling design. For simplicity, we focus on the case of simple random sampling without replacement and assume that the ratio N/n is an integer, so that each sampled unit can be replicated exactly N/n times in the pseudo-population. Extensions to more general settings, including unequal probability sampling designs or situations where N/n is not an integer, are available in the literature (see, e.g., Mashreghi et al., 2016), where more general pseudo-population constructions are considered. A key feature of this approach is that the response indicators are kept fixed throughout the bootstrap procedure: a unit that is observed as a respondent in the original sample remains a respondent in all bootstrap samples, and similarly for nonrespondents. This approximation is justified when the sampling fraction n/N is small, which we assume to be the case here. For each bootstrap replication, a sample of size n is drawn from the pseudo-population using the original sampling design. The propensity score model is then re-estimated based on the bootstrap sample, and the IPW estimator of μ is recomputed. For example, in the case of propensity score stratification (PSS), the strata are reconstructed in each bootstrap sample, while for machine learning methods such as regression trees or random forests, the models are refitted using the bootstrap data. Repeating this procedure B times yields bootstrap replicates of the estimator, and the bootstrap variance estimator is defined as the sample variance of these replicates.
