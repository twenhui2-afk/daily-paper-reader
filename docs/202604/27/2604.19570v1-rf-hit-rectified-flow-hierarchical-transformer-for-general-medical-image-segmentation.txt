Abstract —Accurate medical image segmentation requires both long-range contextual reasoning and precise boundary delin-eation, a task where existing transformer- and diffusion-based paradigms are frequently bottlenecked by quadratic computa-tional complexity and prohibitive inference latency. We propose RF-HiT, a Rectified Flow Hierarchical Transformer that inte-grates an hourglass transformer backbone with a multi-scale hierarchical encoder for anatomically guided feature condition-ing. Unlike prior diffusion-based approaches, RF-HiT leverages rectified flow with efficient transformer blocks to achieve linear complexity while requiring only a few discretization steps. The model further fuses conditioning features across resolutions via learnable interpolation, enabling effective multi-scale representa-tion with minimal computational overhead. As a result, RF-HiT achieves a strong efficiency–performance trade-off, requiring only 10.14 GFLOPs, 13.6M parameters, and inference in as few as three steps. Despite its compact design, RF-HiT attains 91.27% mean Dice on ACDC and 87.40% on BraTS 2021, achieving per-formance comparable to or exceeding that of significantly more intensive architectures. This demonstrates its strong potential as a robust, computationally efficient foundation for real-time clinical segmentation. 

Index Terms —Medical Image Segmentation, Rectified Flow, Vi-sion Transformer, Generative Models, hierarchical transformer, Efficient Deep Learning 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Accurate medical image segmentation is a fundamental pre-requisite for clinical decision-making and disease monitoring. This task demands pixel-level precision across highly hetero-geneous imaging conditions. Manual annotation by clinical ex-perts is time-consuming, subject to inter-rater variability, and does not scale to the volumes required by modern workflows. These challenges have driven sustained interest in automated segmentation methods powered by deep learning. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs), particularly the U-Net architecture [1], long served as the dominant paradigm for medical image segmentation. While U-Net effectively fuses local and contextual information, the inductive biases of convolution limit its receptive field, making it difficult to model critical long-range spatial dependencies. The Vision Transformer (ViT) [2] introduced the powerful capacity of Transformers [3] for modeling long-range depen-dencies in computer vision. By enabling global self-attention, ViT successfully addresses the limitations of convolution, fostering richer contextual reasoning for dense prediction tasks [4]–[6]. However, standard Transformer segmentation models face a fundamental trade-off: global attention scales quadratically and is computationally prohibitive for high-resolution inputs, while purely local attention (e.g., Swin Transformer [7]) restricts long-range communication. To navigate this trade-off, the recently introduced Hour-glass Diffusion Transformer (HDiT) [8] effectively overcomes both the quadratic complexity of standard Transformers and the restricted receptive fields of CNNs. By utilizing global self-attention at a downsampled, low-resolution bottleneck to capture comprehensive global context, and employing efficient neighborhood attention [9] at higher-resolution scales to ex-tract fine-grained local features, HDiT achieves linear O(n)

spatial complexity while preserving global context. Building on these structural advantages, we adopt the HDiT backbone for efficient dense prediction tasks. In parallel, generative paradigms currently lead the state-of-the-art in representing complex distributions and providing uncertainty estimates that discriminative models lack. Denois-ing diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) [10] approach segmentation as a conditional generation problem [11], [12], offering stochastic sampling that yields multiple plausible segmentation hypotheses and provides calibrated uncertainty maps. However, DDPMs require hundreds of inference steps, making them impractically slow for clinical deployment. Rec-tified flow [13] offers a principled remedy. By training a neural ODE to transport noise to data distributions along straight-line trajectories, rectified flow achieves high generation quality in as few as 15–20 steps, massively accelerating inference. Inspired by the inference speed and generation quality of rectified flow, combined with the efficient and powerful back-bone of HDiT, we propose the Rectified Flow Hi erarchical 

Transformer ( RF-HiT ), a novel and highly efficient generative

framework for 2D medical image segmentation. Our frame-work features a dual-pathway design comprising a main flow model and a separate feature encoder. The main flow model employs a hierarchical encoder-decoder structure to predict the generative trajectory. To ensure computational efficiency without sacrificing global context, this flow model applies lightweight neighborhood attention at high-resolution levels and restricts quadratic global self-attention to the lowest-resolution bottleneck. Concurrently, the feature encoder mir-rors the encoder path of the main model to extract multi-scale anatomical representations directly from the original image. Fusing these spatial features into corresponding levels of the flow model embeds rich structural context into the generation process, achieving precise boundary localization while simul-taneously overcoming transformer attention bottlenecks and diffusion sampling delays. The resulting architecture is remarkably lightweight, com-prising only 13.6 million parameters and requiring just 10.14 GFLOPs . Despite this compact footprint, RF-HiT achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art deterministic and generative methods on two challenging benchmarks: the BraTS 2021 multi-region brain tumor segmentation challenge and the ACDC cardiac segmentation dataset. A. Generative Models for Medical Segmentation 

Continuous-time generative models, namely diffusion and flow-based approaches [10], [14], have recently achieved state-of-the-art results in image and video generation [15], [16]. They surpass GANs in image quality and diversity while avoiding the instability and mode collapse issues that GANs are known for. Diffusion models corrupt data into Gaussian noise via a stochastic forward process, then train a neural net-work to approximate the reverse denoising process [10]. Amit et al. [17] first applied them to image segmentation, proposing a dual-encoder architecture that merges features from the input image and current segmentation estimate via summation, then iteratively refines the segmentation through the denoising process. In medical imaging, Wolleb et al. [11] applied diffu-sion models to brain tumor segmentation and leveraged their stochastic sampling by conditioning the reverse process on MRI images as an anatomical prior. This sampling generates multiple segmentation hypotheses that, when averaged, im-prove performance and provide pixel-wise uncertainty maps. Subsequent work extended diffusion-based segmentation to volumetric data. Diff-UNet [18] proposed the first end-to-end 3D diffusion model for medical segmentation, introducing a separate feature encoder for anatomical context and a Step-Uncertainty based Fusion module that weights predictions across denoising steps. MedSegDiff-V2 [12] integrated vision transformers into the diffusion framework through Anchor Conditioning and a Spectrum-Space Transformer, achieving state-of-the-art performance across 20 medical segmentation tasks spanning five imaging modalities. Despite their effec-tiveness, diffusion models suffer from slow inference due to numerous denoising steps. Rectified flow [13] offers a simpler alternative, learning a neural ODE that transports a source distribution to a target along straight-line paths. These straightforward trajectories yield highly efficient models requiring few simulation steps. Recently, Bekhouche et al. [19] incorporated rectified flow into a Diffusion Transformer for skin lesion segmentation, achieving strong results in just 15 steps. However, rectified flow remains largely underexplored for broader medical segmentation. 

B. Medical Segmentation with Transformers 

Originally proposed for machine translation, the Trans-former architecture [3] has been widely adopted in computer vision tasks [2], [20]. Dosovitskiy et al. [2] introduced the Vision Transformer (ViT), which achieved competitive accu-racy on image recognition by treating images as sequences of patches. In medical image segmentation, Chen et al. [4] introduced TransUNet, a hybrid CNN-Transformer architec-ture that combines the local feature extraction capabilities of CNNs with the global context modeling of Transformers. To effectively utilize 3D volumetric data for brain tumor segmen-tation, Wang et al. [21] proposed TransBTS, which leverages a 3D CNN to extract local spatial features and subsequently applies the Transformer to model global dependencies among consecutive slices. Hatamizadeh et al. [5] proposed UNETR, which employs a pure ViT-based encoder that directly pro-cesses 3D input patches to learn sequential representations, connected to a CNN-based decoder via skip connections to fuse local and global information. Standard ViT-based methods incur high computational complexity for dense prediction tasks due to their quadratic attention cost. To address this limitation, Liu et al. [7] proposed the Swin Transformer, which employs shifted windows and hierarchical feature maps to effi-ciently model multi-scale representations. Building upon this, Hatamizadeh et al. [6] proposed Swin UNETR, which utilizes the Swin Transformer as an encoder to extract multi-scale features and model long-range dependencies, coupled with a Fig. 1. CNN-based decoder to generate the final output. Recently, several excellent works have further optimized hierarchical architectures. It is worth mentioning that an efficient and highly effective vision backbone, the Hourglass Diffusion Transformer (HDiT) [8], was recently introduced and achieved remarkable success in high-resolution image synthesis. This improved backbone leverages neighborhood attention, which Crowson et al. showed to outperform the shifted window attention mechanism used in the Swin Transformer. HDiT also incorporates other architectural enhancements such as learnable skip connection interpolations and 2D axial rotary positional embeddings to enhance generation quality. Inspired by its success, we adapt the HDiT backbone for our task, utilizing it as the basic unit to build an efficient generative model for medical image segmentation. III. M ETHOD 

A. Rectified Flow Models 

Rectified Flow [13] learns an ordinary differential equation (ODE) transporting samples from a prior distribution π0 (e.g., Gaussian noise) to a target data distribution π1 (e.g., segmen-tation masks). The key idea is to learn straight-line paths connecting the two distributions, yielding computationally efficient models that can be simulated accurately with few discretization steps. Given a sample X1 from the data distribution and a noise sample X0 ∼ N (0 , I), we construct an interpolated sample 

Xt at time t ∈ [0 , 1] via linear interpolation: 

Xt = tX1 + (1 − t)X0. (1) The ground-truth velocity, representing the direction of the straight path from noise to data, is: 

vt = dXt

dt = X1 − X0. (2) A neural network vθ is trained to predict this velocity by minimizing the mean squared error: 

L = Et, X0,X1 ∥vθ (Xt, t ) − (X1 − X0)∥2 , (3) where t ∼ U (0 , 1) is sampled uniformly. This objective is a simple least squares regression that can be optimized with standard stochastic gradient methods. At inference, we sample X0 ∼ N (0 , I) and solve the learned ODE forward in time. In practice, we use a first-order Euler solver with N discrete steps. The straight-line formulation of Rectified Flow enables accurate sampling with relatively few steps compared to diffusion-based approaches. 

B. Efficient Hourglass Transformer Backbone 

Our velocity network vθ adapts the efficient Hourglass Diffusion Transformer (HDiT) [8] from image synthesis to medical segmentation. HDiT employs a hierarchical U-Net-like structure [1] with an encoder, bottleneck, and decoder connected via learnable skip connections. Representations are progressively downsampled through token-merging operations as they descend the encoder levels, processed at the lowest res-olution in the bottleneck, and then upsampled symmetrically in the decoder. Skip connections between encoder and decoder levels at matching resolutions are merged using a learnable linear interpolation, enabling the model to adaptively balance fine-grained spatial details and high-level contextual features. To enhance details and reduce computational cost, neighbor-hood self-attention is used at higher-resolution levels, while global self-attention is used at lower resolutions to model the global context. This design achieves O(n) complexity with respect to the number of image tokens n, compared to the 

O(n2) scaling of standard vision transformers. Each transformer block incorporates AdaRMSNorm condi-tioning, where normalization scales are predicted by a mapping network from the timestep embedding. 

C. Proposed Framework 

As illustrated in Figure 1, our overall framework consists of two parts; the main Hourglass flow model (HFM), and a separate Hierarchical Feature Encoder (HFE). HFM follows an encoder-decoder structure derived from HDiT. Specifically, we employ three hierarchical levels. The first two levels use neighborhood attention layers, while global self-attention is reserved for the final bottleneck level. HFE mirrors HFM’s encoder path. First, HFM serves as our velocity network vθ and receives as input the channel-wise concatenation of the noised segmentation map Xt ∈ RCseg ×H×W and the original condi-tioning image I ∈ RCI×H×W , where Cseg denotes the number of segmentation classes and CI the number of original image channels. vθ generates the predicted velocity field matching the spatial dimensions of Xt. Second, HFE processes as input the original image I and extracts the multi-scale features, these are fused with the corresponding encoder features of HFM at each hierarchical scale before downsampling to the next level. this fusion further strengthens the image-guided segmentation refinement by introducing precise anatomical context from the conditioning image. For feature fusion, we employ the same learnable linear interpolation mechanism originally used in HDiT for skip connection merging. Given main features 

F(l) and conditioning image features C(l) at level l, the fused representation is obtained through a learnable interpolation operation: 

˜F(l) = (1 − αl)F(l) + αlC(l), (4) where αl is a trainable scalar parameter. This per-level fusion injects rich, scale-consistent anatomical context into the gen-erative process, grounding the velocity field prediction with stronger structural and anatomical guidance, which in turn improves boundary localization. B. Implementation Details 

All experiments are conducted using Python 3.10 and Py-Torch 2.9.0 on a single NVIDIA TITAN V GPU. Table I details the configurations of RF-HiT architecture. Input images are normalized and resized to 224 × 224 . Furthermore, single-channel multi-class segmentation label maps are converted into multi-channel representations via one-hot encoding. Models are trained for 1000 epochs on ACDC and 300 epochs on BraTS 2021 with a batch size of 32. We use the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1×10 −4 and weight decay of 

1×10 −4. The learning rate follows a cosine annealing schedule with a linear warm-up phase spanning the first 1% of training steps. To improve generalization, we apply a set of data augmentation: random horizontal and vertical flips, random rotations and scaling, intensity scaling and shifting, gamma correction, and additive Gaussian noise. All 3D volumes are processed slice by slice, and the predicted 2D slices are stacked to reconstruct the 3D prediction for evaluation. The final segmentation masks are obtained by applying per class thresholds, determined via a grid search of 100 evenly spaced values within the range [0 .2, 0.8] on the validation set. We find that while 1 Euler step maintains strong performance, using 3 steps provides a small but consistent gain while still ensuring highly efficient and fast inference. Therefore, all reported results use N=3.

D. Ablation Study 1) Effect of Hierarchical Feature Encoder (HFE): To in-vestigate the contribution of HFE, we perform an ablation study on ACDC, comparing full RF-HiT against a baseline omitting HFE. Without HFE, the conditioning image is solely concatenated with the noisy mask at the HFM input, omitting multi-scale feature fusion. As shown in Table IV, incorporating the HFE significantly improves the segmentation performance, increasing the mean DSC from 90.69% to 91.27%. This gain highlights the limita-tions of standard input-level conditioning, where the main flow model must expend representational capacity to independently extract and propagate structural cues throughout its depth. By extracting dedicated multi-scale anatomical representations and explicitly infusing them at matching resolutions in the flow network, the HFE provides critical structural guidance that ensures local details and complex tissue boundaries are faithfully preserved during the generative process. 

2) Effect of Feature Fusion Mechanism: To evaluate the impact of how features from HFE are injected into HFM, we compare the learnable linear interpolation (lerp) mechanism against a simple feature addition baseline. In the addition variant, multi-scale conditioning features are directly added to the corresponding HFM representations. As shown in Table V, simple addition yields a mean DSC of 91.09% on the ACDC dataset. Learnable linear interpolation improves this to 91.27% by adaptively weighting HFE condi-tioning features against HFM’s internal representations at each hierarchical level. This demonstrates that parameterized fusion outperforms naive addition in balancing anatomical guidance with the generative trajectory. E. Visualizations 

In Figure 2, we present representative segmentation results by RF-HiT on the ACDC dataset, covering the left ventricle (LV), myocardium (Myo), and right ventricle (RV). The final row displays pixel-wise error maps highlighting false positives, false negatives, and class confusion. The figure showcases highly accurate segmentations (first 3 columns) alongside more challenging anatomical cases (last 2 columns). Overall, RF-HiT consistently produces anatomically coherent segmen-tations with smooth boundaries. The predicted label maps exhibit strong spatial contiguity, forming continuous, well-connected regions for each anatomical class without spurious fragmentation. The error maps reveal that minor misclassi-fications occur primarily along anatomical boundaries, most notably between the Myo and LV. In cases where the RV is relatively small or geometrically complex (fifth column), confusion can also arise between the RV and Myo. Further-more, in challenging scenarios exhibiting low tissue contrast or ambiguous structural boundaries (fourth column), the model occasionally struggles to delineate precise edges, leading to slight over segmentation of the RV. Despite these edge-case difficulties, RF-HiT demonstrates robust generalization across a wide spectrum of cardiac morphologies. V. C ONCLUSION 

In this work, we introduced RF-HiT, a Rectified Flow Hierarchical Transformer for efficient and accurate medical image segmentation. By combining an hourglass transformer backbone with multi-scale hierarchical feature conditioning, the proposed model effectively captures both global context and fine-grained structural details. Leveraging rectified flow enables fast inference with only a few discretization steps, while efficient transformer blocks ensure linear computational complexity. Extensive experiments on ACDC and BraTS 2021 demonstrate that RF-HiT achieves a strong balance between performance and efficiency, reaching competitive segmentation accuracy with significantly reduced computational cost.
