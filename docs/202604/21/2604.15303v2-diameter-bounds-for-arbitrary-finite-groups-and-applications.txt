DIAMETER BOUNDS FOR ARBITRARY FINITE GROUPS AND APPLICATIONS 

SEAN EBERHARD, ELENA MAINI, LUCA SABATINI, AND GARETH TRACEY 

Abstract. We prove a strong general-purpose bound for the diameter of a finite group depending only on the diameters of its composition factors and the maximal exponent of a normal abelian section. There are a number of notable applications: (1) if G is a finite soluble group of exponent e, diam (G) ≪ e(log |G|)8,(2) anabelian groups with bounded-rank composition factors have polylogarithmic diameter, (3) transitive soluble subgroups of Sn have diameter ≪ n5, and (4) Grigorchuk’s gap conjecture holds for any finitely generated group acting faithfully on a bounded-degree rooted tree. Additionally, conditional on Babai’s conjecture, (5) any transitive permutation group of degree n has diameter bounded by a polynomial in n (a folkloric conjecture), and (6) Grigorchuk’s gap conjecture holds for residually finite groups, and thus the conjecture reduces to the simple case. 

1. Introduction 

If G is a group and X ⊆ G (usually a generating set), we write ℓX for the length function with respect to X, i.e., if g ∈ G then ℓX (g) is the length of the minimal representation of g as a product of elements of X ∪ X−1, or 

∞ if none exists. For a subset S ⊆ G we write ℓX (S) = sup g∈S ℓX (g). The 

diameter of a finite group G with respect to X is diam (G, X ) = ℓX (G), and we define diam( G) = max {diam( G, X ) : X a generating set for G}.

The most important conjectures about diameters of finite groups are the following two. The first is folkloric, but seems to have first taken definite form in the early 1980s: it is implicit in Driscoll–Furst [16], and explicit in Kornhauser–Miller–Spirakis [37] and McKenzie [45]. The second is universally attributed to Babai, even though it first appeared in a joint paper with Seress [3]. 

Conjecture 1 (folklore) . If G ≤ Sn is transitive then diam( G) ≪ nO(1) .

Let us briefly give the idea of the proof of Theorem 1.1 as well as its corollaries. The starting point is that there is a subadditive extension bound for lengths: for any normal subgroup N ⊴ G,

ℓX (G) ≤ ℓX (N ) + ℓX (G/N ).

Here ℓX (G/N ) is just the diameter of the quotient G/N with respect to the projection XN/N of the generators, so 

ℓX (G/N ) ≤ diam( G/N ).

Bounding the term ℓX (N ) is tricky, however, as X is a generating set for G,not N , but at least 

ℓX (N ) ≤ m diam( N )provided that N has a generating set Y with ℓX (Y ) ≤ m. In general a basic lemma of Schreier gives m ≤ 2 diam( G/N ) + 1, and this implies diam( G) ≪ diam( N ) diam( G/N ).

However, because the diameters are multiplied in this bound, this is an expensive inequality that we must apply sparingly. Our arguments therefore focus on constructing generating sets for small normal subgroups by more efficient means than Schreier’s lemma. In the case of a soluble group, we achieve this by descending the derived series using commutators and conjugation in a natural way, taking advantage DIAMETER BOUNDS FOR ARBITRARY FINITE GROUPS 5

of an old lemma of Milnor (used in the proof of the Milnor–Wolf theorem). We also take advantage of the fact that the derived length cannot be very large: the derived length of a soluble group G is bounded by O(log log |G|) by a result of Glasby [23]. If G happens to be a soluble transitive permutation group of degree n then we have even sharper bounds for its derived length, and also for the exponent parameter ε(G). In the case of an arbitrary finite group, we first consider the quotient of G

by its soluble radical. The quotient embeds as a subdirect product of a finite collection of monolithic groups with nonabelian socles. The prototypical example of such a group is the iterated wreath product 

G = A5 ≀ · · · ≀ A5.

For such groups we use an inductive argument to bound the length of some nontrivial element of the socle. For this we rely on a generalization of an argument of Wilson from [57] (in fact, the study of Wilson’s papers [56, 57] motivated much of the present paper). There is a well-known theorem of P´ alfy–Wolf [50, 58] that asserts that the order of a primitive soluble group G ≤ Sn is bounded by a polynomial function of n; in fact |G| ≤ 24 −1/3nc, where c ≈ 3.25. This influential result was extended by Pyber [51, Theorem 2.10] (see also Guralnick–Mar´ oti– Pyber [29]), who showed that if G ≤ Sn is primitive then the product of the orders of its abelian composition factors is bounded by the same function, and moreover that the number of nonabelian composition factors is O( log n). We now discuss important applications of our results to the growth of residually finite groups. If G is a group generated by a finite set X, the 

growth function of X (or of G, by abuse) is defined by 

γX (n) = |BX (n)|, BX (n) = {g ∈ G : ℓX (g) ≤ n}.

Growth functions are conventionally compared in the following way: we write f ⪯ g if there is a constant C > 0 such that f (n) ≤ Cg (Cn ) for all n > 0. This relation defines a preorder on the set of growth functions, and the equivalence class [ γX ] is an invariant of G, i.e., independent of the choice of generating set X; in fact it is a quasi-isometry invariant. Growth functions of typical examples (abelian groups, soluble groups, linear groups, ...) are generally either polynomially bounded or exponential. A group 

G = ⟨X⟩ is said to have intermediate growth in all other cases, i.e., if 

nd ≺ γX (n) ≺ exp( n) for all d.It is not difficult to prove that a virtually nilpotent group has polynomial growth, i.e., γX (n) ⪯ nd for some integer d. Gromov [27] famously proved the converse: if G has polynomial growth then G is virtually nilpotent. 

Grigorchuk’s gap conjecture predicts the existence of a constant β > 0 such that the same conclusion holds under the much weaker hypothesis that 

γX (n) ≺ exp (nβ ). The strong version asserts that one can take β = 1 /2. See the introduction to [17] for more on the history of this question, and see [17, Section 5] for even stronger variants. 

Conjecture 3 (Grigorchuk) . There is a constant β > 0 such that the following holds. If G is a group generated by a finite set X and γX (n) ≺

exp( nβ ) then G is virtually nilpotent. 

As observed by Grigorchuk (see [25, Theorem 7.4]), the main result of Bajorska–Macedo´ nska [4] implies that the gap conjecture reduces to two extreme cases: residually finite groups and simple groups. For further reduction theorems in this spirit, see [26]. All the early examples of groups of intermediate growth were residually finite. Non-residually-finite examples were first constructed by Erschler [19] and Bartholdi–Erschler [8]. The first simple group of intermediate growth was constructed by Nekrashevych [48] (solving a long-standing open problem). Using our methods we can show that Babai’s conjecture (Conjecture 2) implies Grigorchuk’s gap conjecture for residually finite groups. We formulate the result in an unconditional way as follows.
