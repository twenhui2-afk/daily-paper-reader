# On the volume of the elliptope and related metric polytopes 

## David Avis and Luc Devroye April 21, 2026 

Abstract 

In this paper, we investigate the relationships between the volumes of four convex bodies: the cut polytope, metric polytope, rooted metric polytope, and elliptope, defined on graphs with n vertices. The cut polytope is contained in each of the other three, which, for optimization purposes, provide polynomial-time relaxations. It is therefore of interest to see how tight these relaxations are. Worst-case ratio bounds are well known, but these are limited to objective functions with non-negative coefficients. Volume ratios, pioneered by Jon Lee with several co-authors, give global bounds and are the subject of this paper. For the rooted metric polytope over the complete graph, we show that its volume is much greater than that of the elliptope. For the metric polytope, for small values of n, we show that its volume is smaller than that of the elliptope; however, for large values, volume estimates suggest the converse is true. We also give exact formulae for the volume of the cut polytope for some families of sparse graphs.

The metric polytope, rooted metric polytope and elliptope all contain the cut polytope and hence, for optimization purposes, provide relaxations. The rooted metric polytope contains the metric polytope, but neither is contained in or contains the elliptope. The metric polytope and rooted metric polytope are linear relaxations defined by the triangle inequalities, all of which are facets of the cut polytope. The elliptope provides a semi-definite relaxation that contains all vertices of the cut poly-tope as extreme points. Optimization over these three bodies can be performed in polynomial time, whereas optimization is NP-hard in general for the cut polytope. The goal of this paper is to compare how well the three relaxations approximate the cut polytope in terms of their respective volumes. We denote the volume of a closed, bounded convex set K as vol( K). Consider polytope P ⊆ Rn and closed bounded convex set Q ⊆ Rn such that P ⊆ Q. Such pairs of convex sets appear frequently in cases where optimization over P is NP-hard, but can be done in polynomial time over Q. 19 6 Conclusion and open problems 

In this paper, we use volume bounds to give a measure of how well metric polyhedra approximate the cut polytope compared to that provided by the elliptope. Firstly in Section 3, we derived exact formulae for vol(Cut( G)) for various sparse graphs. Since these graphs have no K5 minor this is also the same as vol(Met( G)) .In Section 4, we gave a tight bound on the volume of the elliptope of the complete graph Kn. Exact computation for small n showed the metric and semimetric poly-topes gave a much closer approximation to the cut polytope than the elliptope does. However volume estimation indicates that this relationship reverses with n ≥ 13 and for large n the elliptope appears to give a far better approximation. As a continuation of this work we give two open problems. 

Open problem 1. Find formulae for vol( E(Cn)) , where Cn is a cycle of length n ≥ 4.

On the other hand, we have an exact formula and asymptotic bound on vol( En)

but no analytic bounds on the corresponding cut and metric polytopes. 

Open problem 2. Prove bounds on vol(Met n) and vol(Cut n).

Finally, we recall Lee and Skipper [19] posed the challenge of finding a polynomial time algorithm for finding vol( Cor (G)) for graphs G with no K4 minor (series-parallel graphs). Via the covariance, this is equivalent to computing vol(Cut( ∇G)) . These are a subset of graphs with no K5 minor and so Cut( ∇G) = Met( ∇G). An even bigger challenge would be to find an algorithm for computing vol(Cut( G)) for any graph G with no K5 minor or show that it is #P-hard.
