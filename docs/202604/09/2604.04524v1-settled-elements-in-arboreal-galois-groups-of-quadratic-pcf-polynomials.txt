1. I NTRODUCTION 

Let K be a field of characteristic not equal to 2 and let ¯K denote a separable closure of K. Let f (x) be a rational function of degree two defined over K, and fix a point a ∈ K such that for every n ≥ 1 the polynomial f n(x) − a has exactly 2n distinct roots. The set of all iterated preimages of a under f can be organized into a rooted binary tree T :vertices at level n correspond to solutions of f n(x) = a, and edges connect each vertex to its image under f . Passing to the inverse limit over all levels yields an infinite rooted binary tree equipped with a natural action of the absolute Galois group GK := Gal( ¯K/K ), arising from its action on the roots of the iterates of f .This action defines a continuous homomorphism 

ρf,a : GK −→ Ω := Aut( T ),

called the arboreal Galois representation associated with (f, a ). Its image, denoted Gf,a , is referred to as the arboreal Galois group . These groups capture arithmetic information about the fields generated by iterated preimages and have been studied from a variety of perspectives in arithmetic dynamics; see, for example, [16, 18, 21, 23, 24, 26]. Concrete descriptions and explicit computations appear in [1, 2, 4, 8, 9, 11, 12]. One approach to studying arboreal Galois groups is to analyze how their elements act on the tree T . In particular, one can study how the cycle structure of an automorphism changes at higher levels of the tree. Following Boston and Jones [5, Definition 3.2], [6, p. 216], a cycle of an element τ ∈ Ω at level n is called stable if its length increases at every higher level. An automorphism τ is called settled if the proportion of vertices contained in stable cycles at level 

n approaches 1 as n → ∞ (see Definition 3.1). This notion was originally motivated by questions about polynomial factorization modulo primes [7, Conjecture 2.2] and is useful for studying the arithmetic structure of arboreal images. A subgroup of Ω is called densely settled if it contains a dense set of settled elements. Boston and Jones [5, pg. 30] conjecture that the arboreal Galois groups of quadratic polynomials are densely settled. Settledness has been studied in the case of polynomials—particularly by Boston–Jones [5–7] and Cortez–Lukina [10]. In contrast, in the non-polynomial setting, the first author [13] showed that there exist many quadratic rational functions f for which the groups Gf,a do not contain any settled elements. We define the postcritical orbit P of a polynomial f by 

P := {f n(c) | c is a critical point of f, n ≥ 1}.

If the set P is finite, then f is called postcritically finite (PCF). The group Gf,a is contained in a self-similar subgroup called the arithmetic iterated monodromy group (IMG) associated to f . Roughly speaking, this group can be described as a quotient of the ´ etale fundamental group of P1

Cortez–Lukina analyzed settledness of the (profinite) iterated monodromy groups (IMG) of quadratic PCF polyno-mials with strictly preperiodic postcritical orbit P . They established dense settledness in one specific configuration of the postcritical orbit and provided substantial evidence for the general strictly preperiodic case, and raised the problem of understanding the periodic case (see [10, Problem 1.17]). In this article, we resolve this problem completely by proving that if f (x) ∈ K[x] is a quadratic polynomial with a periodic postcritical orbit, then its arithmetic IMG is densely settled. 

Theorem 1.1. Let K be a field of characteristic not equal to 2, and let f (x) ∈ K[x] be a PCF quadratic polynomial with a periodic postcritical orbit. Then the arithmetic iterated monodromy group of f (x) is densely settled.

While Cortez and Lukina [10] showed that the normalizer of an odometer is densely settled in a more general d-ary setting, our result provides a finer, element-by-element description of settledness in the binary case. Although related estimates appear implicitly in their work (for example, in the proof of Lemma 7.6), the statement above does not appear explicitly there. Moreover, our proof relies on the recursive structure of γ rather than on topological dynamics. Next, we show that the odometer γ occurs naturally in the chosen model for the geometric IMG. A central innovation of this paper is the use of descendants to define stable blocks, which serve as the main tool for constructing settled elements. See Definition 3.6 and Definition 6.2 for precise formulations. Stable blocks play an important role in the proof of Theorem 1.1. We prove the following key result. 

Theorem 1.4. Let K be a field of characteristic not equal to 2, and let f (x) ∈ K[x] be a PCF quadratic polynomial with a periodic postcritical orbit. Then any stable block in the arithmetic IMG of f (x) is settled.
