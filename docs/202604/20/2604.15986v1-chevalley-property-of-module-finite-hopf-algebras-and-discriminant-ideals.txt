CHEVALLEY PROPERTY OF MODULE-FINITE HOPF ALGEBRAS AND DISCRIMINANT IDEALS 

YIMIN HUANG, TIANCHENG QI, QUANSHUI WU, AND RUIPENG ZHU 

Abstract. In this paper, we study the Chevalley property of Cayley-Hamilton Hopf al-gebras in the sense of De Concini-Procesi-Reshetikhin-Rosso using discriminant ideals. For any affine Cayley-Hamilton Hopf algebra (H, C, tr ) whose identity fiber algebra has the Chevalley property, we prove that an irreducible H-module V has the property that V ⊗ W

is a completely reducible H-module for every irreducible H-module W if and only if V is annihilated by the lowest discriminant ideal of (H, C, tr ), which establishes a bridge be-tween the tensor-nondegenerate behaviour of the irreducible representations of H and the lowest discriminant ideal of (H, C, tr ). Using discriminant ideals, we prove that an affine Cayley-Hamilton Hopf algebra (H, C, tr ) has the Chevalley property if and only if its iden-tity fiber algebra H/ mεH has the Chevalley property and all the discriminant ideals of 

(H, C, tr ) are trivial, thereby resolving a question posed by Huang-Mi-Qi-Wu. Moreover, it is shown that the lowest discriminant subvariety Vℓ of the algebraic group maxSpec C is a closed subgroup, which reflects the rigid nature of Vℓ and is effective in determining the lowest discriminant subvarieties in certain examples of low GK dimension. Consequently, the lowest discriminant subvariety Vℓ is a smooth equidimensional algebraic variety, a property not generally shared by other discriminant subvarieties. This rigidity property provides a method, via the lowest discriminant ideals, for constructing a large family of Hopf algebras with the Chevalley property and finite GK dimension. The results are illus-trated through applications to the big quantized Borel subalgebras at roots of unity and to certain Artin-Schelter Gorenstein Hopf algebras of low GK dimension. In particular, the framework yields (non-finite) tensor categories with the Chevalley property arising from some big quantum groups at roots of unity. 

Introduction 

Over the past three decades, Hopf algebras of finite Gelfand-Kirillov (abbreviated GK) dimension have attracted considerable attention. See for example [And25, AAY23, AAH21, BC21, BG97, Bro98, BZ10, GZ10, DCPRR05, MWY25, Liu20, WLD16] and the refer-ences therein. The Chevalley property , introduced in [AEG01], is an active research topic in represen-tation theory and tensor categories, see [AEG01, EG01, Mom13, EGNO15, AGM17, EG21, Gel21, YLL24] for a partial list. A (rigid) tensor category is said to have the Cheval-ley property if the tensor product of any two irreducible/simple objects is completely re-ducible/semisimple. A Hopf algebra H (with bijective antipode) is said to have the Cheval-ley property if the tensor category of finite-dimensional H-modules has the Chevalley prop-erty (the dual Chevalley property can be defined similarly by considering the category of finite-dimensional comodules). This terminology is named after a classical result of Cheval-ley, which states that for any group, the tensor product of any two finite-dimensional ir-reducible representations over C is completely reducible. In fact, any group algebra and the universal enveloping algebra of any Lie algebra over an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero have the Chevalley property [EGNO15, Theorem 4.12.1]. All finite-dimensional semisimple or basic Hopf algebras have the Chevalley property. For example, the small quantum Borel subgroups u≥0

Molnar proved that the group algebra of a finite group G over a field of char-acteristic p has the Chevalley property if and only if G has a unique Sylow p-subgroup [Mol81, Corollary 1]. For any restricted Lie algebra (g, [p]) , the restricted enveloping alge-bra u(g) has the Chevalley property if and only if the Jacobson radical of u(g) is generated by the p-nilpotent radical of g [Mol81, Proposition 2]. Etingof-Gelaki [EG03] showed that all finite-dimensional triangular Hopf algebras over an algebraically closed field of char-acteristic zero have the Chevalley property. Moreover, Etingof-Gelaki proved that any finite symmetric tensor category over an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero is equivalent to the category of finite-dimensional representations of some finite-dimensional triangular Hopf algebra [EG03, Theorem 5.2]; consequently, all such tensor categories have the Chevalley property. All finite-dimensional triangular Hopf algebras with the Cheval-ley property over an algebraically closed field of characteristic 6 = 2 have been completely classified [AEG01, EG01, EG21, Gel21]. There are finite-dimensional quasitriangular Hopf algebras without the Chevalley property (e.g., the Lusztig’s small quantum groups uϵ(sl 2)

at roots of unity ϵ). Although extensive work has been done on finite-dimensional Hopf algebras with the Chevalley property, little is known in the infinite-dimensional case. Inspired by this insight, we consider irreducible representations whose tensor prod-ucts with all irreducible representations satisfy certain nondegeneracy conditions. An H-module V is called left tensor-reducible (resp. right tensor-reducible ), if V ⊗ W (resp. 

W ⊗ V ) is completely reducible for any irreducible H-module W . An H-module is said to be tensor-reducible , if it is both left tensor-reducible and right tensor-reducible. Clearly, all 

1-dimensional H-modules are tensor-reducible, and any left or right tensor-reducible module is itself completely reducible (see §2.2). We regard the tensor-reducible representations of H

as the “most nondegenerate” representations of H with respect to the tensor product. More-over, it is clear that H has the Chevalley property if and only if all irreducible H-modules are tensor-reducible. Thus, the study of tensor-reducible irreducible H-modules is essential to understanding the tensor-nondegenerate behaviour of irreducible representations of H,and the tensor-reducible property is closely tied to whether H has the Chevalley property. Another perspective to study the irreducible representations of H is to investigate the dis-criminant ideals [Rei03, CPWZ16] of (H, C, tr ), as exploited in [BY18, MWY25, HMQW25] recently. The third result in this paper, Theorem 0.4, addresses the rigidity of the lowest discrim-inant subvarieties of Cayley-Hamilton Hopf algebras. More precisely, we prove that, under the hypotheses of Theorem 0.1, the lowest discriminant subvariety Vℓ of (H, C, tr ) is a sub-group of the algebraic group maxSpec C. In particular, Vℓ is a smooth equidimensional subvariety of maxSpec C. This feature is not shared by other discriminant subvarieties, see Example 3.10. Furthermore, we show that the lowest discriminant ideal of (H, C, tr ) induces a (not necessary finite-dimensional) Hopf quotient of H satisfying the Chevalley property. Thus, Theorem 0.4, on one hand, reflects the rigid nature of Vℓ, and in some examples of low GK dimension, allows the closed subvariety Vℓ to be determined efficiently; on the other hand, it provides a method for using the lowest discriminant ideals to construct a large family of Hopf algebras, often infinite-dimensional, with the Chevalley property. 2.2. Tensor-reducible representations. Let H be a Hopf algebra over k. An H-module 

V is said to be left tensor-reducible , if V ⊗ W is completely reducible for all irreducible 

H-modules W . Similarly, one can define the notion of a right tensor-reducible module . An 

H-module is said to be tensor-reducible , if it is both left tensor-reducible and right tensor-reducible. Since the trivial module H k satisfies that W ∼= W ⊗k ∼= k⊗W for all H-modules 

W , it is clear that any left or right tensor-reducible module is completely reducible. It is obvious that if both V and W are tensor-reducible, V ⊗ W are also tensor-reducible. Note that an H-module is tensor-reducible if and only if it is a direct sum of some tensor-reducible irreducible H-modules. Hence, studying tensor-reducible representations of H reduces to studying the tensor-reducible irreducible representations of H.Let (H, C ) be a pair of Hopf algebras satisfying the hypothesis (FinHopf). Then all irreducible H-modules are finite-dimensional over k. By [MWY25, Theorem 3.1(b)], all 

1-dimensional H-modules are tensor-reducible. It is clear that the following are equivalent. (a) H has the Chevalley property; (b) All irreducible H-modules of H are left tensor-reducible; (c) All irreducible H-modules of H are right tensor-reducible; (d) All irreducible H-modules of H are tensor-reducible. Thus, the study of tensor-reducible irreducible representations of H is closely related to the investigation of the Chevalley property of H, and the greater the density of tensor-reducible members among the irreducible representations of H, the closer H is to possessing the Chevalley property.

Remark 3.12. Example 3.11 shows that, in the setting of Theorem 3.7, the lowest discrim-inant subvariety of (H, C, tr ) is not necessarily a normal subgroup of maxSpec C.The corollary below shows that Theorem 3.7 imposes a strong restriction on the possible shapes of the lowest discriminant subvariety for prime examples of GK-dimension one. 

Corollary 3.13. Let (H, C, tr ) be an affine Cayley-Hamilton Hopf algebra such that the identity fiber algebra H/ mεH has the Chevalley property. Assume that H is prime and is of GK-dimension one. Then either H is commutative or the lowest discriminant subvariety of (H, C, tr ) is a finite cyclic subgroup of the algebraic group maxSpec C.

Proof. If H has the Chevalley property, then H is commutative by [HMQW25, Corollary 4.10]. If H does not have the Chevalley property, then the lowest discriminant subvariety of 

(H, C, tr ) is a proper closed subvariety of maxSpec C by (2.14) and Theorem 3.1. By [MR01, Proposition 8.2.9 (ii)], maxSpec C is a connected affine algebraic group of dimension one. Hence [Hum75, Theorem 20.5] implies that as an algebraic group, maxSpec C is isomorphic to either the additive group k+ or the multiplicative group k×. Thus it suffices to show that every proper subgroup of k+ and k× is finite cyclic. Note that the only finite subgroup of the additive group k+ is the trivial one, and any finite subgroup of the multiplicative group of an infinite field is cyclic. So the result holds. □
