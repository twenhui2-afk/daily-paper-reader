# LLM-Driven Large-Scale Spectrum Access 

Ning Yang ∗, Member, IEEE , Jinliang Gao, Haijun Zhang, Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —Efficient spectrum management in massive-scale wireless networks is increasingly challenged by explosive ac-tion spaces and the computational intractability of traditional optimization. This study proposes a Large-Scale LLM-Driven Spectrum Access (LSA) framework rooted in Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). To overcome the computational collapse caused by ultra-long prompts in large-scale scenarios, we develop a hierarchical state serialization mechanism that synthesizes global environment statistics with localized critical constraints, enabling the LLM to perform high-dimensional reasoning within a bounded context window. Simulation results under strictly time-bounded inference protocols reveal that the code-driven paradigm eliminates the SFT cold-start bottleneck and leverages direct execution feedback to achieve superior scaling laws. The framework maintains robust spectral utility and generalization across varying network scales, yielding consistent and empirically superior performance over non-deterministic heuristics, and surpassing partitioned classical solvers in ultra-dense regimes under matched compute budgets. 

Index Terms —Large Language Model (LLM), Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), Dynamic Spectrum Access (DSA), Scalability. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# THE rapid evolution of 6G wireless networks has cat-alyzed an unprecedented demand for high-throughput transmission and massive connectivity. To support the bur-geoning requirements of the Internet of Things (IoT) and autonomous systems, Intelligent Spectrum Access (ISA) has emerged as a fundamental paradigm shift from static frequency allocation to autonomous, dynamic management. By allowing heterogeneous nodes to opportunistically access underutilized bands, ISA significantly enhances spectral efficiency in dense environments [1], [2]. However, despite its theoretical potential, spectrum man-agement in large-scale scenarios faces severe technical bot-tlenecks that hinder practical deployment. Modern wireless environments are increasingly defined by high dimensionality, extreme dynamism, and complex coupling between network parameters. Consequently, as node density increases, the joint optimization of channel selection and interference alignment becomes computationally intensive, frequently reaching NP-hard complexity in decentralized settings. Furthermore, in massive deployments, the signaling overhead required for centralized multi-agent coordination often scales exponen-tially, eventually exceeding the available bandwidth. This will

Existing solutions, primarily based on Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), exhibit notable limitations when applied to these high-dimensional and non-stationary settings [3], [4]. While DRL improves the handling of larger state spaces compared to traditional mathematical optimization, standard agents often suffer from low sample efficiency and slow convergence when facing explosive action spaces. A primary cause of this instability is the moving target problem inherent in multi-agent environments; as all agents learn and update their policies simultaneously, the environment becomes non-stationary from the perspective of any individual agent, leading to severe policy oscillation and divergent training curves. To address these multi-faceted challenges, we propose a Large Language Model (LLM)-driven Spectrum Access (LSA) framework. Unlike traditional actor-critic reinforcement learning structures, GRPO facilitates stable pol-icy updates across massive agent groups by eliminating the need for a separate value function critic. This significantly reduces the memory overhead and computational complexity during training, circumventing the instability typically associ-ated with approximating value functions in high-dimensional state spaces. The development of ISA has transitioned from model-based optimization to data-driven autonomous learning. This section categorizes existing literature into three primary research di-rections. 

A. Conventional Mathematical and Game-Theoretic Frame-works 

Early research on Dynamic Spectrum Access (DSA) fo-cused on rigorous mathematical modeling and optimization. To characterize the stochastic nature of primary user behaviors, renewal-theoretical approaches [6] and queuing analysis [7] were introduced to model temporal channel availability. In multi-user environments, game theory has been widely used to model competitive or cooperative interactions. Contract-theoretic models [1], [8] and auction mechanisms [9] were de-veloped to incentivize spectrum sharing. Furthermore, match-ing theory [10], [11] and graph coloring formulations [12] have been applied to coordinate allocation and mitigate interference. These conventional methods are heavily model-dependent, requiring precise prior knowledge of channel statistics that are often unavailable in real-world 6G deployments. Additionally, the computational complexity of solving these optimization problems, such as Lyapunov optimization [13] or aggregative games [14], scales exponentially with the node count, render-ing them intractable for large-scale IoT networks. 

B. Reinforcement Learning-Based Autonomous Access 

To address model dependency, reinforcement learning (RL) enables agents to learn policies through environmental inter-action. Initial frameworks utilized Markov Decision Processes (MDP) to establish sensing sequences [15], [16], while sub-sequent efforts employed multi-armed bandit (MAB) formu-lations [11], [20] and stochastic learning automata [17], [18]. The integration of deep learning led to DRL for handling high-dimensional state spaces [3], [21]. Architectures such as Deep Q-Networks (DQN) [22], [23] and Actor-Critic frameworks [24], [25] have been proposed for channel selection and power control. Current DRL-based methods face challenges in massive-scale environments. Most DRL agents rely on neural networks that lack semantic understanding, resulting in slow conver-gence when action spaces explode. Furthermore, although RL is designed for non-stationary environments, traditional policy gradients often suffer from high variance [26] and policy in-stability during multi-agent coordination. The LSA framework addresses these constraints by replacing rigid networks with an LLM engine, providing the semantic reasoning necessary for efficient decision-making. 

C. Scalability and Emerging Paradigms 

Recent literature has explored advanced paradigms to im-prove ISA scalability. Federated Learning (FL) has been integrated with MARL [5], [26] to facilitate distributed policy updates. To meet complex service requirements, DRL has been extended to NOMA-aided networks [27] and mobile edge computing (MEC) scenarios [28]. Additionally, Gaussian process-based RL [29] and heuristically accelerated Q-learning [30] have been investigated to improve convergence. While these methods enhance scalability, they often suffer from a generalization gap when transitioning to unseen con-figurations. The emergence of LLMs provides a potential so-lution for zero-shot adaptation [2], [31]–[33]. LLMs solve the scalability bottleneck by mapping complex numerical states into low-dimensional semantic spaces, where the model’s pre-trained sequence logic identifies latent coordination patterns without explicit signaling. To resolve the computational over-head of applying LLMs to high-frequency decisions, this work utilizes a GRPO mechanism to stabilize training in massive-scale dynamic networks. III. FRAMEWORK 

To address the combinatorial intractability and the slow convergence of conventional DRL in large-scale deployments, this section introduces the LSA framework. By reformulating the spectrum allocation problem as a structured reasoning and sequence modeling task, the LSA framework employs the in-context generalization of LLMs to navigate massive-scale heterogeneous networks without the explicit construction of high-dimensional transition matrices. 

A. Architectural Design and Semantic Mapping 

The primary challenge in massive-scale spectrum access is the factorial expansion of the action space, which renders value-based RL methods prone to policy instability. The LSA framework shifts the paradigm from numerical value estimation to semantic reasoning. The core architecture is built upon three technical pillars: First, a Semantic Feature Mapping mechanism translates raw physical parameters into a structured linguistic representation, enabling the LLM to identify latent coordination patterns. Second, a Group-based Policy Alignment strategy utilizes GRPO to stabilize policy updates in discrete decision spaces. Third, a Deterministic Repair Pipeline ensures that the reasoning outputs strictly adhere to the physical constraints defined in (5) and (6). 

B. Hierarchical State Serialization and Prompt Engineering 

To bridge the gap between numerical wireless environment states and the linguistic reasoning domain of LLMs, we implement a Hierarchical State Serialization mechanism. As illustrated in Figure 1, the normalized environment state st

is not merely flattened into a sequence; instead, it is trans-formed into a structured, context-aware prompt Pt designed to minimize token consumption while maximizing logical consistency. The serialization process translates raw physical parameters into three functional semantic blocks:

Network Statistical Context : This block synthesizes macroscopic features, including the current noise floor 

N0(t) and the real-time density of active users Kt.To facilitate rapid convergence, we provide empirical 5

distributions (maximum, minimum, mean, and standard deviation) of the received power {Pu,c } and channel bandwidths {Bc}. This statistical abstraction allows the LLM to perform high-level resource prioritization without processing every individual node’s raw data.  

> •

Operational Constraints and Guidance : To enforce the physical laws defined in Section III, the prompt explicitly embeds the Shannon-Hartley theorem and the linear-scale conversion logic (dBm to Watts). By defining the mapping constraints {(5) , (6) } as logical directives, the LLM is guided to treat spectrum access as a collision-free assignment task.  

> •

Output Schema Specification : The model is constrained to generate a structured Python dictionary action =

By utilizing standard sci-entific libraries (e.g., NumPy, SciPy), the framework ensures that the generated reasoning is mathematically grounded and executable within the simulation environ-ment. For the sake of reproducibility and technical clarity, the semantic backbone of the serialized prompt Pt is summarized as follows. The prompt begins by defining the objective, which is to maximize aggregate system throughput and spec-trum utilization. It then provides the environment statistics, detailing the status of idle channels ( Cempty ) and the signal characteristics of requesting users. Finally, a critical guidance section is included, emphasizing the avoidance of co-channel interference and the necessity of one-to-one mapping. Unlike conventional natural language prompts, this template prohibits descriptive explanations and focuses strictly on algorithmic code generation, maintaining a compact 2048-token context window even in high-density 6G scenarios. This hierarchical approach allows the LLM to perform high-dimensional reasoning within a specific context window, sig-nificantly reducing the computational overhead per scheduling slot.

D. Execution-Aware Composite Reward Formulation

This magnitude alignment is critical: without normalization, the discrete nature of code execution feedback would cause structural penalties to dominate the gradient landscape. Crucially, during the training phase, the deterministic repair pipeline is intentionally disabled to enforce strict credit assignment. The reward R(o)

is computed directly on the raw LLM output o; malformed or constraint-violating generations incur severe structural penal-ties ( ˆRstruct → 0), forcing the policy to internalize valid syntac-tic and logical priors. The repair operator R(·) is exclusively activated during the inference/deployment phase to guarantee physical feasibility. 

1) Hierarchical Structural Compliance ( ˆRstruct ): To bridge the gap between stochastic token generation and deterministic execution, we formulate structural compliance as a progressive validation process rather than a binary filter. Capped at a maximum of 1.2, this hierarchical design serves as a soft curriculum that penalizes superficial hallucinations at early stages while guiding the policy to internalize hard physical constraints. By filtering structurally invalid samples before environment interaction, it prevents gradient pollution and compels the model to transition from mere syntactic formatting to semantically grounded, context-driven algorithmic generation.

B. Baseline Methodologies and Compute-Equivalent Protocol 

To ensure rigorous and unbiased evaluation, the LSA frame-work is benchmarked against four algorithmic categories:  

> •

Combinatorial Optimization: Exhaustive Enumeration establishes the theoretical upper bound for small-scale instances. The Kuhn-Munkres (KM) algorithm serves as the standard bipartite matching baseline. For ultra-dense regimes where KM incurs O(N 3) complexity, a 

Grouped-KM heuristic partitions the network into disjoint subgraphs to maintain tractability.  

> •

Meta-Heuristic Search: Differential Evolution (DE) with population size 50 and crossover rate 0.9 represents classical iterative optimization without learning priors.  

> •

Connectionist Reinforcement Learning: Standard DQN and PPO architectures with 128-dimensional hidden lay-ers are deployed. The output layers are dynamically reshaped to match the action space cardinality, explicitly testing their resilience to combinatorial explosion.  

> •

Stochastic Allocation: Uniform random channel assign-ment serves as the lower-bound performance reference. All learning-based agents are evaluated under a strictly compute-equivalent protocol. Training and inference are con-strained to identical GPU FLOP budgets and runtime windows to isolate algorithmic efficiency from hardware disparities. Performance metrics are reported as mean values over inde-pendent Monte Carlo realizations, with qualitative stability and convergence behavior analyzed to validate robustness. 

C. Evaluation Metrics 

System performance is quantified through three core di-mensions: (1) Aggregate Throughput , computed as the mean Shannon capacity per scheduling step across all active users; (2) Valid Action Rate , measuring the proportion of generated allocations that satisfy hard physical constraints prior to repair; and (3) Inference Latency , recorded as the wall-clock time per scheduling decision. Zero-shot generalization capability is assessed by evaluating trained models on unseen topologies and node densities without parameter updates or prompt fine-tuning. VI. Unlike DRL agents that learn tabula rasa and require complete architecture reshaping and full retraining for new action space dimensions, the LLM’s generalization capability fundamen-tally stems from its massive-scale pre-training priors. Dur-ing pre-training on diverse code repositories and algorithmic corpora, the transformer implicitly internalizes scale-invariant logical structures and constraint satisfaction patterns. When confronted with unseen network topologies, the hierarchical serialization mechanism projects the novel physical state into this pre-established semantic manifold via prompt injection. The model does not update weights to solve the new problem; instead, it leverages distilled algorithmic priors to rapidly iden-tify coordination patterns and generate valid mappings through 

in-context learning . This paradigm shift from gradient-based optimization to reasoning-based adaptation eliminates the generalization gap that plagues connectionist models, enabling robust, topology-agnostic deployment without parameter fine-tuning. VII. C ONCLUSION 

This paper presented a generative coordination paradigm for DSA to address the combinatorial intractability inherent in massive-scale 6G-enabled IoT networks. By reformulating spectrum allocation as a structured reasoning task, the pro-posed LSA framework effectively decouples decision-making efficiency from the exponential growth of the action space, a feat historically unattainable for value-based reinforcement learning and classical bipartite matching. Through the in-tegration of GRPO and a deterministic repair pipeline, the framework achieves a near-optimal balance between combi-natorial intuition and physical feasibility. Extensive evalua-tions across varying network densities demonstrate that LSA provides a complexity-resilient solution, maintaining superior spectral efficiency and structural stability even in ultra-dense configurations scaling to 2000 nodes and channels, where tra-ditional DRL architectures fail due to action-space explosion (N/A at ∼20 nodes) and partitioned classical solvers exhibit synchronization bottlenecks under matched compute budgets. Ultimately, this work establishes that LLM-driven structured reasoning offers a scalable and architecturally flexible alterna-tive to numerical optimization, providing a robust foundation for autonomous and interpretable resource management in future hyper-connected wireless environments. 11
